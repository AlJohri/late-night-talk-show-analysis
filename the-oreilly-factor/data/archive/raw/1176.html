Talking Points Memo & Top Story 
Fairness & justice at Duke University
Guest: Stephen Miller, Duke Chronicle

"Shortly after three Duke lacrosse players were charged with rape last spring, 88 Duke professors signed an ad decrying the 'anger and fear of many students who know themselves to be the objects of racism and sexism.' Talk about a rush to judgment! There's now strong evidence that the accuser fabricated at least part of the story, and charges of racism and sexism could be completely bogus. So we called most of the professors who signed that ad, wanting to talk to them about it. None of the 88 teachers who signed the original ad will apologize or even explain themselves. Not one! The university has invited the accused players back on campus, but these professors continue their irresponsible behavior. If I were those students, I wouldn't go back. Hopefully the North Carolina Attorney General will drop the case and prosecutor Mike Nifong will resign. That would be justice, and Duke needs all the justice it can get."

For more on the situation in Durham, The Factor was joined by Duke student and journalist Stephen Miller. "You have a segment of professors," Miller reported, "who are very powerful and very far to the left. For the first time, a lot of students saw the depth and ferocity of that radicalism, and now those professors are running for cover. This was never about what happened to this woman or the players, this was always a political agenda - to make a big deal about racism, gender and class warfare." The Factor condemned the cowardly Duke professors and administrators who refuse to own up to their misjudgment. "What kind of message does this send students who may want to take their courses? All the professors have to do is say 'maybe I made a mistake.' But they won't even do that. Duke is now being portrayed as a radical left-wing university with no voices of moderation on the faculty."

Factor Follow Up Segment 
Shawn Hornbeck: Questions raised
Guest: Jamie Allman, 97.1 FM Talk

More details are emerging about Shawn Hornbeck, the 15-year-old who stayed with his abductor for four years. According to published reports, Hornbeck had access to the police on a number of occasions, he had a computer and even went to a school dance. Nevertheless, St. Louis radio host Jamie Allman reported that his listeners are giving Hornbeck every benefit of the doubt. "This particular crime hits at our deepest fears as parents, so the more information we have about what exactly happened, the better off we are as parents and as a community. But no matter what level of cooperation or coercion occurred, Shawn Hornbeck was without question a victim." The Factor agreed that the case deserves scrutiny, and Hornbeck deserves sympathy. "Every kid who is kidnapped is a victim, but what are the associations between the victim and the person who is victimizing them. I'm getting blasted for raising these questions."

Personal Story Segment 
Presidential race contenders line up
Guest: Author Terry McAuliffe

The Factor was joined by former Democratic National Committee boss Terry McAuliffe, who is now organizing Hillary Clinton's campaign. He downplayed the media infatuation with Senator Barack Obama. "The media want a race," McAuliffe asserted. They want to make it exciting and sell newspapers. But if you look at the polls, Hillary Clinton is way ahead. We're going to have a tough, aggressive campaign, and we are all unified against George Bush and this war in Iraq." The Factor countered that not all Democrats are united. "You don't address the tension between the moderate wing of the Democrat party and the far left wing. There's a real ideological division in the party."

Unresolved Problems Segment 
Debate surrounds Bush library
Guest: Rev. Fred Kandeler, Methodist Federation for Social Action

Southern Methodist University has emerged as the frontrunner to house the George W. Bush Presidential Library, which has led to protests by some Methodist ministers. Rev. Fred Kandeler outlined his objections. "There is a disconnect between the Bush presidency and the social teachings of the Methodist church. We're talking about war, the use of torture, the death penalty, the environment, and poverty. We're really looking at unjust systems that keep people in poverty. There is a widening gap between the wealthy and the poorest." The Factor challenged Kandeler on various issues, including the economy. "Poverty is very low in the United States, and even those who are poor have the basic staples of life unless they're addicted or mentally ill. So what's the problem with the president's economic policies, which have been very successful?"

Impact Segment 
Support for Iraq war dwindling
Guests: Fox News analysts Michelle Malkin & Kirsten Powers

Fox News analysts Michelle Malkin and Kirsten Powers joined The Factor with their views on two major stories. Malkin, who just returned from Iraq, provided details about her visit. "The troops told us this is not a short term commitment, they're in it for the long haul. When you listen to the mainstream media coverage and the daily death count, you don't get a full sense of the progress that is being made - Iraqi civilians who are cooperating with our troops, Iraqis who are joining the Iraqi security forces." On another subject, Powers predicted Hillary Clinton will be an inviting target for both parties. "I expect Hillary to be attacked constantly. The far left doesn't like her, and the Republicans will attack her because they see her as a major threat. It's going to be ugly, she brings out the worst in a lot of people." The Factor pointed out that Clinton's prospective opponent Barack Obama has the support of billionaire George Soros. "If Obama makes a serious run for this and if he has Soros money behind him, this is going to get pretty vicious. They're going to go after her."

Back of Book Segment 
Teenage sexuality flourishes on TV
Guest: Dr. Sari Locker, Columbia University

Magazines, the Internet, and TV shows are filled with images of sexualized young women. According to Columbia University's Sari Locker, teen girls are walking a delicate tightrope. "It's natural for adolescents to be striving for their identity, they're experiencing their sexuality for the first time. But the dangerous part is if your kids are following these disgusting role models like Paris Hilton and Lindsay Lohan and Britney Spears. This behavior is pornographic." The Factor urged parents to be attentive. "I don't care what anyone over 18 does, I'm a libertarian there. But girls from 11 to 16 are now under siege by their role models acting inappropriately and all the other things they see. The peer pressure is enormous."
