Talking Points Memo & Top Story 
More warnings about the terror war
Guests: Kamal Nawash, Free Muslim Coalition Against Terrorism & columnist Dr. Mamoun Fandy

"Angry Muslims rioted in Beirut, burning the Danish embassy over some political cartoons in the Danish press that mocked suicide bombers using images of Mohammed. Once again, the hair-trigger rage of some Muslims is on display. Paris burned a few months ago, and all over the world Islamic matches are out and ready to be lighted at almost any provocation. Europe is generally weak in the face of growing Islamic ferocity, and here in this country there is a substantial minority that wants nothing to do with fighting the terror war. Everything the Bush administration has done to fight Al Qaeda is opposed by the far left and its shock troops at the ACLU. On the right, many big US corporations refuse to sacrifice a dime to help in the terror war - we see this in the record oil company profits. The truth is the world is going to have to confront Islamofascism or millions will perish. Talking Points believes the war on terror and extremism in the Muslim world could be the most grave danger the USA and Europe has ever faced. We should all face that fact. God help us if we don't."

Fox News Video: FoxNews.com

Muslims Kamal Nawash and Mamoun Fandy joined The Factor with more on the cartoon controversy. Fandy rationalized the violence as an understandable reaction to religious slander. "Those cartoons are very offensive, and you should not defame the very core of Islam, which is the prophet Mohammed." But Nawash was mortified by the response of his fellow Muslims. "I understand that not everyone loves my prophet like I do, but I'm not going to go out in the streets and burn anything. Muslims who are offended by this cartoon should be more offended by people who commit acts of terror in the name of Islam." The Factor agreed that many Muslims seem to practice selective outrage. "Things are getting worse in the Islamic world - anything seems to set off violence. But you don't see demonstrations like this when little children are blown up."

Impact Segment 
Newt Gingrich on the news
Guest: Fox News analyst Newt Gingrich

Former Speaker of the House Newt Gingrich added his analysis of the Islamic violence. "In Saudi Arabia, no Jews or Christians have any rights of any kind. But the Saudis get mad at a cartoon in Denmark. Think about the one-sided arrogance of that definition of reality. This is a world that we are in a collision with, and the challenge of a free society is to stand up and say that this violence should be condemned." The Factor questioned whether Western democracies have the will to confront Islamic fascism. "Europe is not going to fight, and here in the United States there is a tremendous division about confronting the war on terror in any way. The West is weak and it seems to me the jihadists are getting stronger."

Factor Follow Up Segment 
Parent who attacked teacher to sue
Guest: Attorney Ron Kurpiers

42-year old Floridian Dave Swafford, who punched a teacher's assistant he suspected had inappropriately touched his 16-year old daughter, is now suing the school. Swafford's attorney Ron Kurpiers claimed that Swafford's daughter was molested and the school is engaged in a cover-up. "We believe the school district has gone into a spin campaign that rivals anything you see in Washington. I have a number of witnesses that will come forward and talk about the teacher's actions over the last year that are consistent with my client's story." The Factor warned Kurpiers that his client's allegations will be hard to prove. "These are some pretty strong charges you're putting out there. If this comes down to he-said-she-said, it is going to be very hard. The sheriff's office says there are no witnesses and there is no proof."

Unresolved Problems Segment 
Child abuse cases in Massachusetts
Guest: Child advocate Wendy Murphy

The Bay State has recently been the scene of some horrendous crimes against children. One case involves high school teacher Gregory Pathiakis, who pleaded guilty to raping a 15-year old male student. Prosecutors requested a jail term of at least four years, but Judge Suzanne Delvecchio gave Pathiakis a suspended sentence and probation. Child advocate and lawyer Wendy Murphy elaborated on the case. "The judge said the boy was nearly 16 and it was consensual, so let's not throw the guy in jail. What she missed is that he's a teacher. You can't send this message to teachers who have the trust of the parents." Murphy lamented that her state is notoriously lenient toward child molesters. "You get punishment for hurting an animal faster than you get punishment for hurting a child. We have a terrible reputation." The Factor accused state leaders of simply not caring about children. "This reminds people that Massachusetts has no Jessica's Law or no minimum sentences. And these judges just don't think these crimes are a big deal."

Personal Story Segment 
Maher on Maher
Guest: Bill Maher

Comedian and political observer Bill Maher joined The Factor to talk about various topics. He began by downplaying the importance of fighting illegal immigration. "I live in Beverly Hills - the town would completely collapse if there were no illegal immigrants." The Factor replied, "You can't have millions of people walking in here unsupervised, even if people in Beverly Hills want their lawns trimmed twice a week." Maher also dismissed the suggestion that President Bush has been effective in the war on terror. "Just because we haven't been attacked doesn't mean the Bush administration has disrupted anything. We haven't been attacked because Al Qaeda is taking its time. They're planning it and working on it." Finally, Maher joked that "Brokeback Mountain" is not the first gay cowboy film, claiming that distinction belongs to "Butch Cassidy and the Sundance Kid." "One of the actors went on to start a film festival, and the other has his own salad dressing. So you make your own judgment."

Back of Book Segment 
Toles ducks The Factor
Guest: Fox News analyst Ellis Henican

Political cartoonist Tom Toles, whose depiction of a soldier with missing arms and legs outraged many in the military, refused an invitation to appear on The Factor. Instead, he defended his cartoon in the friendlier setting of CNN. Fox News analyst Ellis Henican stood up for Toles' decision to choose his venue. "A cartoonist has the right to let his art do the speaking for him. You want him to come into your arena, but the notion that he has an obligation to explain his work to anyone is wrong." The Factor denounced Toles' for refusing to debate. "Toles is a weasel, and I'll tell you why. When you put something out like that you know it is going to cause a reaction. You have to have enough courage to defend it, and he doesn't. And I don't have any respect for him at all."

Factor Mail 
Viewers sound off
Many of you sent e-mails about the new hospital being built for wounded veterans. Some excerpts:

Bonnie Stevens, Overbrook, KS: "Bill, thanks for your help with the new hospital. My son-in-law was severely hurt in Iraq and needs ongoing care."

Jim Rowlett, Traverse City, MI: "Bill, recognizing companies for contributing to the new veterans hospital is great - threatening those that don't is extortion."

Dick Dinkins, Denver, CO: "O'Reilly, I was appalled at your implied threat to blackmail corporations that won't help."

Other viewers commented on the Tom Toles cartoon that depicted Defense Secretary Donald Rumsfeld visiting a severely wounded soldier.

Guy Smalley, Waynesville, NC: "Bill, as a professional cartoonist, I was amazed at the lack of creativity in the anti-Rumsfeld cartoon by Tom Toles. It was designed to shock."

Dan Gavigan, Seaford, NY: "Sometimes political messages have to be insensitive. I believe Toles has the best interests of the troops in mind."

Greg McCauley, Westfield, IN: "Our son is a Marine and was critically wounded in Iraq. Secretary Rumsfeld paid him a personal visit and we found him to be a caring and compassionate man."
