Talking Points Memo & Top Story 
President Obama and your money
"The huge AIG insurance company sold policies to bad lenders, and when the junk mortgage fraud industry crashed, AIG had to pay some of those losses. The feds bailed out these screwups and gave them $173 billion in taxpayer dollars. Now we learn that the incompetents running AIG are paying $1.2 billion of the bailout money to their own executives in bonuses, saying they are 'contractually obligated' to do so. President Obama says he will try to block the bonus payments, so he seems to be standing up for the folks on this one. That's good, because he is definitely not standing up for the folks in Detroit. The Detroit school system is a shambles, with only 5% of graduating seniors ready to do college academic work. President Obama's s solution is to give the school system $535 million in taxpayer money. But here's the real problem - audits have been shown that about $54 million tax dollars are missing or have been misspent by Detroit school officials. Talking Points says no to AIG and no to the Detroit school chaos. The Detroit public schools are violent and chaotic, and many of the kids there are unsupervised at home. Money is not going to solve that problem. If President Obama continues to simply throw money at complicated problems, he will bankrupt the USA, and we will all suffer greatly."

The Factor asked FNC analysts Juan Williams and Mary Katharine Ham to weigh in on the AIG bonuses. "If the people at AIG were hunters," Williams quipped, "they would be starving. They would be out of business if not for taxpayer money, and they do not deserve any bonus." Ham suggested that President Obama is earning political points with his opposition to the bonuses. "There's nothing that brings people in Washington together faster than the need to get on the right side of a populist backlash. The great irony of Barack Obama is that in the AIG situation he's on the right side, saying we shouldn't reward bad behavior. But when it comes to these school systems, he wants to do the liberal thing and throw money and reward the bad behavior of school officials." The Factor reiterated that "you do not allow AIG people to get bonuses, and you do not put $535 million into a school system where millions of dollars are missing."

Culture Warriors Segment 
Kids and violence: A major misunderstanding?
According to some surveys, many teens believe pop star Rihanna was responsible for her beating by Chris Brown. The Factor asked FNC contributors Margaret Hoover and Tammy Bruce about the impact of "hip-hop" music on abuse of women. "The lyrics condone violence against women," Hoover lamented, "and that's why a lot of black leader have come out and said these lyrics can not stand. Hip-hop culture and icons are reinforcing this." Bruce also blamed self-proclaimed feminists. "This is also an indication of the failure of the liberal feminist establishment. You have a generation that thinks Rihanna just mistakenly put her head in front of his fist, and American women have to educate the next generation." The Factor indicted rap music and some of its major stars: "This hip-hop rap stuff has made it so that some kids think it's acceptable to cuff women around."

Personal Story Segment 
Jackie Mason's disparaging Obama comment
Comedian Jackie Mason is taking some heat because he referred to Barack Obama with the Yiddish term "schwartza." The Factor welcomed Fox News contributor Angela McGlowan, who accused many of her fellow blacks of being grossly oversensitive. "We've had Imus," McGlowan began, "then we had the New York Post and a chimp cartoon. 'Schwarza' is a German term for 'black,' and I don't consider what he said as being degrading to black people. When are we going to get away from this double standard, where certain people can say certain things? Chris Rock can use 'cracker' when referring to white people and the left doesn't go after him. We need to stop being so sensitive."

Impact Segment 
Defending the United States Armed Forces
After he intentionally fired his weapon in front of captured civilians in Afghanistan, Army Captain Roger Hill was dismissed from his command and given a general discharge. Captain Hill, who is trying to change his discharge to "honorable," joined The Factor and made his case. "We had irrefutable evidence that some civilians were giving information to Al Qaeda," Hill asserted, "and I shot my gun into the ground, some twenty meters away from the nearest detainee. I was taken out of command just days after this incident occurred." Hill's lawyer Neal Puckett asserted that his client received unjust treatment. "We're trying to convince the Secretary of the Army to upgrade his discharge, and we hope enough people will write in to help convince the Army that this man deserves an honorable discharge." Without judging the issue at hand, The Factor thanked Captain Hill for his service.
Miller Time Segment 
Dennis Miller... in New York!
The Factor welcomed Dennis Miller, who led off with the Obama administration's decision to stop using the phrase "enemy combatant." "I can not believe," Miller said, "that we've decided to battle Al Qaeda by vernacular-ing them to death. They call us Satan, and I can't even say they're an 'enemy combatant.' Now I have to call them 'neighbor with a beef' or something like that. Instead of confronting them with Petraeus and Odierno, we've decided to go at them with Strunk and White." Miller also scrutinized the on-air showdown between Jon Stewart and CNBC's Jim Cramer. "Jon doesn't like disingenuous people and I knew Cramer was going to be eviscerated, as he should. Cramer went on the show because he hates the idea that someone as quintessentially cool as Jon Stewart thinks he's a putz."

Back of Book Segment 
Reality Check: Protesting government spending
There have been rallies around the country to protest government spending and bailouts. The Factor offered this Reality Check: "The latest was in Cincinnati, where about 5,000 folks showed up to show their displeasure with the Obama administration's money strategy. These gatherings are being dubbed 'tea parties,' and we expect to see a lot more of them." Meanwhile, across the pond, a TV critic in the London Sunday Times referred to Bill as "a skin-crawlingly unpleasant right-wing polymath." The Factor's check: "The Sunday Times is owned by the News Corporation, which owns Fox News. Nice to know there is so much love in the company for me."
