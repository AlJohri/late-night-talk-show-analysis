Talking Points Memo 
Obama tries to rescuscitate the stimulus bill
"I think the government has to stimulate the economy, but why the president allowed Nancy Pelosi and her far-left crew to hijack the situation is way beyond me. The majority of Americans are now opposed to spending a trillion dollars on social engineering and other liberal causes; the folks want the money to help put people back to work and stimulate buying power. And according to a new Gallup Poll, a whopping 85% of Americans are concerned that the news media will not provide oversight on President Obama. The Obama administration needs to help the folks right now, it needs to pass a bill that puts as many Americans to work as possible and provides incentives for people to spend money. We don't need research on global warming in this bill, we don't need birth control for the kids, we don't need income redistribution. All of those things can wait for another time and another debate. Talking Points urges President Obama to wise up, to help the folks, and to get an effective stimulus bill passed."

Top Story 
Irony surrounds Daschle's dashed cabinet hopes
After revelations that he "forgot" to pay certain taxes, former Senator Tom Daschle withdrew his nomination to lead the Department of Health and Human Services. The Factor was joined by Linda Chavez, whose own nomination as President Bush's Labor Secretary was scuttled by none other than Tom Daschle. "Daschle actually threatened to filibuster my nomination," Chavez recalled, "when it was revealed that I had taken in a woman who had been battered by her husband a full decade earlier. I gave her some financial assistance and gave her a place to live, and Daschle seemed to think that was way out of bounds and I was not a fit Labor Secretary." The Factor pointed out the irony in then-Senator Daschle's opposition to Chavez: "He said that if the proposed Labor Secretary can't obey the law, we've got problems here. He used this as a sledgehammer on you and the hypocrisy is staggering."

Week in Review Segment 
Laura Ingraham: State oversight in octuplet case
California authorities say they will supervise the home of 33-year-old Nadia Suleman, who gave birth to octuplets and now has fourteen children. Fox News contributor Laura Ingraham called the situation a predictable result of modern science. "When we deny nature and say people have to control the reproductive process," Ingraham said, "this kind of stuff is going to happen. There will be multiple births and there will be people who can't take care of the kids. But would it have been better if the kids were selectively reduced? There's something wrong with this scenario, where the 'moral' choice in some quarters was to have aborted these kids." The Factor urged California to provide continual oversight. "These children are at risk physically, and there are 17 people living in a three-bedroom house. All I am asking is that we protect these children. You don't create 14 babies when you're broke, the mother is a nut."

Impact Segment 
Mexico, the border fence and the war on drugs
With many cities in Mexico being terrorized by ultra-violent drug lords, The Factor was joined two men who warned that American border towns are also in danger. "This is a big issue that Americans don't know enough about," said James Carafano of the conservative Heritage Foundation. "We send money and guns south, and drugs and people come north. This is a multi-billion dollar industry and you have drug cartels that are robbing and kidnapping anyone who gets in their way." Sigi Gonzalez, a sheriff in Texas' Zapata County, complained that drug violence is escalating. "My deputies have seen people coming across, 25 at a time, with machine guns. We've been telling the federal government about this, and the problem has been here for a long time." The Factor reported that partially-completed border fence has helped curtail some of the drug violence. "There are entire cities in Mexico that are almost shut down by the violence, it's a war down there. The New York Times and other far left entities objected to the border fence, but now any sane person has to be glad that we have it."

Personal Story Segment 
Is a Cheerios commercial insulting men?
In a TV spot for Cheerios, a man and wife bicker over the health and diet benefits of the cereal. "Male advocate" Marc Rudov cited the spot as one more example of marketers speaking down to men. "There is an institutionalization of the hatred of men," Rudov claimed. "In this commercial the man is stupid and weak, the woman is smart and powerful. That's a common thread, and what would happen if you reversed the genders? Little kids are always looking at men being portrayed as doofuses on TV." The Factor jokingly advised Rudov to lighten up. "I don't take this as seriously as you do, Marc, because I'm confident in my masculinity."

The Kelly File Segment 
Another cabinet embarrassment for Obama?
The Factor invited Fox News anchor Megyn Kelly to scrutinize David Ogden, nominated to serve as Deputy Attorney General in the Obama administration. "A lot of his positions have been extreme," Kelly reported. "He represented porn kings like Playboy and Penthouse, but that doesn't make him some porn king. He also argued that abortion rarely causes emotional problems to the mother. He's been promoting a very liberal agenda when it comes to child pornography and abortion, but what he did in private practice doesn't necessarily dictate what he's going to do at Justice." The Factor criticized Ogden for failing to protect children because "he fought against a proposed law that would ban pornography in libraries."

Back of Book Segment 
Reality Check: Ambushing Russell Tice
During a recent appearance on MSNBC, former National Security Agency analyst Russell Tice claimed his former agency routinely eavesdropped on Americans, particularly journalists. The Factor's check: "That would be illegal and, if true, a very frightening situation. NBC News didn't push Tice for any specific proof, no surprise there. So we respectfully called Tice, inviting him on the program. He said no, so we sent producer Jesse Watters to visit Tice." Watters challenged Tice to "back up his accusations," but Tice refused any comment. The Factor continued: "The bottom line is that Tice made some very serious accusations and he can't back them up. If he can't back them up, he is disgraceful and so is NBC News."
Pinheads and Patriots 
USPS & Etta James
Thursday's Patriot: The U.S. Post Office in Coconut Creek, Florida, which is now named after Army Specialist Daniel Agami, who was killed in Iraq. And the Pinhead: Blues singer Etta James, who denounced Beyonce for singing her signature song "At Last," and added that Barack Obama "ain't my president."
