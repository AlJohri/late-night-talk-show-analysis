Talking Points Memo & Top Story 
The arrest of O.J. Simpson
"While most Americans are good people, we do have our share of villains in this country. A good example is O.J. Simpson, a man who literally got away with murder. So when Simpson was arrested over the weekend for armed robbery, many people feel karma had kicked in, and maybe Simpson would finally get some kind of formal punishment. Obviously, Simpson's behavior since the murder trial has been abysmal. He's cavorted with questionable people, participated in that vile book, and has demonstrated that he's totally devoid of any conscience or judgment. O.J. Simpson is quite simply a sociopath - that is, he doesn't have any feeling about anybody other than himself. In addition, Simpson remains a dangerous person - he has a vicious temper, low impulse control and a sense of entitlement. But this time Simpson may well go to jail. Let's hope karma and the Las Vegas prosecutor join together to put Simpson in prison for as long as possible."

The Factor welcomed two people who have been following the Simpson saga. Investigative reporter Pat LaLama began by casting some doubt on the prosecution's case. "This whole thing is starting to smell like rotting fish," LaLama opined. "This guy Thomas Riccio used a voice recorder to record the 6-minute scuffle that went on, with guns waving in the air. What was his man's motive, who offered him what and how much money is involved? The tape is pretty incriminating, but they may not get Simpson on all those felonies." But legal analyst Royal Oakes argued that the audio tape is sufficiently damning. "The words Simpson uttered on that tape are going to land him in prison, and the violent context of that tape is going to sink O.J.'s ship. A sentence of five years and up will not be a shock if he is convicted." The Factor reminded everyone that juries are capricious. "The jury will have to decide whether a crime was committed, or whether Simpson was led into a crime by con men. There are enough disturbing things surrounding this situation where a good attorney could stand this on its head."

The Factor next welcomed former LAPD detective Phillip Vannatter, who recalled the savagery of the 1994 double murder. "He almost decapitated Nicole from anger," Vannatter said, "and this new tape sounds like the 9-1-1 tape from Nicole when O.J. was threatening to break into her house. He's a totally angry person and could kill again." Dave Cook, attorney for the Goldman family, described their reaction to Simpson's latest arrest. "They are smiling because Mr. Simpson will probably spend some time, maybe a lot of time, behind bars. It's tragic that it didn't happen for the murders of Ron Goldman and Nicole Brown Simpson, but justice is where you find it."
Impact Segment 
Gen. Wesley Clark endorses Hillary Clinton
Former presidential candidate General Wesley Clark, who is supporting Hillary Clinton this time around, pointedly denounced the MoveOn ad that slandered General David Petraeus. "That was way beyond the bounds," Clark declared. "If they had come to me, I would have stopped that instantly. It's a tremendous distraction from what we should be talking about." Clark also stressed the need for high-level talks in the Middle East. "We're going to be in that region for a long time. We need a diplomatic offensive, to go through and sort out all the tangles of interests and influence." The Factor suggested that Clark may be the ideal running mate for Senator Clinton. "Hillary Clinton putting you on the ticket gets a military guy in there. Because people suspect her terror warrior credibility, and you have credibility, you may be the VP."
Factor Follow Up Segment 
Hardee's releases (and pulls) offensive TV ad
As The Factor reported last week, the Hardee's burger chain produced a TV spot in which a young teacher dances suggestively in front of her testosterone-laden students. Hardee's CEO Andy Puzder entered the No Spin Zone and justified the ad. "I've got six kids," Puzder said, "and didn't think it was about teachers abusing students, I thought it was funny. There is no physical contact between the students and the teacher. But when teachers reacted negatively to it, we changed the ad and took the teacher out." Factor applauded Puzder for stepping up and answering questions, but criticized his judgment. "We know your target audience is young men, but this elevates into a really offensive zone where adults in authority are actually harming children. You made a big mistake here."
Personal Story Segment 
Censored at the Emmys!
After winning an Emmy Sunday night, Sally Field launched into a rambling and partially censored acceptance speech, during which she said this: "If the mothers ruled the world, there would be no G-- damned war." The Factor actually leaped to Field's defense. "I agree with her - if mothers were in charge of the world, there would be far less war. Women are hard-wired to be nurturing, and guys are hunter-gatherers. As a result, I think women are much more reasonable when it comes to conflict resolution."

The Factor then changed the subject to influential left-wing blogger Jane Hamsher, who denounced Elizabeth Edwards for criticizing the "General Betray Us" ad. Hamsher's admonishment had the tone of a threat: "You never repeat right wing talking points to attack your own, ever ... knock it off!" Democratic strategist Kirsten Powers accused Hamsher of juvenile behavior. "It is like a child wrote this, she's very petulant. But this tone is not unusual when people on the left are dealing with people they feel have gone off the reservation." Talk show host Tammy Bruce elaborated on Hamsher's questionable judgment. "This is the same woman who put Joe Lieberman in blackface on her web site during the Connecticut primary. These are infantile threats - if you don't stop this, we will stop loving you."
Back of Book Segment 
The Great American Culture Quiz
Martha MacCallum and Steve Doocy returned for their weekly Great American Culture Quiz. This week's questions included these: "Who was the author of 'The Wizard of Oz?'" ... "Which president was rumored to be poisoned by his wife?" For the second consecutive week, Doocy spit the bit on the final question, and MacCallum eked out a one-point win.
Pinheads and Patriots 
Who's helping, and who's hurting?
Monday's Patriots: The Dallas Cowboy cheerleaders, who are entertaining U.S. soldiers in Iraq. And the Pinhead: The rapper Nas, who said this about Bill: "When (O'Reilly) wants to come holla at me ... I'll bite his nose off."
Factor Mail 
Viewers sound off
Many of you wrote about the Hardee's commercial showing a teacher dancing salaciously for her male students. Some excerpts:

Jordan Dunn, Greeley, CO: "Mr. O'Reilly, I am 18 and am insulted by the Hardee's commercial about the teacher. I think the company has no respect for my demographic."

John Yates, Vancouver, MA: "I find the Hardee's commercial to be funny. And you, O'Reilly, are a fuddy-duddy."

Millie Meyr, St. Louis, MO: "Such poor taste! We've torn up our Hardee's coupons."

Other viewers weighed in on the MoveOn ad that slandered General Petraeus.

Brian Stauffer, Iraq: "I've been watching the Petraeus testimony and to hear people call him a liar is a disgrace."

Roger Gregory, Sydney, Australia: "The attacks on General Petraeus are vile. I hope Americans react with anger."
