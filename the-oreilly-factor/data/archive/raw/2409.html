Talking Points Memo 
Barney Frank on the offensive on health care
"Barney Frank is on the offensive on health care, trying to convince Americans that the government should run the nation's health care system. But as we all know, that's a very tough sell these days. When asked about the cost of reform at a town hall meeting, he shot back that the Iraq War was a waste of money. What Frank did there is pettifog the issue. He introduced Iraq when the war has nothing to do with the man's question on the present controversy. Frank does that kind of stuff all the time. He does it here when he's presented with points he can't answer. As far as President Obama is concerned, the health care war is breaking down along party lines. Democrats well understand the president can't afford to be embarrassed on the issue. That would taint his entire presidency. But there's no question most Americans are now dissatisfied with the president's health care deal. According to brand new NBC News poll, 47 percent now disapprove of the way Mr. Obama is handling health care reform, while 41 percent approve. It's clear Obama's health care policy is not playing among the folks."
Top Story 
Is Obama losing support from his core?
Political analyst Dick Morris joined The Factor to look at whether Obama's core support was abandoning him over health care reform. Morris said that what Obama had to worry about was the elderly abandoning the Democratic party: "This goes back to Social Security being passed in 1937 when seniors became Democrats because of FDR and Medicare being passed in `65 when it reinforced that under Johnson. The Democratic party could now be losing the elderly." The Factor understood the mindset of these older voters: "So the elderly Americans -- and I think that's what's making up most of this polling -- are saying 'My security is in danger here because I am old. And I am going to have medical problems. And this seismic shift is going to negatively impact me.' It's as simple as that."
Weekdays with Bernie Segment 
David Axelrod's health care conflict of interest
NBC's Meet the Press host David Gregory pointed out on the air that a man carrying a gun at an Obama rally was holding a sign with a slogan favored by Oklahoma City bomber Timothy McVeigh. The Factor asked media analyst Bernie Goldberg if that was a valid comparison. Bernie thought it was not: "The man outside the town hall meeting in New Hampshire is not in the same moral universe as Timothy McVeigh. There is not an ounce of moral equivalence, whether David Gregory meant there to be or not, between an angry but peaceful protester, even one with a gun on his leg, and a psychopathic killer." The Factor saw a pattern emerging from NBC News: "If you look at the sum of NBC's reporting on the protests for the last five weeks the group think is 'These people who come out to the town hall meetings to protest health care are all a bunch of yahoos. They all bang the same drum that there's no legitimate protest here, and it's all far right crazies.'"
Personal Story Segment 
Religious leaders getting involved in health care debate
The Obama administration is now reportedly courting religious leaders to support the health care plan. Sister Simone Campbell, executive director of Network, a Catholic social justice lobby, thought that there was a pretty strong religious basis for reform: "I think what Jesus says over and over again both in our lives and in the scriptures is that it's our responsibility to make sure that the least are cared for. And that it is a societal responsibility. Therefore, we must as a nation make sure that everyone has access to health care." The Factor agreed with the principle, but was worried that Obamacare might do more harm than good: "If the government takes over health care, and begins to ration care, begins to tell people when they can live and when they can die, and the country goes bankrupt in the process, more people will be harmed, including poor people."
Impact Segment 
Will conservatives oppose Obama no matter what?
The Factor asked radio host Monica Crowley if some people will automatically oppose everything Barack Obama wants to do because they hate him so much. Monica insisted that her opposition was based on his policies: "Barack Obama ran for president as a virtual moderate. But since he's been in office, he has governed from the far left. And so even those of us who wanted to give him the benefit of the doubt are going, 'Whoa, he is governing out of control.'" The Factor disagreed that Obama was far left on every issue, pointing out that his Iraq and Afghanistan policies were fairly moderate. "There's a lot of people who have Obama Derangement Syndrome," The Factor said. "They hate him. Every time I say something good about Barack Obama, I get letters. I have vitriolic stuff coming on in."
Miller Time Segment 
Dennis Miller on Barney Frank
Comedian Dennis Miller was actually a little amused by Barney Frank's town hall shoutfest: "When he goes out with the hoi polloi like this, I think he likes to feel intellectually a little superior. And I'll tell you what. He's sort of good at it. It made me laugh. It's sort of like Frederick Nietzsche meets Liberace." The Factor agreed with at least one part of Dennis' assessment: "Barney is a guy who has an attitude. And the attitude is he's always right, and if you don't know it, you're an idiot." The Factor wondered if Barney's attitude would hurt the president. Miller didn't think so: "You know, at this point I don't think Barney cares about Barack. He's in such a weird district, Barney could literally come dressed in the Barney dinosaur outfit to Congress and nobody would care. He's going to get voted in."
Back of Book Segment 
Did You See That?
Fox News anchor Jane Skinner and FoxNews.com reporter Courtney Friel gave their opinions one of the hottest videos on the Internet right now -- a British PSA showing teens getting into a car crash after being distracted by a text message. Friel thought that the ad was effective: "Teenagers' brains aren't fully developed, but they do respond to peer pressure. We need to shock the kids to get this message across. I think that everyone should see this, frankly, because we're all at risk of someone texting and driving and running into us." Skinner agreed: "It was very disturbing. But at the end of the day when I went back and watched it, I decided if I had a teenager who's in a car, I want them to see that. It's like that "Scared Straight" documentary back in the late '70s." The Factor also saw the value: "There are idiots all over the place doing the texting and talking on the phone and all of that business. Even though it's painful to watch, you should watch it."
Pinheads and Patriots 
Don Hewitt & Britney Spears
Wednesday's Patriot: Don Hewitt, the CBS News producer who created "60 Minutes." He died Wednesday at the age of 86. And you decide if she's a Pinhead: Britney Spears, who read David Letterman's Top 10 list wearing a bikini.
