Talking Points Memo 
Our do-nothing Congress goes on vacation
"The House of Representatives left for its August vacation with an abysmal record of accomplishment. This is the same bunch that recently earned the worst national approval in history - nine percent! The anti-exploration Democrats, led by House Speaker Nancy Pelosi, acted more like schoolyard bullies than national leaders as they refused to allow votes on any proposal that would have allowed for more domestic oil production. What exactly has the Democrat-controlled House done for us lately? Congress authorized the FDA to regulate tobacco; it also apologized for slavery and Jim Crow laws; and let's not forget, the House moved to ban in-flight cell phone use. Since it did virtually nothing to alleviate our energy woes, Congress should have done at least what so families have had to do this summer because of high gas prices - cancel their vacation plans."

Top Story 
Race card resurfaces on the campaign trail
Barack Obama has claimed that "they," without specifying whom, will try to scare voters by saying he "doesn't look like all the presidents on the dollar bills." Laura welcomed professor L'Heureux Lewis, who denied that Obama is playing the race card. "He wants to acknowledge that he's different in a number of ways," Lewis contended. "He's younger than most presidents, he has a different heritage. He's interested in policies and he's trying to extend the dialogue beyond race." Bishop Harry Jackson vehemently disagreed. "He's playing the race card. What he hopes to do is to cast a shadow that anything negative McCain says is racist. He did that against Hillary Clinton and it was very powerful. He's trying to say 'you can't attack me.'" Laura pointed out that Barack Obama has been reluctant to debate. "If he wants a substantive debate, why won't he agree to a town hall meeting with John McCain?"

Unresolved Problems Segment 
Outrage over recent immigration raid
Some open-borders advocates are protesting a raid on an Iowa meatpacking plant; the New York Times called immigration raids "a national disgrace." Laura spoke with attorney Benjamin Johnson, who agreed with the Times position. "You're talking about communities," Johnson began, " that are already experiencing economic pain, and this is inflicting more pain on them. We need real solutions, and we need to figure out how to supply legitimate workers to employers who need them. Showing up at these worksites is a show of force to make up for years of inaction and bad immigration policy." Laura told Johnson that most Americans disagree with him. "When you are in this country and have phony Social Security numbers, you'll be charged with fraud and possibly deported. This is federal law, and these measures are wildly popular."

News Link: NYT editorial outraged over immigration raid
Personal Story Segment 
McCain campaign ad drags in celebs
Many Obama supporters object to the McCain TV commercial comparing Barack Obama's celebrity status to that of Paris Hilton and Britney Spears. Entertainment reporter Jeanne Wolf told Laura why she and many in Hollywood find the commercial offensive. "John McCain is respected in this community as a hero," Wolf asserted, "and now he looks silly. I hear all over Hollywood that this commercial shows that he doesn't 'get it.' This is like high school behavior." Laura suggested that McCain doesn't particularly care about the Hollywood vote. "This election isn't about either coast, these guys are talking to the middle of America."

Factor Flashback Segment 
Explaining cosmetic surgery to children
Laura introduced a previously aired segment in which Bill spoke with Margaret Hoover and Marc Rudov about a children's book that tells tots about cosmetic surgery. "The book is pretty reasonable," Hoover said. "You sit down with your kids and read it before the surgery, and the kid realizes that mom is going to be okay. But the problem with our culture is that 40-year old moms feel they need to get a nose job and a boob job and a tummy tuck." Rudov condemned the book and its message. "This book is a sad and pathetic statement about our superficial culture, especially the way women behave. A plasticized woman reminds me of a termite-infested house with fresh paint and new shutters. Little girls are growing up thinking that plastic surgery is a natural part of life." Bill came down as squarely pro-choice on cosmetic surgery. "I would never do it, but I don't think there's anything wrong with improving yourself. And if you have plastic surgery, you have to understand that it will have an impact on your children."
Impact Segment 
Obama's oil profits proposal
Barack Obama wants to tax the "excess" profits of major oil companies; he also claimed that the need to drill for more oil can be negated if Americans inflate their tires properly and get their cars tuned up. Energy analyst Tyson Slocum endorsed Obama's proposals. "This is not the kind of grand vision we expect from presidential candidates," Slocum conceded, "but he is on to something. Tapping into these resources of oil isn't going to bring down prices or make us energy-independent. And there's nothing wrong with making money, there's something wrong with making a windfall profit." Fox News business analyst Jonathan Hoenig argued that oil companies should be free to make money for their shareholders. "Barack Obama can't define what 'excess profit' is, and this is just another way to redistribute income from people who have earned it to people who haven't. ExxonMobil provides a service by giving us a clean and reliable energy source, and their profit margin is lower than Apple Computer, lower than Microsoft, and lower than your local delicatessen." Laura suggested that some on the left like high oil prices because "we'll become more like Europe with little clown cars and bikes and scooters."

Factor Follow Up Segment 
Obama distances himself from Ludacris
In his new "song," the profane rapper Ludacris wishes paralysis on John McCain, calls Hillary Clinton a "b _ _ _ _ ," and talks about painting the White House black. Barack Obama, who previously praised Ludacris for his talent and business acumen, is now denouncing the rapper and his latest work. Laura welcomed author and columnist John McWhorter, who has written extensively about rap. "I'm not going to stick up for that particular song," McWhorter said, "but if you listen to Ludacris, he is an artist. A lot of the stuff is more fun than you think, so I can see why Obama would have liked Ludacris' past work." Laura took issue with McWhorter's critique. "I looked up a Ludacris 'song' and it is so filthy, talking about women as whores and using every possible word. You understate how poisonous this stuff is."

Back of Book Segment 
Is the "Montauk Monster" real?
A mysterious and grotesque animal corpse has washed up on a beach in Montauk, New York; people claim the hideous looking beast was a dog, a raccoon, or even Satan. The Animal Planet's Maggie Gallant reported that the gargoyle lookalike has become the talk of the town. "This 'monster' has become the biggest celebrity of the week. This area is where beautiful people go to look fabulous, and they're not used to something not so beautiful on their beaches. The rumor is that scientists have taken this animal to be tested to find out exactly what it is. My colleague Jeff Corwin thinks this may be some kind of raccoon, and a lot of people think this could even be a PR stunt. If it is, it's genius!"
