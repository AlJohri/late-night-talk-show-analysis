Talking Points Memo and Top Story 
The Democrats strike back
Watch more video in the Video Center...
"New Rasmussen polls show that Mitt Romney has surged in Florida, Ohio, and Virginia, and the Democratic Party has decided to go into full attack mode. The strategy seems to be simple: Call Mitt Romney a liar. Does the DNC really believe that kind of rhetoric will help the President, that independent voters will respond positively? For the Democrats to call Mitt Romney a liar is foolish and destructive to their guy because the Obama campaign has made just as many false and misleading statements as the Romney camp. Beyond that, there is the usual hysteria from rank ideologues. Racial hustler Michael Eric Dyson has injected color into the debate, claiming President Obama could not speak candidly because he can't come across as 'an angry black man.' Here's the reality, Mr. Dyson: You can not analyze anything without injecting race. Both President Obama and Governor Romney should warn their campaigns to stop the slander. The truth is that the President could not defend his economic record on Wednesday night and that's why Governor Romney won the debate. 'Lying' had nothing to do with it."

The Factor solicited reaction from Democrats Mary Anne Marsh and Kirsten Powers. "I am shocked to say," Powers confessed, "that I agree with your Talking Points. It is not right for the Democratic Party to go out and call Mitt Romney a liar. When a campaign starts using that word, a lot of people will think they're being nasty." Marsh opined that President Obama himself should have been better prepared. "The better course would have been to correct the statements during the debate, but now, with 32 days left, is when you call your opponent's truthfulness into question. The problem is that once you use the 'l word' there aren't a lot of places left to go." The Factor added that Democrats risk looking churlish: "This comes across as petulant 5th grade stuff. I got my butt kicked, so now I'm going to call the other side names."
Impact Segment 
Unemployment rate drops to 7.8%
The official unemployment number has dipped below 8% for the first time since Barack Obama took office. Fox Business anchor Lou Dobbs analyzed the jobless number. "It's good news for President Obama," Dobbs stated, "but it doesn't really describe the state of unemployment and real pain in the country. 23-million Americans remain unemployed or have given up hope of being employed." Dobbs also dismissed the notion that the Bureau of Labor Statistics manipulated the unemployment figure. "That's nonsense! The last thing that anyone should be looking at in these numbers is somebody cooking the books." The Factor contended that Friday's unemployment number is not nearly as important as some other dire figures: "Household median income is down $5,000 since the President has been in office, and there are rising gas prices, rising health insurance prices, and rising-almost-everything prices."
Fridays with Geraldo Segment 
Pro-Obama union puts out anti-Romney ad featuring his garbage collector
A pro-Obama public employee union has actually put out an ad in which Mitt Romney's garbage collector complains that Romney has never come out to shake his hand or give him a hug. The Factor scrutinized the trashy spot with Geraldo Rivera. "Richard Hayes is the garbage collector at Romney's La Jolla mansion," Rivera reported, "and he's a member of AFSCME, a vehement opponent of Romney's. The Democrats and this militant group of public employees are trying to portray Romney as a man who is out of touch. It's really a low blow, it's like the ad that accused Romney of killing a guy's wife." The Factor denounced the ad as ineffective and in poor taste: "This is bogus and I guess they're appealing to ignorant voters." Rivera also explained why his 33-year-old son declined an invitation to enter the No Spin Zone, saying, "He said he didn't want to be the stooge for O'Reilly, he doesn't agree with your politics."
Body Language Segment 
Breakdown of the presidential debate body language
Watch more video in the Video Center...
While political pundits are still scrutinizing the words exchanged in Wednesday's debate, Tonya Reiman analyzed the candidates' body language. She began by observing how the rivals entered the stage and greeted one another. "The first thing I noticed," Reiman said, "was the swagger that the President came out with, while Romney was very stiff. They shook hands and patted each other on the arm, which is an attempt to demonstrate power. Romney tried to break away but Obama held on for just a beat longer. He had the same dominant body language with John McCain in 2008." Reiman also commented on President Obama's tendency to look down while Mitt Romney was speaking. "Typically when you look down you're trying to show disagreement, and you could see that with the President's clenched jaw. Governor Romney used very powerful gestures, but he has to get rid of his smirk. People think of it as arrogant when he smirks like that."
What the Heck Just Happened 
Advice for the O'Reilly-Stewart Rumble
Friday regulars Greg Gutfeld and Bernard McGuirk also weighed in on Wednesday night's debate. "How can this be a surprise?" Gutfeld wondered. "You have a successful businessman who wiped the floor with a grad student. You have a guy who knows jobs and a guy who knows Jay-Z. There was no comparison and Mitt had to win big. If he had merely won, the press would have given it to Obama, so he had to decimate Obama just to get a victory." The Factor asked McGuirk to identify President Obama's strongest moment during the debate. "That's like asking me to highlight the strong points in the Italian Army in World War II," McGuirk quipped. "But if you get past some of the hatred on the right and some of the abject panic on the left, President Obama didn't look at his watch, he didn't yawn, he didn't give a shout-out to Reverend Wright, and he didn't ask for any bathroom breaks."

On the eve of the great Bill O'Reilly vs. Jon Stewart debate, McGuirk and Gutfeld gave The Factor some last-minute advice. "You're going onto Jon Stewart's turf on a college campus," McGuirk pointed out, "so you have to come out a little different - maybe on a skateboard wearing a hoodie and flashing the peace sign. Tomorrow night could also be a good night for you to come out of the closet because they eat that up, they love that." Gutfeld added that some college students in attendance might need medical attention. "This may be the first time anybody in the audience actually sees a real-life conservative who hasn't been written into an HBO mini-series, so they might be shocked. There could be some fainting, so I'll be there to give mouth-to-mouth to anybody."
