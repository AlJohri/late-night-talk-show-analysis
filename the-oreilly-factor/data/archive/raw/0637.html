Talking Points Memo & Top Story 
Will a hero emerge in Vermont?
Guest: Vermont Attorney General William Sorrell

"Plenty of villains are surfacing in that terrible Vermont child rape case, where Judge Edward Cashman sentenced child rapist Mark Hulett to 60 days in prison. Villain number one is the Vermont print media, with the exception of the Burlington Free Press. In a shocking editorial, the far left Brattleboro Reformer declared that 'lengthy jail terms without rehabilitation will not accomplish anything.' This is how insane things are in Vermont - this newspaper believes long prison terms for child rapists accomplish nothing. The editor of the Brattleboro paper is clueless and that editorial is grossly irresponsible. Vermont Judge Amy Davenport has also emerged as a villain. She could remove the judge from criminal cases and put him in civil court. Ms. Davenport has not done that. The bottom line - few powerful people care about this little girl, who was brutalized and terrorized for four long years. She is poor and now a ward of the state, and she must be very frightened and confused. How could something like this happen in the United States of America? How could it happen?"

Fox News Video: FoxNews.com

The Factor was joined by Vermont Attorney General William Sorrell, who criticized the light sentence but vigorously defended his state. "The suggestion that Vermonters don't care about sex crimes is unfair and untrue. Our jails are full, primarily with sex offenders and those guilty of domestic violence. We filed a motion for the judge to reconsider his sentence, and we're hopeful he will. We think the judge should have considered punishment and not just rehabilitation." The Factor asked Sorrell why Vermont's citizens and leaders seem so passive. "Can you point out one public demonstration anywhere objecting to Judge Cashman's sentence? With all due respect, do you realize how passionless you are coming off here? I think the impotence of the state of Vermont in the face of this atrocity is insane."

Impact Segment 
Senator Clinton causes controversy
Guest: Al Sharpton

Speaking before a largely black audience in Harlem, Senator Hillary Clinton compared the Republican-led House of Representatives to a "plantation." Some conservatives accused her of race baiting, but Reverend Al Sharpton defended the comparison. "Plantations did not have the right to protest or dissent, and that's what she was saying. These conservatives are the same ones who defended Clarence Thomas saying his hearing was a 'high tech lynching.' They can't have it both ways." The Factor reminded Sharpton that Senator Clinton refuses to appear on the show. "The solution is for you to tell her to come on the program. I'll give you an hour - you and Hillary Clinton and me. And we'll get to the bottom of all of this." Sharpton promised to pass along the invitation to Senator Clinton.

Impact Segment II 
Sharpton on Nagin
Guest: Al Sharpton

While Hillary Clinton was using racially charged words in New York, New Orleans Mayor Ray Nagin pledged that his city will again be "chocolate" because "it's the way God wants it to be." Mayor Nagin later apologized, which Sharpton said was appropriate. "He was right to apologize, but there are concerns about relocation, and the mayor was right to say people who lived there should have the right to come back." On another topic, Reverend Sharpton expressed indignation about the Vermont rape case. "I want to go to Vermont and look into this case. Maybe people need to know that people like me care about girls like her." The Factor suggested that both Vermont and Sharpton could benefit from his involvement. "The state needs somebody to rally the folks. And people will say it's not just about race for Sharpton."

Unresolved Problems Segment 
Tough law to punish immigrant help
Guest: James Rogers, Jesuit Conference

The House passed a bill that would severely punish anyone who harbors or helps illegal aliens. James Rogers of the Jesuit Conference vehemently objected to the proposed legislation. "If you're trying to build a compassionate nation, you can not do this. How we will be judged at the end of our lives will depend on what we have done for the least among us. We have great faith that the Senate will do the right thing and reverse this approach." The Factor acknowledged Rogers' good intentions, but asserted that a nation has a duty to protect its borders. "The government has to be able to say you can not come here unless you go through legal channels. But you're telling me that if you find someone who is here illegally you're going to help them. You may be aiding and abetting a crime."

Factor Follow Up Segment 
Movies making gays mainstream?
Guests: Fox News entertainment reporter Bill McCuddy & film critic Michael Medved

The gay-themed film "Brokeback Mountain," a major winner at the Golden Globe Awards, is considered a front-runner for an Academy Award. Conservative film critic Michael Medved claimed the movie and others like it are Hollywood's attempt at indoctrination. "They're promoting this as saying 'love is a force of nature.' This is a very insidious message because the love they show in the movie destroys two marriages. The movie is not just pro-homosexual, but it's anti-marriage." Medved also claimed that the critically acclaimed films "Syriana" and "Good Night, and Good Luck" were bankrolled by a far left organization. "I have no problem with liberal message movies. The problem is that there are no conservative message movies. There ought to be balance in this country." Fox News reporter Bill McCuddy pointed out that director Ang Lee isn't responsible for "Brokeback Mountain's" advertising campaign. "Film makers don't decide how their movies are going to be marketed, that's something the studio does. The movie is doing better than you think - it's made $32 million in 39 days." The Factor joked that he will not lining up to see "Brokeback Mountain." "I'm afraid of gay cowboys, but then I'm afraid of all cowboys. I'm from Long Island."

Back of Book Segment 
Abortion laws in New Hampshire
Guest: Fox News chief judicial analyst Judge Andrew Napolitano

By a vote of 9-0, the Supreme Court refused to strike down a New Hampshire law that requires girls younger than 18 to notify their parents or a judge prior to undergoing an abortion. Fox News analyst Andrew Napolitano hailed the decision as good news for parents. "This decision says there is nothing in the Constitution to prevent a state from requiring the involvement of parents in an abortion. And it is the first abortion decision in modern times that was unanimous." The Factor pointed out that some groups will consider this a terrible setback. "The pro-choice people who are on the extreme - Planned Parenthood and the ACLU - are horrified by this ruling. But it's a victory for parents and I'm glad to hear it."
