Talking Points Memo & Top Story 
Power and the Fox News Channel
"Extremists on both the right and the left did not like my interview with President Obama, but for far different reasons. On the far right, many people simply despise the President and want him disparaged at all times. But on the far left, this is about power, and they are losing it. The uber-liberal coverage of my interview is nothing short of hysterical. I really got a kick out of Bill Maher telling the world he was offended by the tone of the interview, that I was far too aggressive with the President. Bill Maher was offended! Putting that aside, there is a serious issue here, and that is that the interview made Fox News look credible and responsible. We covered a lot of ground in a fair and balanced way; thus the world saw the real Fox News - an aggressive operation but a fair one. That damages the far left tremendously, so they have to spin the situation. As Fox News gets stronger, the far left gets weaker. The bottom line is that power is shifting in America away from the left wing zealots and toward a more moderate, traditional approach to problem-solving. I believe last Sunday proved that."

Radio talk show host Laura Ingraham expanded on the left's obsession with Fox News and the Sunday interview. "We went through a couple of the craziest left wing websites," Ingraham said, "and one of them mentioned Fox News 17 times in the last 17 hours. The left doesn't know what to do with liberalism because it's not working on a whole host of issues, so they've defined themselves by demonizing Fox. It doesn't surprise me that they're criticizing you for the interview because if they don't criticize you, they actually have to stand for something." The Factor reiterated that the interview bolstered Fox's credibility: "This is such a nightmare for the uber-left because the world was watching and this was a huge success for Fox News. It takes away all the lies and propaganda that these people trade on."
Impact Segment 
Mixed approval ratings for Pres. Obama
According to a new poll, a plurality of Americans approves of how President Obama is dealing with Egypt and Afghanistan, but large majorities disapprove of his handling of the budget deficit and taxes. The Factor asked political strategist Dick Morris the break down the numbers. "The President's overall job approval is between 47% and 50%," Morris said, "but on every single issue his approval is below that. Obama is moving into a new political dimension where he's coming across as a nice guy and a good person, but not an effective president. Most people watching your interview would say they like the guy but they don't agree with him, and, ultimately, voters vote on issues." Morris advised the President to temper his optimistic pronouncements. "He's making a great big mistake when he tells people the recession is over, that we've turned the corner. It gives people the feeling that he's just totally out of touch."
Personal Story Segment 
Gov. Tim Pawlenty's White House ambitions
Former Minnesota Governor Tim Pawlenty, a prospective Republican presidential contender in 2012, entered the No Spin Zone to express his main concerns. "There are some big issues lurking," Pawlenty began, "and one is the Ponzi scheme that is the federal government and its spending patterns, and President Obama's lack of urgency and seriousness about that. It concerns me a great deal and that's going to be a huge issue. People see a government that is not working very well, that is growing and spending like crazy." Pawlenty also criticized the President's foreign policy. "There's a sense that he doesn't view America's role in the world as exceptional. You asked him twice during that interview during the Super Bowl whether he would allow the Muslim Brotherhood to lead Egypt, and he ducked the question."
Body Language Segment 
Body language of Bill's Obama interview
Body language expert Tonya Reiman focused her trained eye on the pre-Super Bowl interview. "Look at your body language compared to President Obama's," Reiman observed. "It's very different and you guys weren't in rapport. You're sitting on the edge of your chair in a bit of a 'pounce' position, while he's leaning back. You're very open, but he's a little more closed, which tells me he was expecting some heavy questions. Because of those differences, you can see that you guys were not in synch. You might have been getting a good back-and-forth, but it wasn't a smooth back-and-forth." Reiman also analyzed President Obama's tendency to laugh at the beginning of some answers. "He's deflecting in order to give himself time and also to mask the emotion that he's feeling, which was probably annoyance or frustration. We always do that - we laugh to deflect."
Miller Time Segment 
Dennis Miller on the Super Bowl
Dennis Miller entered the No Spin Zone to register a complaint about the O'Reilly-Obama interview. "It seems to me," Miller said, "that it's important to you that he likes you, but that's not what I'm looking for in an interview with Barack Obama. I want him to come in without a tie and leave in a neck brace. When he's meeting O'Reilly, I want him to dread it beforehand, I want him to sweat bullets in the middle of it, and afterwards I want him to say to Axelrod, what did I just say? With Barack Obama, it doesn't come down to whether I like him or not - I see this country as lean and mean, and I want the citizens to be self-reliant. He sees it as soft and he's willing that some of the citizens be dependent. I would have said, Mr. President, there's a big game later today - do you think the winning team should give the losing team enough of their points so that it's a tie because that's fair?" The Factor explained that verbal altercation was not part of the game plan: "I wanted to establish for millions of people all over the world who hear that we're the devil that we're not the devil. I also wanted to show the world that we're not in business to demean Barack Obama."
Back of Book Segment 
Did you see that?
Fox Business anchor Dagen McDowell picked the finest and most foolish ads that aired during the Super Bowl. Her pick of as the worst of the lot was the nightclub spot for Stella Artois beer starring actor Adrian Brody. "If you're going to hire an Oscar winner," McDowell asked, "wouldn't you pick somebody that people in this country recognize? Name one movie that he's made since he won that Academy Award. This ad didn't make me want to drink beer, it made me want to drink bleach." And the pick of the litter - the Doritos ad featuring a dog running through a door to reach the snack. "This was a one-two punch," McDowell said, "of a cute little doggie and a dude falling on his butt. It's a winning combination every time."
