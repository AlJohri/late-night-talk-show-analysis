Top Story 
Trump's Surge
Eric began Wednesday's program with Donald Trump's continuing dominance of the Republican race and his escalating feud with Hillary Clinton. "It doesn't seem like Donald Trump can make a mistake," said Republican strategist Chris Begala, "even though the mainstream media and everyone on the left wants to attack him at every point. Those pesky folks known as American citizens don't really care - they are sick of politicians and Donald Trump is riding this wave while Hillary's energy level looks woeful. She went globe-trotting as secretary of state and never uttered a single world about all the women around the world who have no civil liberties." Political analyst Eboni Williams theorized that both front-runners may benefit from their war of words. "I think this is good for Trump, but Hillary benefits too. There will be some people who are offended by this language from Trump and she will have a default victory from the anti-Trump people."
Campaign 2016 
Steering Clear of Trump
Some Republicans, notably Chris Christie and Ted Cruz, have been loath to criticize front-runner Donald Trump. Eric analyzed their reluctance with FNC's Monica Crowley. "Chris Christie learned his lesson from other candidates," she said, "while Ted Cruz has played this brilliantly from the start. He understood Donald Trump and he created somewhat of an alliance with Trump. Christie learned that anytime anyone attacks Trump, Trump hits back and he can flatten you. I think it's very smart for them just to lay back, run their own campaigns, and let Donald Trump run his own campaign." Crowley also lambasted the Washington Post cartoonist who depicted Cruz's two daughters as trained monkeys. "This comes from a leftist political cartoonist at the leftist Washington Post, and this exposes the inherent racism of some on the left and their indulgence in identity politics. Can you imagine if a cartoonist portrayed a liberal's children in this way? Holy Hell would break loose!"
Unresolved Problems 
Refugee Crisis
The Obama administration plans to admit at least 10-thousand refugees next year, even though most Americans are opposed to that idea. Eric asked Pastor Robert Jeffress how Christians should view this political issue. "Most people assume that the Christian response is to allow immigrants and refugees unlimited entrance into our country," Jeffress said, "but I think they are confusing Jesus Christ and the Statue of Liberty. The historical Jesus did urge compassion for those in need, but he also said we should render unto Caesar the things that are Caesar's. In other words, support government with your taxes because the government has the legitimate function of protecting citizens. Those of us who believe in the sanctity of life believe in protecting people from terrorist attacks. That's a Christian value as well." Jeffress donned his political analyst hat and surveyed the current Republican battle. "This has come down to a two-man race between Trump and Cruz, especially in Iowa. If I were going to handicap it, I would say Cruz has a slight edge right now, but Trump is also doing amazingly well with evangelicals. They believe Trump is going to protect us, which is the number one desire of all Americans."
Miller Time 
Wrapping up the Year
Eric introduced a few of Dennis Miller's greatest hits. This was Miller after the most recent GOP debate in Las Vegas: "I would vote for any of them over Hillary," he said. "To say that Hillary lies like a rug is to forget that during a tornado a rug might get airborne. She is a serial liar and a breakfast cereal liar because she starts lying the moment she opens her eyes. I missed most of the debate because I had the option of being water boarded, so I did that instead." Miller also offered some unsolicited advice to marijuana aficionado Willie Nelson, who will sell his own brand of pot in states where the drug is legal. "I have some marketing ideas for him. If you order an ounce, it's a 'full Nelson,' a half-ounce is a 'half Nelson,' and a quarter-ounce is a 'little Willie. The guy is 81 years old and when he gets to Heaven it'll be a little cloudier up there."
Unresolved Problems 
U.S. Deportations
The Obama administration has revealed that fewer illegal immigrants are being deported than at any time in a decade. Eric asked Republican strategist Evan Siegfried and Democratic strategist Chris Hahn to scrutinize the numbers. "There are fewer illegal immigrants coming into the country," Siegfried quipped, "because Obama's economy is so bad for them. Right now 30% of the illegal immigrants coming here are criminals and there are tremendous problems." But Hahn endorsed the fact that more immigrants, especially children, are being allowed to stay in the United States. "This is the only country these people have ever known, we should take them out of the shadows and make them part of our society. People on the left are criticizing President Obama for deporting too many people, people on the right are criticizing him for deporting too few. This president has done a good job of protecting the country."
Watters' World 
Watters' World's Wildest of 2015
Finally, Eric introduced a few of Jesse Watters' most memorable segments from the year gone by. Watters spoke with some heavily-inked folks at a Long Island tattoo festival. "For me this is a road map of all of the sketchy places I have been in my life," one tattoo aficionado told Watters, while another man said, "Nothing I have on me means anything." Watters also spoke with drivers stuck in New York traffic, which seems to have gotten worse under Mayor Bill de Blasio. Here's what a few frustrated drivers had to say: "De Blasio is making us worse than Los Angeles" ... "He's not doing a good job, that's for sure." Then, when he headed south to Miami, Watters visited a high-priced art show. Here is what some of the sophisticates told him: "Art is love and art is passion" ... "Artists are about truth" ... "Artists are about critical thinking." Sylvester Stallone was in attendance and put forth this art-related compliment to a certain humble correspondent: "Bill O'Reilly is a wordsmith and he paints with vowels."
