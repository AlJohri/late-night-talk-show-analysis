Talking Points Memo & Top Story 
The battle of the vice presidents
"Polls show Americans are somewhat divided over how the feds should fight terrorism. 59% of Americans want captured terrorists tried by the military; 55% want the prison at Guantanamo Bay to remain open. However, 55% of Americans believe President Obama is doing a good job on terrorism. So most Americans disagree with the President on two key anti-terror issues, but they generally support him on terrorism. Yesterday there was an interesting political display on the Sunday morning programs. Dick Cheney said trying Khalid Sheikh Mohammed in civilian court is a big mistake, as is closing down Guantanamo Bay. In reply, Vice President Joe Biden said the Obama administration is fighting terrorism with unprecedented 'vigor.' Both sides have a decent argument - the Bush administration's aggressive anti-terror policies kept the nation safe for seven years; the Obama administration has terrorized the terrorists with a very aggressive drone missile program. However, there is a major philosophical difference. The Bush administration saw the war as a war, while the Obama administration largely sees it as criminal activity. We are in a war with the jihadists - that's the truth, and I believe truth is a powerful weapon in defeating the Islamic killers who are trying to destroy us."

The Factor asked FNC political analyst Brit Hume to assess the war of words between Cheney and Biden. "It's worth looking beyond the back and forth," Hume said, "to the broader question of how effective Dick Cheney has been in his attacks against the administration. This administration sent out a sitting vice president to respond to Cheney, a man they claim they're delighted to be in an argument with because he's so unpopular. If that were so, they wouldn't be so sensitive about him." Hume also suggested that Attorney General Eric Holder may soon be out of a job. "If anybody in that administration is on thin ice, you would think it would be Holder. In political terms, he has been nothing but trouble for this administration and for Democrats." The Factor predicted that Holder's desire to try terrorists in federal court will go unfulfilled: "I think there's a good chance that Khalid Sheikh Mohammed will go back to being tried in a military court, which will be a tremendous embarrassment to the administration."

For more on the battling VPs, The Factor welcomed FNC analysts Juan Williams and Mary Katharine Ham. "Vice President Biden has the stronger argument," Williams averred, "if you just look at the facts. The Bush administration handled people under the criminal justice system in civilian courts and it was a success. I don't think Dick Cheney is the best spokesman for criticizing the Obama administration's activity - people see him as extremist and he is very unpopular." Naturally, Ham felt Cheney won by a TKO. "People don't trust Dick Cheney on other issues, but on terror he's the tough guy and people are listening to him. The American people like their president to be tough on terror, but President Obama has deputies out there, people like Eric Holder and John Brennan, sounding like they are complacent."
Impact Segment 
Will California reject liberal politicians?
With California on the brink of insolvency, The Factor welcomed two of the Golden State's most prominent radio talk show hosts. Leslie Marshall placed the blame squarely on Governor Arnold Schwarzenegger. "This is his fault," Marshall declared. "This is a guy who said, 'I'm going to come in and fix this problem,' but it became worse." Tammy Bruce, who was on Schwarzenegger's transition team, also lambasted the governor. "He said he was a Reaganesque conservative, but apparently this is what happens when you sleep with a Kennedy every night. We've seen Massachusetts elect a pretty serious conservative, and Californians are ready to do the same." The Factor opined that at least some of the blame lies with the ultra-liberal state legislature: "Is it even possible for Governor Schwarzenegger to fix anything knowing how many loons are in Sacramento? He's outnumbered and outgunned."
Personal Story Segment 
Alabama college prof murders 3, injures 3
Professor Amy Bishop, apparently irate at being denied tenure, pulled out a handgun and killed three of her colleagues Friday. It has since been learned that Bishop shot and killed her 18-year-old brother in 1986. Fox News anchor Megyn Kelly elaborated on that earlier shooting. "She fired three rounds," Kelly reported, "one into the ceiling, one into the wall, and one into her brother. She claimed it was an accident, but she reportedly threatened other people and tried to escape. She was questioned, but the case was dropped. And in 1993, she and her husband were reportedly suspects in connection with a mail pipe bombing of a Harvard professor. They could never prove it." Kelly also analyzed the case of Alissa Blanton, a Florida woman who was stalked and killed by a 61-year-old man. "The person responsible for is the psycho stalker murderer," Kelly said, "but there were many others who could have stepped in. This girl went to the police and said this freak is stalking me and she requested a restraining order. But Judge Dean Moxley would not issue an order of protection. That's insanity!" The Factor argued that Judge Moxley is partly responsible for the killing: "It is his fault in that he did not do his job and protect someone who needed protection."
Weekdays with Bernie Segment 
Who was stronger: Biden or Cheney?
FNC media analyst Bernie Goldberg evaluated the Sunday duel between Dick Cheney and Joe Biden. "The media loves confrontation and drama," Goldberg observed, "and that's what we had - dueling vice presidents slugging it out over terrorism. We now know that the White House is really troubled by Dick Cheney. They put Biden on before Cheney on NBC and after Cheney on CBS, so they got the first word and the last word. These guys may not be good at fixing the economy or getting people back to work, but they're really good at media strategy. That makes me feel a lot better about what's going on in Washington." The Factor summed up the VP "debate" thusly: "I think Cheney won by a little bit because when he comes on he has one big cannon - saying we weren't attacked again. So when he talks, people listen."
Back of Book Segment 
Reality Check: Offensive Australian ad?
Australian TV aired a commercial in which a woman boasts about "porking" her husband. After showing the spot, which promotes pork, The Factor offered this Check: "I think I'm going to become a vegetarian." And in Scotland, a man dropped his kilt and bared all while a TV reporter was doing a live report. The Factor's Check: "There is one rule for all TV reporters to follow: Never involve drunken people in a live shot, especially guys wearing kilts."
Pinheads and Patriots 
Jamie McMurray & Seth MacFarlane
Monday's Patriot: NASCAR driver Jamie McMurray, who expressed unalloyed joy after eking out a win in the Daytona 500. And the Pinhead: Seth MacFarlane, producer of the animated show "Family Guy," which tastelessly mocked Sarah Palin.
