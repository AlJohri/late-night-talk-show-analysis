Top Story 
Surprise ruling by judge blocks Arizona immigration law
U.S. District Judge Susan Bolton, a Clinton appointee, blocked key sections of Arizona's anti-illegal immigration law and granted the Obama administration's request for an injunction arguing essentially that immigration matters should be handled by the federal government. Chapman University law professor John Eastman spoke with Laura about the legal fallout from the ruling. The good news for supporters of the AZ law, according to Eastman: "Sanctuary cities in Arizona are still illegal. If you're interfering with federal enforcement of federal immigration law, you can be prosecuted. And citizens can bring suit against you." Eastman continued, "the second thing is what the judge enjoined or stopped effectiveness of is the requirement that every law enforcement official in Arizona enforce federal law. They still have the discretion to enforce federal law." Ingraham responded "so what you're saying basically is when the police want -- if they want to ask somebody about their immigration status, they can. But they can't be required to under this preliminary junction." Eastman agreed and explained the judge's reasoning, "Because her argument is that if every single law enforcement was required to, it would overwhelm the federal government. And it would impose on their resources." Summing up, Eastman said that "what they're trying do is chill law enforcement from enforcing federal law at all" and Ingraham agreed, pointing out that "they're going to be afraid to... because the lawsuits are coming."

After the ruling came down, Arizona Governor Jan Brewer (R) promised to continue the fight to keep Arizona citizens safe, saying, "We are going to continue to request that we get heard on this, and that the citizens of Arizona are protected. I think that it's important to remind everybody that today, they absolutely, the federal government got relief from the courts to not to do their job. And that means that now they've got this temporary injunction. They need to step up, the Feds do, and do the job that they have the responsibility to do for the people of America and for the people of Arizona." Laura spoke with two immigration activists who support amnesty for illegals and were happy with the ruling: Cathy Areu, publisher of Catalina Magazine and Ali Noorani, the executive director of the National Immigration Forum. Noorani said the judge came down with this ruling because, " She feels the government has a very strong case that questions the constitutionality of this law. Therefore, we are very happy in that she took this ruling." Areu claimed that "what's led to these laws is pure racism" and compared Arizona to the Jim Crow laws. Ingraham disagreed with these arguments and pointed out that "the majority of Americans supported this law... This is a fact. Gallup, Rasmussen... The majority of people say well, why shouldn't they be able to check on someone's status?" In closing, Ingraham simply said, "The reality is the people of this country are fed up. That's the reality."
Impact Segment 
Political implications of Arizona ruling
Laura Ingraham asked political analyst Dick Morris if "a long drawn out court battle over the Arizona law good for the Democrats and this president?" Morris doesn't see a silver lining for Democrats, "No, it's very bad. And in fact, the lawsuit itself was very bad. And winning the lawsuit is very bad. Be careful what you wish for. Obama should never have brought this lawsuit. And having brought it, he should have prayed he lost it, because now he's in a position of taking a law that two-thirds of the American people support." Morris also thinks that "this will probably be reversed by the Supreme Court. Laura played devil's advocate and argued the Obama administration's position, saying, "We're doing more than the Bush administration did. We're sending National Guard troops down to the border. We're fingerprinting aliens. You know, we're putting the resources in, but this is a diversion of resources to require these police officers to check the status. And we have to go through our system. Check the status. It diverts attention and resources. Let's go after those real criminal aliens." Morris countered, "There are two flaws to that argument. First of all, the success the Obama administration has had in reducing the flow of the illegal immigration to the United States is not due to his enforcement. It's due to the recession. All right? There are no jobs here. So the number of illegal immigrants crossing the border, of course, has been decreased. And that's a wildly successful Obama program, this recession. The second problem is this argument that we go after the criminals, we don't go after everybody is totally specious. In Orange County, they used to have it as a sanctuary county, where you couldn't go after people on immigration. And the theory was that illegal immigrants would tell on others and you could solve crimes. Then they repealed the sanctuary and enforcement went way up and crime rate dropped. And the reason is that you often couldn't get a guy on drugs, but you could get him on being here illegally. And you could send him out of the country. and the result was a big reduction in the crime rate." Morris closed by arguing that the President was supposed to be post-racial but in fact he's a racial president and has used situations like the Arizona law to try and gain support from Hispanics. Laura discussed this in detail later in the show.
Factor Follow Up Segment 
Support for war waning amongst Democrats
Are the leaked military documents eroding support for the fight in Afghanistan among Democrats? Liberal Talk Radio Host Nancy Skinner made a partisan argument, calling Afghanistan " George Bush's war." Laura didn't like that line of reasoning and said, "I'm not focusing on George Bush. That old saw has really worn thin" and turned the tables on Skinner by pointing out that Obama is "at odds with the base of his party. He's at odds with the base of his party and actually, he is following more of the Bush approach in Afghanistan." Skinner stuck with the partisan argument: "I know so many Republicans... who don't think it's time to get out of Afghanistan."
Miller Time Segment 
The best of Dennis Miller
In the "Miller Time" segment, our pal Dennis had the night off so we decided to put together the best of Miller over the past few months. Miller on the General McChrystal mess: "Think of McChrystal as Jack Nicholson on 'A Few Good Men' and think of Obama as Tom Cruise in 'A Few Good Men.' I think McChrystal was percolating and he was going to blow because he thinks Obama has no code." Miller isn't a fan of the World Cup: "I don't know how sexy is it when a guy says I just participated in a 0-0 tie. You know, it's just not that sexy. I can miss an entire game and see all the scoring that the guy who watches the whole game sees. I mean, what's going on here?"
Unresolved Problems Segment 
Obama the divider?
In a provocative Wall Street Journal OpEd, two former advisors to Democratic presidents argued that, "Rather than being a unifier, Mr. Obama has divided America on the basis of race, class and partisanship." One of the co-authors of the piece, Doug Schoen, who was pollster for former President Bill Clinton, joined Laura. Laura asked, "And you say that this president far from being post racial is in many ways the most racial figure that we've had in the office for some time. Why?" Doug said, "Well, Laura, if you look at what has happened recently with the Arizona Immigration Law, which you were discussing... It's clearly an attempt to divide and mobilize the Hispanic vote. The president and the Skip Gates case rushed to judgment. This isn't the kind of behavior you would expect from somebody who said he was going to unify the country, bring people together and be post partisan and hopefully beyond race. It's very sad, I think." Doug also argued that this divisive leadership has political consequences, "The only reason you're going to want President Obama now in a swing state or a swing congressional district is to raise money. But as election day approaches, I think more and more Democrats are going to be afraid and more and more Democrats are going to distance themselves from the administration."
Back of Book Segment 
Student clashes with school over beliefs
At the campus of Augusta State University in Georgia, twenty- four-year-old Jennifer Keeton is a graduate student studying to be a school counselor and she's filed a lawsuit against the university claiming they are trying to kick her out of the master's program unless she changes her position on homosexuality. According to the law suit, Augusta State thinks her Jennifer's Christian beliefs are unethical and that they're incompatible with the code of ethics and counseling in the entire profession. Ingraham spoke with Jennifer's attorney David French of the Alliance Defense Fund. French argued that "It's absolutely not a direct conflict with the Code of Ethics" and "the Code of Ethics prohibits religious discrimination." French continued "telling a Christian student she has to change her beliefs or she will be expelled from a program... is religious discrimination."
