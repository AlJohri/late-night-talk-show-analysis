Talking Points 
Illegal Immigrants
"Homeland Security has launched raids in Los Angeles and the New York City area, arresting thousands of people who are in the country illegally. These raids are extremely controversial, and the open-border lobby has condemned them. There is no question that many in the liberal media do not want to stop the flow of illegals into the USA. This point of view is best illustrated by the Houston Chronicle, which calls the proposed border fence 'hostile symbolism.' The open-border crowd opposes the fence, sanctions on people who hire illegals, deportations, and on and on. In San Diego the border fence has actually cut illegal crossings and crime, and the fence will make it much more difficult for smugglers to bring cocaine and heroin to Houston, a city that has a huge crime problem. The fence will make it harder for individuals to cross illegally and many won't even try. Talking Points is generally sympathetic to honest people who have come to the USA to earn a living and have taken advantage of the feds looking the other way for more than twenty years. But now the situation is out of control and most Americans want the problem solved without blanket amnesty. So the open-border crowd must be called out and challenged. Illegal immigration is hurting the country; the law must be defined and enforced."
Top Story 
Racist Professor?
During an appearance last week on CNN, Syracuse University professor Boyce Watkins called Bill a "racist" and Juan Williams a "happy Negro." "This guy was intending to marginalize me," Williams pronounced, "and make me out to be some kind of 'Uncle Tom' and a self-hating lackey. It was meant to tell people that I have no standing as a black man, which is incredible given the work that I have done." But civil rights lawyer Keith Watters minimized the impact of Watkins' comments. "I don't see it as a grave insult. I don't endorse what he said and we may not like it, but there's an overriding principle of academic freedom." The Factor complained about the glaring double standard. "This was a racial slur and if he were white, I don't think he'd still be with Syracuse. And if I said that to Juan, there would be people marching outside demanding that I be fired."
Impact Segment 
Apologize to Filipinos
In a recent episode of "Desperate Housewives," one character tried to insult a physician by questioning whether he earned his degree from a "med school in the Philippines." Some Filipinos demanded and received an apology from ABC. "The line was completely unnecessary," professor Kevin Nedal told The Factor. "They're saying the educational system in the Philippines is inferior to the United States, and that's not true." The Factor accused Nedal and other protestors of being hyper-sensitive. "This is a dopey satire and this was a joke. If we can't overlook these kinds of things, nobody's going to be able to say anything."
Unresolved Problems Segment 
Death of Princess Diana
As a British government panel formally investigates Princess Diana's death, British journalist Sarah Baxter reported that many people view Diana as a legend. "She's entering into myth like Cleopatra and Helen of Troy, and people will be discussing her 500 years hence. She died young and beautiful." Fox News contributor Simon Marks added that people will always question whether Diana was murdered. "That tunnel in Paris is for many Britons what the 'grassy knoll' in Dallas is to many Americans. This will never be put to rest." The Factor urged British authorities to let Diana rest in peace. "There have been investigations and inquests, and why do we need another one? This is not healthy any more, it's almost ghoulish."
Personal Story Segment 
Internet Safety
The Factor welcomed reigning Miss America Lauren Nelson, who has made Internet safety her mission, partly because of her own experience at age 13. "I was on a sleepover with some girlfriends," Nelson explained. "A man approached us on line and we didn't know any better. About a week later he corresponded with one of my friends and sent inappropriate pictures of himself." Nelson described her "Miss America" software, which is intended to protect other children from similar experiences. "It's a browser for kids, and I pop up as an animated character and give Internet safety tips. Kid-friendly web sites are the only ones kids can get to." The Factor praised Nelson for her commitment, and offered a suggestion. "I think there should be an Internet safety class in school. Every day we're seeking kids molested and kidnapped and even murdered by people they meet on the Internet."
Weekdays with Bernie and Jane Segment 
Is the Elite Media Doomed?
Bernie Goldberg and Jane Hall debated whether network news and major newspapers are destined for the dustbin of history. The Factor began with this proposition: "Because of the controversies over me and Rush Limbaugh, millions of Americans have seen grossly unfair presentations by the elite media. The ratings at CNN have been catastrophic, MSNBC has ratings that a chimp could get, the New York Times stock price is at its lowest in ten years, and the network news programs have lost 40% of their audience. So I think it's almost over." Goldberg described the wounds as self-inflicted. "There used to be a diversity of opinion in newspapers, but when women and minorities came into journalism they pushed the newsroom to the left. There has been an eroding trust in journalism for a long time and they're near death." Hall quarreled with Goldberg's assertion. "It's hard to plot the ratings of CNN and blame women and minorities. There's the Internet, there's cable news, and the fact that shows like this one, which mix opinion and news, are appealing to people." The Factor reiterated that recent events have hastened the mainstream media's decline. "News watchers know when something is brutally unfair, and in the situations involving me and Limbaugh, people were able to see that it was wrong. It again showed that many in the media are not trustworthy."
The Dhue Point Segment 
Laurie Dhue
"Ombudswoman" Laurie Dhue relayed complaints to The Factor from some dissatisfied customers. One viewer objected because The Factor "gives Bill Maher a pass" and described Maher as "one of the worst hate mongers in the business." The Factor's reply: "As long as Maher is reasonable, I want him to put forth his point of view, because that's what makes the program interesting. When he goes over the line I tell him." Another viewer chastised The Factor for not asking Jesse Jackson "about his previous corporate shakedowns." The Factor explained that "every interview has a context, and the topic was race in America. I could have done what the viewer suggested, but that would have resulted in Jackson never appearing again." Viewers with grievances can contact Laurie Dhue at dhuepoint@foxnews.com.
Pinheads and Patriots 
Who's helping, and who's hurting?
Thursday's Patriots: Child actors Jerry Mathers and Tony Dow, whose "Leave it to Beaver" first appeared on television fifty years ago. And the Pinhead: Former Child star Danny Bonaduce, who threw Jon "Johnny Fairplay" Dalton to the floor during an awards show, knocking out some of the man's teeth.
