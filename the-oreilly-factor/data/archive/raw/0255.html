Talking Points Memo & Top Story 
Attacking Gov. Schwarzenegger
Guest: Edward Orendain, immigration attorney

"California Governor Arnold Schwarzenegger has called on the feds to secure the southern border, and has praised the Minutemen for bringing attention to the issue. That caused the head of the Mexican American Political Association Nativo Lopez to call the governor's remarks 'nothing short of base racism.' When some minorities don't like a policy, they cry racism. That's what's happening with Schwarzenegger and it is despicable. That kind of extremism is sometimes aided by the media. The Los Angeles Times demonized Schwarzenegger, editorializing that 'he has found a new group to rail against--as someone born and raised in the shadow of the Third Reich, he should also know better than to be fanning this anti-foreigner frenzy.' What anti-foreigner frenzy? All Schwarzenegger wants is for federal law to be enforced because illegal immigration is costing California $10 billion a year and the state is nearly bankrupt. Schwarzenegger is looking out for his constituents, and the governor is one of the few politicians in the country with the guts to address the issue honestly. What's happening to Arnold Schwarzenegger sends a clear message to other people in power - demand border security and you'll be branded anti-Hispanic. And few of our leaders will stand up to that kind of heat."

Transcript/Video: FoxNews.com

Immigration attorney Edward Orendain joined The Factor with another point of view on the border controversy. He excoriated the band of private citizens known as the Minutemen. "I don't think sending armed civilians to the border region is the way to control illegal immigration. The border is a very volatile area, and to have those people insert themselves in that region armed and untrained is adding fuel. Immigration reform should be humane and it should be done by the federal government." The Factor defended the Minutemen and Governor Schwarzenegger, who have been attacked as racists and bigots. "I am angry that Arnold Schwarzenegger is being demonized. All the Governor is asking is for the federal government to do its job. And what the Minutemen did is in the American tradition of protest. They behaved respectfully and didn't do anything wrong."
Impact Segment 
Another crime against children
Guest: Kara Spak, Chicago Daily Herald

The latest child abuse horror story comes from Chicago, where Tonya Vasilev allegedly killed her 3-year old daughter and 9-year old son Christian. The crime was reported by her husband Nikolai, who discovered the bodies and called 9-1-1 with this frantic message: "My wife stabbed both my son and my daughter. Send ambulances please." Chicago reporter Kara Spak has been investigating the crime and the family. "We talked to a lot of people," Spak told The Factor. "They all said she's a very loving mother, almost over-protective of her children. They belonged to a Bulgarian evangelist church and religion was a big part of their lives." Spak hinted that a past incident may have some bearing on this case. "In 2000 Tonya Vasilev put her baby daughter on top of the dryer, which was on at the time, because she said it soothed the baby. The laundry room caught on fire and the baby died. By all accounts she was extremely distraught."
Factor Follow Up Segment 
Disciplining the troops
Guest: Fox News military analyst Col. David Hunt

Private First Class Lynndie England has pleaded guilty to mistreating prisoners at the Abu Ghraib prison, and other soldiers are being disciplined for actions in Iraq, Afghanistan, and elsewhere. Fox News military analyst Col. David Hunt outlined how he would punish England. "I'd reduce her to private and throw her out with a dishonorable discharge. But I wouldn't put her in jail. The problem with Abu Ghraib is that no one above the rank of sergeant has stood trial. There are captains, majors, and colonels who are responsible and should be sitting there taking some of this heat." Col. Hunt also weighed in on another incident in which various female soldiers were photographed mud wrestling in Iraq. "It was disrespectful - you don't treat soldiers that way. It is pornographic, it's terrible leadership, and it's men abusing their power over their women. There is a chain of command that set the wrong climate in this unit."
Personal Story Segment 
13-year old Fla. girl gets abortion
Guest: Pro-life activist Genevieve Wood

As reported previously on The Factor, a 13-year old Florida girl became pregnant while under state supervision. She requested an abortion, and a judge has ruled that she has the right to terminate the pregnancy. Pro-life activist Genevieve Wood explained that, under state law, a young woman can have an abortion without parental notification or consent. "Florida voters have passed parental notification laws twice and the Florida Supreme Court has struck those laws down. The court says there is some right to privacy that turns into the right for a minor to have an abortion." Wood contended that allowing the girl to abort her child sends all the wrong messages. "This says to girls in the foster care system and elsewhere - you go out and act irresponsibly and the state is going to pay for you to have an abortion. I don't think that's a responsible message to tell any a child or anybody." The Factor categorized this episode as a total failure on the part of everyone involved. "This is a tragedy for everybody - for the fetus and for the 13-year old. And the state of Florida has failed."
Unresolved Problems Segment 
The handcuffed 5-year old: Who is at fault?
Guest: Attorney C.K. Hoffler

There is continuing fallout from the case of the 5-year old Florida girl who was put in handcuffs after police were called to her school. Attorney C.K. Hoffler, who is representing the girl and her mother, said the family has grounds for a lawsuit. "I would not have four armed adult police officers take a 5-year old kindergarten child, put her in handcuffs, and leave her in a police car with shackles on her feet for almost three hours. That is excessive use of force, that is inexcusable, and that is illegal." The Factor questioned whether legal action will accomplish anything beyond enriching the family. "I don't know what good it does to sue the police and the school - I don't think they had any malevolent intent. I predict this will never even go to trial, that you'll settle for money. And cynical people are going to say that's what this is all about - that you're just trying to shake down the cops and the school."
Back of Book Segment 
Students defying authority
Guest: Dr. Shari Lusskin, NYU Medical Center

Two high school students in Minnesota were suspended for wearing buttons inspired by the play "The Vagina Monologues." Psychiatrist Shari Lusskin contended that the students are being punished unfairly. "This is a tempest in a teapot," Lusskin told The Factor. "One-third of girls under the age of 16 have had sex. If you can't say the word 'vagina' how are you going to protect them?" The Factor argued that the suspensions are justified. "Doctor, I think you're out to lunch on this. I am stunned that a woman as level-headed as you thinks that high school kids can come into their schools with slogans on a button or a shirt that describe body parts and sexuality. To wear a tee-shirt that celebrates an intimate part of the body in a school is totally inappropriate."
