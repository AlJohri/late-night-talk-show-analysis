Talking Points Memo & Top Story 
Barack Obama and Sarah Palin
"John McCain surprised the world by selecting Alaska Governor Sarah Palin as his running mate. The governor is 44-years-old, the mother of five. She is pro-life, a member of the NRA, and has successfully challenged corruption in Alaska. But Governor Palin is not without problems - she is under investigation for trying to get her ex-brother-in-law fired from the Alaska State Police. Also, she has been governor for just two years and has scant experience. This story has stolen the spotlight from Barack Obama's speech, but his presentation is worth analyzing. The senator convinced me that his intentions are good, but he lost me in two important areas. Obama said John McCain 'won't even follow Osama Bin Laden to the cave where he lives.' What does that exactly mean, that Obama will invade Pakistan? Nobody knows. Obama lost me on another vital topic when he said we will end our 'dependence on oil from the Middle East.' But Obama didn't say how he's going to end that dependence other than throwing $150 billion into some kind of big alternative energy deal. On balance, Obama's speech appealed to the Democratic philosophy of big government protections and spending to cure problems. Republicans will scoff, but that's what the Democratic Party believes, and Obama's appeal to the core may be enough to get him elected. His speech certainly did not hurt him."

The Factor was joined by political strategist Karl Rove, who admitted his surprise at John McCain's VP pick. "This is John McCain at his most John McCain-ish," Rove said. "The maverick independent senator decides to discard all conventional wisdom and pick the maverick reformer from Alaska, a woman who turns out to be a gun-totin', hockey-playin', basketball-playin' woman." Rove praised the choice and Sarah Palin's credentials. "While she was running a state for two years Barack Obama was on the campaign trail running for president. The Republican convention will be energized by this." The Factor analyzed the political math behind the choice. "This is a pure demographic play. It seems to me that the McCain campaign learned that they need women voters to get elected, which is what Governor Palin's selection is all about. This makes it the most interesting election of my lifetime."

Campaign 2008 Segment 
Analysis of the Palin pick
The Factor turned to FNC's Juan Williams for his analysis of the McCain-Palin ticket. "It knocked my socks off," Williams said. "Most of us thought it would be Lieberman or Pawlenty or Romney, but she came out of the blue. She was the mayor of a small town not long ago, and the experience issue reminds you of Dan Quayle." Democratic strategist Jane Fleming Kleeb accused McCain of raw political calculation. "This shows a lack of judgment by McCain. He's trying to take the Hillary Clinton vote, but Clinton voters were voting for Hillary, not just for a woman, and in no way will they go to that side. It would have been smarter to pick Romney." The Factor argued that Sarah Palin deserves a fair hearing. "Give her a chance to make her case. I'm sure she'll come on The Factor, let's see what she's got."

Geraldine Ferraro, the first woman VP nominee, provided her take on the Palin selection. "Some Clinton supporters," Ferraro declared, "will never vote for Obama because they are so livid at how Hillary Clinton was treated by the sexist media. Maybe some of those people will go over to John McCain because of this. I have not made a commitment, I will go to the polls and do what I consider is best for my country. It is not enough to move me to vote for a ticket simply because there is a woman is on it." Pollster Scott Rasmussen, who mentioned Palin as a VP prospect weeks ago, gauged her value to the GOP. "She is the only candidate who can unite and excite the Republican base and at the same time reach out to disappointed Hillary Clinton supporters. This might give some people a reason to take a second look at McCain."
Unresolved Problems Segment 
Sarah Palin: Does Obama have a problem?
Continuing the analysis of Sarah Palin, political analyst Larry Sabato called her selection a calculated gamble. "I think this changes the race in big ways for McCain and the Republican Party," Sabato said. "They're seen as the 'white male party' in a country that is increasingly diverse, and now McCain has changed that. The conservative base has a heroine who takes all their positions and also reaches out to women and others who want to see diversity in the GOP. She is very frank and blunt, and anybody whose favorite dish is moose stew clearly is an unusual person." The Factor delineated some of the problems brought on by the pick: "McCain can't go running around claiming Senator Obama is inexperienced when he has Palin on his ticket. The only way she can overcome that rap is to convince people that she can go up against Ahmadinejad, Putin and the biggest savages in the world. That's not going to be easy."
Week in Review Segment 
Laura Ingraham: Obama's +8 bump
The Factor was joined by radio host Laura Ingraham, who dissected Barack Obama's Thursday night speech. "I know a lot of people were bowled over," Ingraham said, "but I didn't take away anything that was new and different. If you're going to fill a stadium, you had better leave people with a few lines that are going to transcend the current political debate. This was the same-old same-old with a better delivery." Turning to another subject, Ingraham commented on the reported infighting at MSNBC. "As someone who spent a lot of years at MSNBC, let me say that it's sad and disappointing. This is a left-wing network that is completely dishonest, and the people you really have to feel sorry for are Tom Brokaw and Brian Williams. " The Factor took exception to Ingraham's analysis: "I don't feel sorry for Williams and Brokaw, who lend their credibility to the network. I think they're part of the problem and if they had any courage, they would go in and tell Jeffrey Immelt that he has to stop this. I don't know how GE and Immelt can allow the NBC News brand to be destroyed forever."

Back of Book Segment 
Brit Hume on Denver
Finally, The Factor asked Fox News anchor Brit Hume to give his post-mortem of the Democratic gathering in Denver. "It was carefully orchestrated," Hume reported, "as all conventions are. While there was some feeling in the early going that Hillary Clinton didn't give it her all, her husband made up for that. And that final night was such a visual extravaganza that it's hard to imagine that it didn't have a positive effect for Obama. I think this was a solid B+ at least."
Pinheads and Patriots 
New Orleans natives & Jimmy Carter
Friday's Patriots: New Orleans natives James Carville, Donna Brazile and John Larroquette, who organized a fund-raiser to help people still suffering in their home town. And the Pinhead: Former President Jimmy Carter, who accused John McCain of "milking" his years in a North Vietnamese prisoner-of-war camp.
