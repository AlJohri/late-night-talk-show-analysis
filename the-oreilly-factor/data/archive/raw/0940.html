Talking Points Memo 
Politicizing the war on terror
"The anti-Bush crowd is furious with last week's terror arrests in London because it might help the Bush administration and the Republicans in the critical upcoming November election. The left is putting forth that the President is trying to scare people and that his policies have increased the dangers from the jihadists. It's impossible to say with any certainty that the Iraq conflict has made the war on terror worse. Surely things were bad when the US was mostly ignoring the terrorists. Our embassies were attacked in Africa, a warship bombed off the coast of Yemen, Muslim fascists in Afghanistan allowed Al Qaeda to train for 9-11. So it's hard to see that America's soft approach to terror was very effective. Talking Points believes Democrats have not yet put forth a plan to defeat the terrorists. Simply criticizing Bush is not a plan. DNC chief Howard Dean continues to be the chief bloviator, talking in generalities that don't really cut it any more. I want fresh ideas on how to defeat terror, and if a Democrat has a good idea I'll put it on this broadcast in a heartbeat. But so far it is just 'Bush is bad, Bush is trying to scare us.' You know what? Not having a plan after all this time is really the scary thing, is it not?"

Top Story 
Objections to
Guest: Nihad Awad, Council on American-Islamic Relations

The Council on American-Islamic Relations has criticized President Bush for using the term "Islamic fascists." CAIR's Nihad Awad elaborated on the organization's objections. "There is nothing Islamic about fascism. The terrorists are Muslims, but their actions are not inspired by Islamic philosophy. You don't call neo-Nazis 'Christian terrorists.' We have to enlist Muslims, here and worldwide, in this war on terrorism. And we should not alienate them." The Factor argued that accuracy should trump political correctness. "Your group does not want people like me and President Bush to use the term 'Islamic fascist.' But I submit to you that is exactly what these killers are. 100% of the terrorists we are fighting are Muslims who believe Allah is commanding them to kill the infidels, so you've got to label them what they are."
Impact Segment 
UN cease-fire begins in Mideast
Guest: Dan Gillerman Israeli Ambassador to the UN

As Israel and Hezbollah agreed to a cease-fire, Israel's UN Ambassador Dan Gillerman declared that his nation has accomplished its goals. "Hezbollah was dealt a very heavy blow. The whole international community demands their disarmament, Lebanon wants them out and will hopefully send soldiers to disarm them. Hezbollah has been degraded, but unfortunately they have not been eliminated." The Factor questioned whether Hezbollah has been seriously damaged. "I don't know how badly they've been degraded, because it's hard for us in the West to quantify that. I'm worried that this is just a regrouping for Hezbollah, and then they'll let you have it again."
Unresolved Problems Segment 
Airline security regulations
Guest: Amy Ziff, Travelocity.com

US airline passengers are not allowed to carry any drinks, liquids or gels on board a plane. Travel expert Amy Ziff told The Factor that things might seem bad, but they could be far worse. "We're still allowed to bring on our electronics - our iPods, computers, and cell phones. But in the UK that is not the case. Americans are still flying, and I will remind you that this is all for our own safety." The Factor contended that this is another blow for an already-reeling industry. "If I have to go to Boston I'll drive or take the train. This could be one more problem for the airline industry, and the companies are in bankruptcy anyway."
Factor Follow Up Segment 
Border Patrol agents to be sentenced
Guest: Attorney Barry Sorrells

Two Border Patrol agents, convicted of covering up the shooting of a Mexican drug dealer, face a sentence of up to 20 years. The Factor explained more about the incident. "This drug dealer had 750 pounds of pot, and the agents shot him in the butt when he was trying to run away. I'm not trying to justify what they did, but I don't think the government should be asking for twenty years. I can see giving them five to ten years." But according to former prosecutor Barry Sorrells, the agents deserve hard time. "These two agents broke the law and unjustifiably shot an unarmed man in the back. Then they didn't report the incident, and on top of that they tampered with the crime scene."
Personal Story Segment 
New poll on social issues
Guests: Republican strategist Karen Hanretty & Fox News analyst Kirsten Powers

Pew Research has released a poll showing that Americans are generally conservative when it comes to contentious social issues - 56% oppose gay marriage; 52% oppose gay adoption; and 66% want abortion restricted or banned. Fox News analyst Kirsten Powers predicted that attitudes toward gay marriage will eventually change. "When they poll the younger generation, they support gay marriage more often. The root of this is that most people have religious objections to gay marriage." Republican strategist Karen Hanretty asserted that Democrats may pay a price for supporting unfettered abortion. "The Democratic Party leadership is very much to the left of where mainstream America is on abortion. In the last Democratic convention, they would not allow one pro-life speaker." The Factor expressed surprise about American attitudes regarding-sex marriage. "Opposition to gay marriage stayed stagnant, despite overwhelming support from the media. With religion on the decline, you would expect a corresponding rise in the support for gay marriage, and it is not happening."
Back of Book Segment 
Cynthia McKinney under fire
Guest: Clarence Page, Chicago Tribune

After Congresswoman Cynthia McKinney was defeated in her Georgia Democratic primary, some of her bodyguards hurled anti-white and anti-Jewish insults at reporters. The Factor reported that the men were apparently affiliated with the radical New Black Panther organization. "To be fair, Cynthia McKinney said none of these guys were on the payroll. But I don't know why a congresswoman would allow these people around her - they were dressed in black suits and had patches saying belonged to the New Black Panther party. I believe she has anti-white tendencies." Columnist Clarence Page countered that McKinney is probably more concerned with politics than race. "She is someone for whom politics are a family business. They've run things down there in Georgia like an old-fashioned political machine. The New Black Panther Party is a militant organization and has been accused of anti-Semitic attitudes, but she has repudiated them on her web site."
