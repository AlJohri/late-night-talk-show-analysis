Talking Points Memo 
On the attack in Vermont
"Same song, different child molester. You may remember last year Vermont Judge Edward Cashman sentenced a man who sexually abused a six-year-old girl over a four year period to 60 days in jail. Cashman wanted rehab for the predator, but after our reporting, he had to up the sentence to three years—still far too lenient. The Vermont media supported Cashman and attacked me... here comes déjà vu. On Friday, we broke the story of Judge David Howard, who accepted a plea deal from 37-year-old Andrew James. James molested a 4-year-old boy in a terrible way. Judge Howard gave him probation and treatment. Today, the corrupt Vermont media sprung into action. The Times-Argus newspaper said, 'Another year, another national condemnation of Vermont by right-wing cable talk show host Bill O'Reilly... O'Reilly [...] called for a national boycott of Vermont.' That's a lie. I have not called for a boycott of Vermont, but I am calling for a boycott of the Times-Argus, a dishonest enterprise. The Bennington Banner, which did no original reporting on the James case while it was in motion, even though it happened in its backyard, said this: 'Everyone in the news business knows O'Reilly's reputation for starting, smearing and sensationalizing.' Then there's former Vermont Associated Press writer Chris Graff, who was fired from his job last year. He said this: 'What the leaders of Vermont are saying is that they think that Bill O'Reilly doesn't know what he's talking about.' If I don't know what I'm talking about, set me straight. Where exactly are we misreporting? The truth is that we have told the truth. Vermont is a perfect example of a state held hostage by a corrupt secular-progressive power structure that is bringing shame on what was once an outstanding place to live. You want to debate putting a 4-year-old on the stand? We're up for that debate. You want to put a violent, convicted felon child molester back on the street without a court fight? We're not for that. And that, ladies and gentlemen, is the disgrace of Vermont."
Top Story 
Prosecuting sexual assault cases
Guests Former federal prosecutor Georgia Goslee

The Factor described the motive behind the prosecutor in the Vermont case asking for probation rather than jail time for Andrew James: "Costello, the prosecuting attorney, said we listened to the boy's testimony, and we didn't think he was that credible. He's got a learning disability. We didn't want the guy to walk out with nothing, so we gave him probation and treatment." Former federal prosecutor Georgia Goslee disagreed with that logic: "Once that determination has been made, then there are several alternatives to the child actually testifying in person, in court. If that child's going to be traumatized, for example, you can have two-way circuit court television where the child can testify without being traumatized, without any people around, other than the actual attorneys who are asking the questions of the child. But other than that, that child is isolated. There would be no jury. There would be no judge. And the child apparently would not be as traumatized as if he's sitting there looking at the person who has allegedly committed these crimes against him." The Factor added that there were many people who could have testified: "You have the aunt; she was the original person the 4-year-old told I'm afraid of this guy, James. You have the brother, who is a couple years older than the victim, who says that the 4-year-old told me this guy was molesting him. Then you have social worker Kyle Hoover from Family Services in Vermont, who interviewed the little boy in front of police officers. They heard what the little boy said. And then they gave the little boy a drawing, an anatomically correct drawing, and said tell us on this drawing, point out what happened. And the kid did. This is in front of the social worker and the police." Goslee said, "I'm going to trial with that. I think if I had that kind of evidence, even though it may not be direct, there may be questions about hearsay, but I'm going to trial with that."
Factor Follow Up Segment 
Devlin kidnapping update
Guest: Greta Van Susteren

Michael Devlin has been charged with two counts, kidnapping and kidnapping with a deadly weapon, in the case of Shawn Hornbeck and Ben Ownby in Missouri. Greta Van Susteren commented on the oddity of the case: "It's so bizarre that someone shows up four and a half years later after having been kidnapped. And of course, the other child is in the apartment as well. You've got other missing kids in the state of Missouri. You've got Arlen Henderson, who vanished in 1991. So there are other instances where kids have disappeared. I'm with you that there is a possible another big headline coming on this." The Factor talked about the fate of Michael Devlin: "This guy's going away for life, which is why they threw the gun charge in, because they want to get him. He ought to just plead guilty and just take it like a man and just go away and you never see him again. So that's the good news. The good news in the case is that this terrible guy, he's going to get it." Van Susteren said it wasn't going to be that easy in the end: "But we're going to see, though, is we're going to see multiple jurisdictional prosecutions. There are at least two counties involved, possibly three. And maybe the feds involved. So this is going to be quite a long process."
Impact Segment 
Hillary returns from Iraq & Afghanistan
Guests: Attorney Mark Brzezinski

The Factor asked Mark Brzezinski, a former member of President Clinton's national security team, for his advice for Hillary: "Now what would you advise, if you were an advisor to Clinton? The Bush argument is that if we get out of Iraq prematurely, Iran is going to heighten its power, and al Qaeda's going to get all kinds of ancillary benefits from our withdrawal, which is going to impact on our national security. Are these valid concerns?" Brzezinski said: "Those are valid concerns. But the president's strategy isn't going to address them. And that's why I think Senator Clinton showed real leadership. Americans are anxious and worried and are lacking direction in terms of what is happening in Iraq. They're not getting that direction from the president." Next the Factor went after Clinton on Iran: "Hillary Clinton's going to have to explain to the American people if she wants to win the race, what she's going to do specifically about Iran. What would you tell her to say?" Brzezinski answered: "She said something today that was specifically on this issue. She urged the administration to engage in regional dialogue with Iraq's neighbor." But the Factor was instantly incredulous: "Regional dialogue? That's not going to do it. They're too dangerous. They're actively involved in killing Americans now. I don't mind talking to them, but if that's all she's got, regional dialogue, she's going to lose."
Unresolved Problems Segment 
Border Patrol agents sentenced
Guest: Congressman Tom Tancredo

The Factor returned to a case involving two border patrol agents. "Ignacio Ramos and Jose Compean, two Border Patrol agents, were convicted of shooting a Mexican drug runner in February 2005. A jury found they tampered with evidence in the case and violated the rights of the drug runner, who was an illegal alien. And that drug runner was granted immunity testify against the agents." Congressman Tom Tancredo said, "I want the president to pardon them. Today, they start their prison term. I wish, of course, they could have allowed them to stay out at least while the appeals process goes on. And the Department of Justice told the court, well, we think they should have been incarcerated when they were first found guilty. Where are the priorities in the Department of Justice and with the President of the United States?" The Factor said that, while the agents committed the crime, they still need to obey the law: "They did it. I'm not sympathetic to this drug runner, and 90 percent of Americans aren't sympathetic. He's causing trouble in the United States. He pretty much gets what he deserves. But, we are a nation of laws. These agents made a mistake. They tried to cover it up. They shot the guy in the butt when he was running a way. There are all kinds of things they shouldn't have done. A jury of regular folks found that to be true."
Miller Time Segment 
Dennis Miller joins the Factor
Guest: Comedian Dennis Miller

The Factor introduced a new segment: "In the 'Miller Time' segment tonight, comedian-actor Dennis Miller is an outspoken guy, a rare non-liberal in the entertainment industry. He's done many things in his career, including an Oscar-caliber performance in a film called 'Bordello of Blood' which moved me. Each week Mr. Miller will enter the No Spin Zone with his take on life, liberty and this pursuit of happiness thing." Miller explained his political philosophy: "I have always thought of myself as a pragmatist, and I began to see a degree of certitude on the left that I find unsettling. I don't like lock-step, even if it's lock-step about being open-minded. And after 9/11, I remember thinking we might have to get into some preemptive measures here, and that seemed to put me, I don't know, off to the kids' table." The Factor addressed the liberal mindset vis-à-vis Iraq: "There are people who want us to lose in Iraq. And they're the far left loons who put their ideology above the welfare of the country. But for those of us who want an aggressive war on terror, in the beginning this looked like an OK strategy, and now it's turned out to be a semi-disaster. How do you see it?" Miller responded, "I see it this way. I think of Iraq, at the very least, as like a demented salt lick in that part of the world, where these people come now, because they know there's a fight to be had here. And I like that part of it. As of today, it's been roughly 1,950 days since something has happened on terra firma here in the United States again. It's like that sign on the factory wall, the amount of days without an accident. I give that to Bush and his approach to the war on terror, and part of the approach is the thing in Iraq."
Back of Book Segment 
Girls charged with beating & taping attack
Guests: Teacher Brian Crosby

WThe Factor addressed a scary trend: "If you go on the Internet and dial up 'girl fight,' there are thousands of videos posted. It is revolting, but it's part of the American culture—young girls beating each other up and posting the violence on the Net. In Babylon, New York, two 14-year-olds and a 15-year-old girl have been charged with juvenile delinquency after attacking another 13-year-old girl after it was posted on YouTube.com. So what's going on in teen land these days?" Glendale, CA high school teacher Brian Crosby commented: "You really have to be on your toes these days as a teacher. I know that I just assume that anything I say, anything I do, could be videotaped and immediately posted on YouTube. So you really have to watch out. As far as the education experience goes, it definitely has hampered what you do in the classroom. For example, I have students do oral reports, and this happened to me just a few days ago, someone's cell phone went off. It totally ruined the whole atmosphere here for that poor girl's report." The Factor addressed the YouTube phenomenon: "When you see a video on YouTube like this video that we just showed with these girls—and you know, there's plenty of boy video, too—where they're doing this to get attention. Is this different than what you saw 18 years ago? A different kind of attention-getting mechanism?" Crosby said it wasn't the teens, it was the technology: "Teens are the same as they were 18 years ago, except they have this technology now. And from what I understand, some of them get bonus points from their friends if they're able to be caught and post it on the Internet. But if you grow up in an environment where parents won't draw the line and won't make boundaries, won't teach certain morals, then the kids are just following in the footsteps of their parents."
