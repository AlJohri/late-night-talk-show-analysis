Talking Points Memo & Top Story 
Taxes and morality
"Vice President Biden says he's troubled by not raising taxes on wealthy Americans, believing it might even be immoral. There's no question that some liberal folks are trying to take the high moral ground by pitting the rich against the poor. Congressman Jim McDermott used Christmas to justify the government demanding more money from affluent Americans. I replied in a newspaper column, saying that Judeo-Christian tradition does demand that we help the poor, but people must also help themselves. Enter our pal Stephen Colbert, who accused me of 'complete factual inaccuracy' and said Jesus preached that 'rich people should sell all their possessions and give the money to the poor.' I am not a theologian, but Jesus tells the story of a man who gave three of his servants money. Two of the servants went out and multiplied the cash, paying the man back with interest. The third servant buried the money, doing nothing with it. That man was chastised, with Jesus saying, 'cast ye the unprofitable servant into outer darkness.' If Mr. Colbert takes time to read that parable, he might understand the Judeo-Christian tenet of personal responsibility. Charity is a cornerstone of a good life, but Judeo-Christian tradition does not mandate blind largesse. Our government does not have a right to seize anyone's assets in pursuit of an impossible social nirvana, and I believe Jesus would agree."

The Factor was joined by conservative columnist Ann Coulter, who insisted that conservatives are far more charitable than their political rivals. "The fundamental problem," Coulter opined, "is that liberals think sending a check to the IRS constitutes charity. Liberals are the least charitable with their money, while conservatives - especially Christians - are the most charitable. Nancy Pelosi considers it charitable to give to the San Francisco ballet, whereas Christians are actually giving to poor people. Liberals want to harangue us and say we have to give money to the IRS because that shows we're good people, but that money gets spent on blasphemous art, subsidizing illegitimacy, and government bureaucrats doing immoral things." The Factor criticized folks who invoke morality to promote more taxation: "There are those on the left want to regulate how people live in this country, they want to raise the standard of living of the poor and bring the rich down. The tactic they're now using is guilt."
The Hume Zone Segment 
Brit Hume on Biden & taxation
Fox News political analyst Brit Hume evaluated Vice President Biden's implication that failing to raise taxes on the wealthy is immoral. "The idea that the rich are improperly advantaged in this country," Hume said, "is one that liberals and Democrats have been arguing for a long time. The data on this are interesting: The top 1% of earners in this country pull in about 20% of the nation's income. That's a lot, but they pay about 40% of the taxes. And the bottom 50% pays less than 3% of the nation's income taxes. So we have a pretty steeply graduated income tax code. I don't know to what level of taxation we would have to go to satisfy the moral sensibilities of President Obama and Vice President Biden."
Impact Segment 
The year's political winners and losers
According to a survey conducted by the National Journal, the biggest political losers of 2010 included President Obama, Nancy Pelosi, and Charles Rangel; among the winners were John Boehner, Sarah Palin, and Harry Reid. The Factor invited FNC's Mary Katharine Ham and Juan Williams to assess the survey. "I think Sarah Palin is a winner in terms of celebrity," Williams said, "but I'm not sure she's a winner in terms of politics. Even most Republicans now question whether she has the ability to be president." Ham questioned whether Nancy Pelosi should be included among the year's losers. "In her mind she's a liberal who has passed enough new regulations that they could honor her by covering the entire Golden Gate Bridge in red tape. And she is still the Democratic leader, even though she led them to historical losses, so from her point of view, I'm not sure she's a loser. I would add that one oversight for the losers list was teacher unions and public sector unions. There's been a real shift in public attitude."
Unresolved Problems Segment 
Hearings on radical Islam prompt criticism
Republican Congressman Peter King wants to hold hearings on radical Islam, an idea that is vehemently opposed by the Council on American-Islamic Relations. The Factor spoke with CAIR's Corey Saylor. "The concern is not so much with the hearings," Saylor began, "but with Representative King himself. He has said there are too many mosques in America and that the American Muslim community does not cooperate with law enforcement. Representative King is going to hold hearings that represent his view." The Factor outlined why hearings are warranted: "They're going to have these hearings on Muslim extremism because there have been 24 incidents in the last two years involving Muslims trying to do some kind of violence. That's troubling."
Weekdays with Bernie Segment 
Is President Obama the 'comeback kid?'
Many in the mainstream media are quoting Charles Krauthammer's description of President Obama as "the comeback kid." The Factor asked FNC's Bernie Goldberg why Krauthammer's column has been so widely referenced. "A lot of journalists," Goldberg replied, "are like birds on a wire - one journalist says something clever and then you have 500 journalists saying the same thing. The reason so many liberal journalists are repeating what Krauthammer said is because they can say, 'see, a conservative said he's the comeback kid.' But when Charles Krauthammer says something negative about Barack Obama, no matter how clever, that doesn't catch fire the way this expression did." The Factor added that many reporters remain deeply submerged in the proverbial tank: "The mainstream media has a lot of emotion invested in Barack Obama and they want him to be the 'comeback kid.'"
Back of Book Segment 
Reality Check: Barney Frank and your money
During a roundtable discussion on PBS, NPR reporter Nina Totenberg mentioned that she had attended a "forgive the expression, Christmas party." The Factor's Check: "Some conservatives believe the woman was denigrating Christmas, but I read it as a flippant remark, not overtly hostile to the holiday." Congressman Barney Frank, while advocating a steep estate tax, called inheritance a "pure gift." The Factor's Check: "In other words, if private property exists in your family, but it isn't really yours, it's Barney's. He owns it, he can take it away from you!"
