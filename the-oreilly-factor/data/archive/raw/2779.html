Talking Points Memo & Top Story 
President Obama and the smear merchants
"Most fair-minded Americans recognize that President Obama gave an excellent speech in Tucson last night. The President hit all the right points in defining the terrible atrocity that took place last Saturday. He then defined the current controversy, whereby far-left zealots blame the atrocious crimes on conservatives, and urged Americans to talk with each other 'in a way that heals, not in a way that wounds.' Nice thought, but unlikely to happen. Simply put, many liberals are furious that the progressive agenda is not working, and many conservatives are angry because they feel their country is being badly damaged by forces on the left. And so there is hatred in the air and it all bubbled up after the Arizona mayhem. President Obama's call for civility is reasonable, but some of his most ardent supporters started the brawl by accusing the right of pushing Jared Loughner to violence. Talking Points well understands there is vicious rhetoric on the right as well, and both sides should heed Mr. Obama's call. Robust debate is needed, but enough's enough with the hateful ideological loons."

The Factor asked FNC analyst Bernie Goldberg to evaluate President Obama's call for civility. "He's absolutely right about our need to lower the rhetoric," Goldberg began, "but I don't think he understands how difficult that is going to be. This polarization doesn't stem from political differences; it goes much deeper than that. As a conservative, I think liberals are wrong on many issues, but I also think they mean well. Liberal elites, on the other hand, don't think conservatives are simply wrong - they think conservatives are morally inferior, which is why it's so easy for liberals to say prominent conservatives were accessories to murder. These liberal elites not only think they're smarter than those 'hicks and hayseeds' between Manhattan and Malibu, they think they're better and more decent. The disgusting snobbery is in their DNA." The Factor pointed out that "there are right-wing people who viscerally hate the left and also believe those who disagree with them are inferior."
Week in Review Segment 
Laura Ingraham: Grading Obama's speech
Radio talk show host Laura Ingraham contended that columnists are spending far too much time critiquing President Obama's performance. "I'm really uncomfortable with grading the President's speech when we have six people dead," Ingraham said. "What I think is most relevant is what is happening to our country. We have had an attempt by many who are friends and boosters of the President to vilify and denigrate patriotic Americans and connect them to the acts of a deranged man. I found that appalling and outrageous, and I believe the President could have done a lot more from the beginning to push back against those people." Ingraham also criticized the pep rally-like atmosphere at the memorial service. "Solemnity was required, but there were t-shirts printed up and you had the president of the university introducing President Obama like it's the kickoff of the 2012 campaign. It was a campaign rally!"
Impact Segment 
Columnist: Don't investigate Muslims right now
Washington Post columnist Jeff Stein entered the No Spin Zone to contend that Congressman Peter King's planned hearings on Muslim extremism are unnecessary. "We all want to know what's going on," Stein asserted, "and that's why we have the FBI and Homeland Security. We have people checking visas and we have people doing interviews in the communities. So it's curious to me that someone like you who is opposed to big government would want a big government committee." The Factor explained why hearings could be valuable: "Fifteen years ago England was not a Muslim enclave, but now they have terrible problems with Islamic fundamentalism. The British people had no idea, so I want to know what's going on here, as long as it's fact-based. I don't believe there is a lot of Islamic fundamentalism here, but I want to know what all the information is. That is transparency."
Culture Warriors Segment 
Sarah Palin releases video in wake of shooting
Congresswoman Michele Bachmann and Governor Sarah Palin were singled out by left-wing bomb-throwers who implicitly accused them of inspiring violence. The Factor asked Culture Warriors Margaret Hoover and Gretchen Carlson how the women should respond. "It's unusual that Sarah Palin didn't take up your invitation to talk," Hoover said. "She put out a video and a statement, but she should come in here, which would allow her to take herself out of the debate." Carlson disagreed with that advice. "With Michele Bachmann and Sarah Palin, no matter what they say, everyone dissects every word, every syllable and every vowel. So I would advise them not to come on your show because everything would be dissected." The Factor expressed surprise that Bachmann and Palin have been incommunicado: "On Monday night I launched a devastating attack on the people who did this to them, and I assumed both ladies would come on and say how this affected them personally."
Kelly File Segment 
Did police have extensive records on Loughner?
Suspected Tucson shooter Jared Loughner had various encounters with police but was never arrested. Fox News anchor Megyn Kelly elaborated on Loughner's troubled past. "The people who had the most contact with him," Kelly reported, "were the Pima Community College campus police. One student felt threatened in class after Loughner read a poem about strapping bombs to babies, and a teacher felt threatened after Loughner challenged a grade. Time after time the campus police were called and he was finally suspended. Did the school have a legal obligation to do more than it did? No, but in a perfect world, the college would have referred him to a mental health facility." The Factor reminded Kelly that "everybody was scared to death of this guy."
Back of Book Segment 
Should Palin, Bachmann fight back?
The Factor asked former White House spokesperson Dana Perino whether Sarah Palin and Michele Bachmann should be defending themselves against attacks from the left. "Palin was darned if she did and darned if she didn't," Perino said. "Her statement was late and eight minutes for a video like that was too long and didn't come across as empathetic. If you have third parties defending you, you don't necessarily have to get down in the mud." The Factor argued that Palin and Bachmann should have come out swinging because "they were basically accused of being morally repugnant people who don't care if people are killed."
