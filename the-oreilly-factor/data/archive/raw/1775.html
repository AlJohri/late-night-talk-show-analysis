Talking Points Memo & Top Story 
NY Gov. Spitzer tied to prostitution ring
"New York Governor Eliot Spitzer, a Democrat, admitted violating his obligations as governor after federal investigators linked him to a prostitution ring. The destruction of an elected official should not be cause for celebration, although in our hyper partisan society it often is. The governor is married and the father of three daughters, and he apologized to his family and the public, admitting he 'failed to live up to the standard I expected of myself.' Again, there should be no celebrating the demise of Eliot Spitzer, but elected officials can not lead double lives. Spitzer was elected to enforce the law; he can't be violating it at the same time. All public officials should know that any kind of illegal action will eventually come to light. We live in a society where there are no secrets any more because there is no privacy any more. Governor Spitzer's career is over, and he might even be convicted of a crime. Another sad political story."

For more on the Spitzer story, The Factor welcomed two New York journalists. FNC analyst Ellis Henican reported that Spitzer made many enemies in the state capital. "He arrived in Albany," Henican said, "with an attitude of 'I know what's best' and 'I'm going to do it my way.' I think he's overly cocky, and he handed the club to his enemies that they are now beating him with." Steven Malanga explained why the case is being investigated by federal prosecutors. "He allegedly transported the call girl from New York to Washington, and this is also being investigated by public corruption prosecutors. They may be looking into whether there was any misappropriation of funds." The Factor marveled at Spitzer's recklessness. "He reportedly paid a high-price call girl $4,500 in cash. This is either arrogance or it's self-destructive behavior and he wanted to get caught. He's done, he can't be governor any more."

Unresolved Problems Segment 
Obama defends his middle name
During a radio interview, Iowa Congressman Steve King claimed Al Qaeda will be "dancing in the streets" if Barack Obama is elected president, partly because his middle name is Hussein. King entered the No Spin Zone to defend his assertion. "The message that Obama has delivered," King said, "is a message of defeat. He has announced that he'll immediately withdraw troops, and if he declares defeat our enemies will declare victory, and they will dance in the street. In the Middle East, they see Obama as having a bond with them, and his middle name is only part of the equation." The Factor criticized King for emphasizing Obama's middle name. "I don't have any problem with you criticizing Senator Obama's vision in the Middle East, but I don't think it's fair to bring in his middle name. Babies don't have a choice about what they're named, and maybe he'll have to be even tougher to overcome skepticism."

He Said/She Said Segment 
What's the downside of a woman president?
The Factor introduced a new segment that will regularly examine gender-based issues, in this case the question of a female president. "Men's advocate" and author Marc Rudov denounced any female politician who puts her gender first. "I have no problem," Rudov declared, "with a woman president as long as she's qualified. The problem I have is if a woman has a 'female agenda,' and Hillary Clinton has a 'female agenda.' She wants to be the 'feminist-in-chief,' and a lot of women are voting for her because she's a woman." Margaret Hoover agreed that any politician must represent everyone. "As we all know, there have been fabulous women chief executives. But Hillary Clinton does indulge in identity politics and uses it as her 'victimization' card, which is partly why I hope she is not elected."
Impact Segment 
Advertising San Fran as a sanctuary city
San Francisco Mayor Gavin Newsom won't cooperate with federal immigration authorities, and his city is now printing brochures telling illegal aliens they are welcome. Columnist Debra Saunders denounced Newsom for flouting the law. "It's not just that Gavin Newsom doesn't like our immigration laws, but we're telling people that if you don't like a law, you don't have to obey it, just break it." But Joseph Alioto Veronese, a member of the S.F. Police Commission, defended his mayor. "We're dealing with people in a compassionate way, to make sure that they're getting city services and they're not hiding." The Factor sarcastically offered to lend a hand. "We are going to help Mayor Newsom in a big way. We want everyone in the world to know that if you can sneak into the U.S. illegally, San Francisco is the place to go."

Campaign 2008 Segment 
"3 AM" commercial still getting publicity
The writers at Saturday Night Live are still getting comic mileage from the campaign commercials asking which candidate is better prepared for a middle-of-the-night crisis phone call. The Factor challenged two political observers to answer that very question: who do they want answering the phone at 3 AM? First, humorist Kinky Friedman dodged the question. "I'd say the human soul is unknowable. That is like asking who is going to have courage when the plane crashes, and it's never the guy you'd expect. This is one of those things you can not predict." But FNC's Juan Williams was willing to take a stand. "John McCain can be temperamental, and I don't know if you want someone out-of-control and screaming at 3 in the morning. And if I had a choice between Hillary Clinton and Barack Obama, she's been around the track a few more times and knows these world leaders."

Back of Book Segment 
Body language: Bret Favre / Rosie O'Donnell
Rosie O'Donnell is irate because Larry King seemed to ridicule her friend Fran Drescher during an interview on CNN. But body language expert Tonya Reiman viewed the interview and saw nothing offensive. "Fran Drescher seemed very passionate about what she was talking about, and Larry King was sarcastic in his usual way." Reiman then scrutinized John McCain as he was endorsed by President Bush. "McCain is always fidgeting, and every time he speaks he puts his hands in front of him. It looks like McCain is on the fence, wondering if this was really going well." Finally, Reiman watched quarterback Bret Favre, who broke down while announcing his retirement from football, and explained how hard it is to hide one's emotions. "Some people say if you straighten up your body and smile, you'll feel better. But your body and expressions will still leak information, and in this case he was saying goodbye to a 17-year career."
