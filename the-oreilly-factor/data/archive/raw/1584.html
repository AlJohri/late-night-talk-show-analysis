Talking Points Memo & Top Story 
Are you pro-immigration?
"New York Governor Eliot Spitzer has backed off his plan to give illegal aliens the unrestricted right to drive because the outcry over that rocked his office in Albany. We've asked Governor Spitzer to discuss the situation with us; so far he has declined. In support of Spitzer's plan to license illegals, the New York Times called him 'brave' and his opponents 'nasty.' The ultra-liberal newspaper says Spitzer's reversal offends 'a whole segment of pro-immigration New Yorkers.' Well, I'm pro-immigration. I believe America should offer opportunity to foreigners who obey the rules and want to work hard, but I want it done in an orderly, disciplined way. The most extreme elements in this country want open borders, blanket amnesty, and entitlements for foreign nationals who have come here illegally. They want to change the demographic in the USA so political power can be assumed by the left. There's no question that some who oppose illegal immigration do so because of racial animus, but most of us simply don't want chaos, which is what we have now. Coming to America illegally is a violation of law - it should not be rewarded with a driver's license or any other privilege or entitlement. And enough is enough with the race card! You're not anti-anything if you support the rule of law, you are responsible."

For more on immigration, The Factor welcomed Chicago Sun-Times columnist Esther Cepeda, who agreed that race does not belong in this debate. "We should take race out of this," Cepeda said, "because tons of the illegal immigrants who aren't even from Mexico, they're from all over the place. I don't like Governor Spitzer's plan, and I think a lot of people bristled at how he tried to do it." The Factor condemned those open-border activists who play the race card. "Most Americans just want an orderly process and they're not 'anti-immigration' or 'anti-Hispanic' for asking for that. I've had columnists call me a racist because I used the term 'illegal immigration' and because I want a barrier at the border. I'm tired of it and I'm not going to stand for it anymore."

Impact Segment 
Harrowing 911 calls
The Factor aired portions of two chilling 9-1-1 calls. One caller reported the fire that killed seven college students in North Carolina; in the other call, a frantic youngster screamed that her father was drunk and speeding at up to 100 miles an hour. That driver turned out to be Robert Montoya, had five previous DUI arrests in New Mexico. Radio host Pat Frisch explained that drunk driving is a massive problem in his state. "New Mexico is so far behind most other states in cutting down on the DUI problem. A case will be rescheduled three or four times, then the arresting police officer doesn't show up in court and the case is dismissed. And our judges have too much leeway to plead these cases." The Factor blamed New Mexico Governor Bill Richardson. "Governor Richardson has lost control over this whole process, and it looks to me like there's a culture of permissiveness when it comes to substance abuse. Richardson wants to be president, but he can't control the situation in his own state."

Culture War Segment 
"Exotic-Erotic Ball" takes over San Fran
Last weekend's annual "Exotic Erotic Ball" in San Francisco provided a window into that city's unconventional "values." Journalist Katie Horgan, who was in attendance, described some of what transpired. "I saw a lot that I didn't really want to see. There were people with body parts hanging out everywhere, it was a free-for-all. People buy the $100 ticket to go there because they can do anything they want. The experience was like I was in a porn, and was something I would never do again." The Factor pointed out that the ball was held on public property. "This was in the Cow Palace, which is owned by the State of California. In most places there would be rules of behavior, but you're telling me there weren't any rules."

Personal Story Segment 
Polygamist Warren Jeffs to be sentenced
After being convicted of assisting in the rape of a minor, polygamist cult leader Warren Jeffs is about to be sentenced. The Factor spoke with Carolyn Jessop, who married a polygamist at age 18 and bore eight of his children. "Abuse and violence in the community was as common as the sun coming up in the morning," Jessop asserted. "When my daughter was 14 and I became aware that Warren Jeffs was marrying girls of 14, I was terrified. I escaped in the night with my children, and it's a miracle that we made it out." The Factor praised Jessop for her perseverance and faith. "Your former husband, all told, had more than fifty children with five wives. How this can happen in America is stunning to most people."

"Is it Legal?" Segment 
Supreme Court on child pornography law
Megyn Kelly and Lis Wiehl analyzed various legal issues, beginning with the Supreme Court's decision to rule on the legality of an anti-child porn statute. "Congress passed a law," Kelly explained, "that criminalizes the possession of child porn, whether it's real or fake. It was challenged because of the 'fake' portion, but I don't think the Supreme Court is going to side with the pornographers." In another case, a Florida man wants a strip club charged with fraud and larceny after he ran up a credit card tab of $50,000 in a single night. Wiehl expressed zero sympathy for the big spender. "He's 24-years old, he was buying $2,000 a bottle champagne with his friends, and he was signing all these bills." But Kelly argued the other side. "I think this guy should sue the club for fleecing him. He was totally intoxicated, and this is what these strip clubs do."

Back of Book Segment 
The Great American Culture Quiz: Halloween edition
Steve Doocy and Martha MacCallum met again in the Great American Culture Quiz. The Factor posed five Halloween-related questions, including these: "Who was the object of Ichabod Crane's affection in 'The Legend of Sleepy Hollow?'" ... "Count Dracula was born in Transylvania, but moved to what country?" ... "Which actor was mean to Sissy Spacek in 'Carrie?'" In a truly frightful performance, both Doocy and McCallum missed all five questions, the first scoreless tie in Culture Quiz annals.
Pinheads and Patriots 
Who's helping, and who's hurting?
Tuesday's Patriots: Factor viewers, who made October's ratings higher in every category, with a larger audience at 8 PM than CNN, MSNBC, CNBC, and Headline News combined. And the Pinhead: Jeffrey Immelt, CEO of General Electric, whose NBC News programs registered disastrous ratings. "Mr. Immelt," The Factor pleaded, "please don't change a thing."
