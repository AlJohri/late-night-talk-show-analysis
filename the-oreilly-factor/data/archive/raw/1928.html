Talking Points Memo & Top Story 
Don Imus, race and the election
"Once again radio guy Don Imus has a race controversy on his hands, but this time there's a far different reaction to his comments. While discussing a football player who has constantly been in trouble with the law, Mr. Imus asked the player's race. When informed he was black, Imus said, 'Well, there you go. Now we know.' Imus says he was actually defending blacks who frequently get arrested 'for no reason.' No one can know for sure what Don Imus meant in his original quip. I tend to give most broadcasters the benefit of the doubt because I have been unfairly smeared by operations like Media Matters, which has been silent on this matter. The smear machine, Media Matters, which led the charge against Imus the first time around, not has nothing to say. Talking Points can't be sure, but is very suspicious. The only explanation that makes sense is that any national controversy about race doesn't help Barack Obama. Obama's strongest point is his message that he's looking out for all the folks and has absolutely no racial agenda. If another big racial controversy erupts in America, it focuses people on black-white issues. So I think Media Matters knows that and does not want to up the racial ante."

The Factor welcomed African American radio host Warren Ballentine, who accused Imus of fomenting racial controversy. "If you misstate something on the radio," Ballentine said, "you have the opportunity to go back later in the show and fix it. Imus could have done that when he made the comment, so I'm not buying this at all. Imus has a history of being racially insensitive, not only to African Americans, and that's why people are looking at this under a microscope." The Factor reiterated that "if there's tension on the national scene between blacks and whites, it doesn't help Obama."

Impact Segment 
Karl Rove on oil speculators and Congress
Former Bush advisor Karl Rove entered the No Spin Zone with his analysis of various issues, beginning with the Newsweek poll showing Barack Obama with a 15-point lead. "It's what we call an outlier," Rove said. "The other polls show he's up between one to seven points. Newsweek's poll has habitually been the outlier - it's tended to be more generous to Democrats than Republicans." Rove also repeated his accusation that Obama is "arrogant." "Yes, I do think Barack Obama is arrogant. He made a big deal about how he would take taxpayer dollars in the general election and abide by spending limits, but now he has reversed course. How can you claim to be different if you do the same old, same old?" Finally, Rove defended the much-maligned oil speculators. "OPEC wants everyone to be focused on the speculators because OPEC likes having the blame put on them, rather than on OPEC being a cartel that limits production." But The Factor wasn't about to let speculators off the hook: "They can do a lot of damage manipulating the market, and Congress has to provide some oversight on this."

Campaign 2008 Segment 
Analyzing political advertisements
The Factor invited marketing expert Laura Ries to analyze some new political ads, beginning with an anti-Obama spot that links the candidate with Bill Ayers, Rev. Jeremiah Wright, Father Michael Pfleger and others. "I don't think it's going to work against Obama," Ries predicted. "Guilt by association is not the way to go and these are tenuous connections. Obama has other things they should attack him with." Ries then watched a spot produced by Texas Senator John Cornyn's re-election campaign, which portrays him as "Big Bad John." "It's a wonderful ad," Ries proclaimed. "This was shown at the Republican convention in Houston, and this is perfect for the audience. Bigger is better in Texas."

Factor Follow Up Segment 
Jessica's Law update: Massachusetts
During a debate in Massachusetts about mandatory sentences for child abusers, Democratic state representative James Fagan, a trial attorney, said this: "That 6-year old is going to sit in front of me ... and I'm going to rip them apart. I'm going to make sure that the rest of their life is ruined." The Factor was joined by state representative Karyn Polito, who denounced her colleague's threat. "That was offensive to victims and their families," Polito said. "During that debate I advocated for a ten-year mandatory minimum for the rape of a child. That's not draconian and we ended up with a weaker substitute." The Factor praised Polito for her fight to enact stronger legislation. "Massachusetts is one of the eight states that simply will not put Jessica's Law into effect, and the state is breaking down criminally and socially."

'Is it Legal?' Segment 
Teen girls in Mass. deny pregnancy pact
FNC legal experts Lis Wiehl and Megyn Kelly began with the scandal in Massachusetts, where teenage girls reportedly made a pact to become pregnant. Because most of the girls are minors, Kelly reported that authorities may bring charges of statutory rape. "At least one of the girls," Kelly said, "reportedly got impregnated by a 24-year old homeless guy. Almost all of the girls were under sixteen, which is the age of consent, so all you have to prove is that sex took place." Wiehl brought up an extenuating circumstance. "Yes it's statutory rape. But if there was this 'pact,' then a defense attorney could argue that men were lured by these young girls, who were basically using them as a sperm bank. That would be a huge mitigating factor." The Factor laid part of the blame on school authorities. "I believe there's something in that school that's very wrong. The school is the last line of defense and it collapsed."

Back of Book Segment 
The Great American Culture Quiz
An all-female version of the Great American Culture Quiz pitted veteran Martha MacCallum against rookie Gretchen Carlson, co-host of Fox & Friends. The Factor asked five questions, including these: "What was Chandler's last name on "Friends?" ... "Who sang the title song in the James Bond movie 'The Spy Who Loved Me.'" ... "Which President pardoned the Japanese propagandist Tokyo Rose?" Newcomer Carlson eked out a surprising one-point victory, after which the former Miss America graciously called her upset victory "a fluke." If you want to test your own skill, the entire GACQ is posted here on BillOReilly.com under "Fun Stuff."
