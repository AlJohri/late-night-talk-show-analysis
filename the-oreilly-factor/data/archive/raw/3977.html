Top Story 
Donald Trump Joins the Factor
Watch more video in the Video Center...
The Factor began Monday's show by interrogating Donald Trump, who has been under fire for questioning Senator John McCain's war record. "Look at what McCain said," Trump explained. "We had 15,000 incredible Americans, we were talking about illegal immigration, and John McCain called them 'crazies.' That was a very insulting thing. Also, I see how badly our veterans are being treated and he's the guy who is supposed to be doing something about that." Trump insisted that he did not deny that McCain's is a genuine war hero. "It is very unfair how the media reported on this. I actually said four times that John McCain is a war hero. I have respect for Senator McCain, but I want him to do something about illegal immigration and I want to see him do a better job taking care of veterans." The Factor told Trump that he should have chosen his words more carefully: "You could have made your point, but you got into his war record and I think you'll admit that was a mistake. McCain has done good work for veterans."
Hume Zone 
Reaction to Trump
Fox News analyst Brit Hume evaluated Donald Trump's visit to the No Spin Zone. "He did fine and you did fine," Hume said. "He doesn't seem to buffalo you like he does other interviewers, maybe because he thinks you're a bigger buffalo than him. When he's criticized he always reacts on a personal level, and this idea of belittling McCain because he was captured has been around for a while." The Factor suggested that Trump is relishing this controversy, even the criticism: "I've known the man for a long time and I don't think he means half of what he says. He's a showman and he needs to get attention."
Factor Followup 
Kate's Law
Will Congress pass Kate's Law, which would punish criminal illegal aliens who return to America after being deported. The Factor explored the law's prospects with Republican Senator Chuck Grassley of Iowa, who has proposed a version of the law that sanctions illegal immigrations who have committed an "aggravated felony." "An aggravated felony could be anything from murder to rape and child pornography," Grassley explained. "An advocacy group came out today to say we should not change public policy based upon an isolated incident like the Kate Steinle murder, but in the last five years 121 people have been murdered by criminal aliens." The Factor predicted, "I think this law is going to pass the Senate, it's very well written and very specific."
Watters' World 
Living in America
Jesse Watters headed across town to the United Nations, where he asked some visitors to America what they think of the USA. A few of their replies: "The best thing is that people here can say anything they want" ... "The best thing is the transportation system" ... "The worst thing is the stinky people on the subway" ... "Perhaps America shows too much of its power" ... "It's so expensive here."
Talking Points Memo 
Justice, Mercy and President Obama
Watch more video in the Video Center...
"Last week President Obama told 46 convicted felons, some of whom are serving life, that they would soon be free. The president did this after telling the nation that the men were non-violent offenders whose sentences did not fit the crime. Apparently Mr. Obama believes the justice system is biased against minority individuals, especially in drug cases. I firmly believe that selling hard drugs is a violent crime. Those drugs have killed millions of Americans and enslaved tens of millions over the years, and anyone who sells poison is harming other human beings. Enter 42-year-old Marlon McNealy, sentenced to life in prison in 1993 for being involved in a major crack cocaine ring that terrorized the west coast of Florida for years. McNealy's grandfather started the drug cartel. He died in prison while also serving life. The crack operation took in millions of dollars a month and harmed thousands of people. Yet McNealy is being classified as a nonviolent offender by the president and will soon be released after serving 22 years. Again, the man sold hard drugs for money and the poison he pushed harmed and killed people. So should we feel compassion for Marlon McNealy? You make the call."

The Factor explored the McNealy pardon with Shannon Goessling of the Southeastern Legal Foundation. "I can't fathom why the president sees McNealy as non-violent," she asserted. "They brought crack cocaine to Southwest Florida in the mid-80s, they are the reason there is addiction and violence in the area. His grandfather went to prison for life, these people have destroyed entire communities! It's the opposite of justice, it is justice uprooted." But defense attorney Amy Marion argued that President Obama's assessment of McNealy is at least partially correct. "I agree that his was a non violent crime, he was not tried and convicted of that. We live in a democracy, we want individuals to be tried and charged. But I agree with you that McNealy is not the poster child we want for sentencing reform."
Back of the Book 
More on Donald Trump's Controversy
The Factor asked Charles Krauthammer to weigh in on Donald Trump's complaint that illegal aliens are often treated better than military veterans. "It's a dumb comparison," Dr. K declared. "If we had zero immigration in this country, would that make one whit of a difference about the mistreatment of our vets and the VA system? One has nothing to do with the other. This is simply a way to harness one issue to another as a way to inflame rather than illuminate. Donald Trump made a disgraceful statement and now he's pretending that this is all about his defense of veterans. He gets exactly what he wants - the attention and the oxygen." The Factor reminded Krauthammer that Trump remains very popular among many on the right: "In many conservative circles, the perception is that the government in Washington is pandering to illegal aliens while veterans are being ignored. And in politics, perception is often reality."
