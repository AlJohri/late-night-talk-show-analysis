Talking Points Memo & Top Story 
Bad behavior in politics
Guests: Kellyanne Conway, The Polling Company & Maj. Paul Hackett, attorney

"At a post-election White House reception, President Bush spotted Senator-elect James Webb of Virginia and reportedly asked Webb about his son, a Marine corporal serving in Iraq. 'How's your boy,' said Mr. Bush. 'I'd like to get them out of Iraq,' answered Mr. Webb. 'That's not what I asked you,' the president replied. 'How's your boy?' Webb shot back, 'that's between me and my boy, Mr. President.' That was rude on Webb's part, and inappropriate, especially at the White House. We've invited Senator-elect Webb on The Factor, but he has not accepted. But I will say this directly to him: it's fine to dissent from the Iraq war and to believe the president is wrong to wage it. However, in a meeting like that there should be respect for the office of the president. You made a mistake, Mr. Webb, in not being gracious. Predictably, Bush-hating columnist Paul Krugman applauded Webb's action. But clear-thinking Americans realize that disrespecting people with whom you disagree is now out of control. Over the weekend a caller to C-SPAN told Jimmy Carter he is 'vile ... a racist ... and an anti-Semite.' That caller was totally out of line. We're all living in very dangerous times, and attacking people personally gets us nowhere."

The Factor was joined by Maj. Paul Hackett, a former Marine who defended Webb's encounter with President Bush. "Webb's response didn't sound disrespectful. It's our White House - he's the president, he's not the king. He works for you and me and every other taxpayer in America." But pollster Kellyanne Conway decried Webb's discourtesy. "When you're invited to someone's home, you must be somewhat deferential or you don't go. This was a White House reception, a kind gesture to the incoming majority party." The Factor pointed out that Senator-elect Webb was not obligated to attend the reception. "Webb could have said nothing and walked away. If he had done this before the election, he would have lost. People do not want that kind of incivility toward their elected leaders."

Impact Segment 
Radicals run amok at Columbia
Guest: Cpl. Matt Sanchez, student

New York's Columbia University has been the site of numerous incidents in which radical students harassed people with whom they disagree. Matt Sanchez, a 36-year old student at Columbia and a Marine, told The Factor what happened when he was approached by a socialist group during a student event. "They said the military exploits all minorities. I said I joined and I'm a minority, and they said I'm too stupid to know I'm being exploited. I reported the incident, went through twenty meetings with the administration and faculty, and it went absolutely nowhere." The Factor denounced the cowardice of Columbia's administration. "What you have at Columbia is a group that's out of control. We know some Columbia alumni will not give money to the university because President Lee Bollinger fosters this atmosphere. We appreciate your service, Corporal, and appreciate your coming here."

Unresolved Problems Segment 
Foodborne illnesses spread
Guests: Fox News medical analysts Dr. Robert & Jennifer Ashton

There have been cases of e-coli contamination in New Jersey and Long Island, and hundreds of people were struck by a viral infection on a cruise ship. Fox News analysts Robert and Jennifer Ashton elaborated on the incidents. "It's our practices," Jennifer Ashton said in regard to the e-coli contamination. "People are eating out more and going away more. There is a fear factor, and yes, we can name 24 cases, but look at how many people are eating out . Are we going to live in a plastic bubble?" Jennifer's husband Robert expressed more concern about infections on the ships. "This type of virus is very resilient - it survives on the table, on food, on railings. If you put people in a close environment like that it's going to spread. These viruses are very difficult to stop." The Factor warned cruise ship patrons to be cautious. "In the cruise line this is happening four or five times a season, and there's nothing you can do to protect yourself except wash your hands. It's kind of scary."

Factor Follow Up Segment 
Wal-Mart under fire from unions
Guest: Neil Cavuto

The world's largest retailer has become the whipping boy of some on the left, who accuse Wal-Mart of exploiting its workers. Fox News host Neil Cavuto declared that Wal-Mart is unfairly vilified. "I disagree strongly that the company mistreats workers. The average Wal-Mart worker makes more than ten bucks an hour and among major retailers, it has the least turnover in its workforce, which is a great testament. The fact is that folks who are poor save thousands of dollars a year shopping at Wal-Mart. Wal-Mart is a great American success story."

Personal Story Segment 
Military fights back against San Fran
Guests: Fox News analysts Michelle Malkin & Kirsten Powers

San Francisco leaders have been consistently anti-military, and the military is apparently retaliating - the Navy has scuttled plans to commission its newest ship in the city. The Factor applauded the Navy for its decision. "It's about time. The military ought to say 'see you.' No commissioning, no Fleet Week, no money." FNC analysts Michelle Malkin and Kirsten Powers also expressed their views. "Amen," Malkin exulted. "This should be the first step in sending a strong message to San Francisco - if you are going to dishonor the military, then we will not grace your hate-filled city with our presence." Powers agreed that San Francisco is getting its just desserts. "I don't think San Francisco has anything to complain about. I don't agree with the 'hate-filled' part, but they are generally anti-military. So if they're not supportive of military issues, why would the military want to even be there?"

Related: Navy rejects San Francisco
Back of Book Segment 
Sex offender threatens website
Guest: Steve Roddel, FamilyWatchdog.us

Convicted sex offender William Thornton, angry at being listed on a web site that tracks predators, left a vitriolic voice message threatening violence. Steve Roddell of FamilyWatchdog.us explained how he handled the threat. "We called the local police and let law enforcement handle it. The last time I heard they were trying to make contact with Thornton." Roddell also declared that when it comes to providing information about predators, Minnesota is the least cooperative state. "Minnesota has over 14,000 registered sex offenders and they've put 104 of them on their public registry. The state has decided that the others are not a sufficient threat to make their information available to the public." The Factor lauded Roddell for his work, and warned him to steer clear of offenders like William Thornton. "This guy is obviously unhinged, and you have to watch guys like this. Track this guy and stay safe."
