Talking Points Memo & Top Story 
Insurrection mounts in Wisconsin
"Thousands of state workers are objecting to Wisconsin Governor Scott Walker's call for an immediate 8% cut in their benefits. Wisconsin has a $3.6 billion shortfall through 2013 and just can't afford to pay its bills. This is happening to many states and public workers are the first ones to take the hit. That's not going over well in Wisconsin - in the capital city of Madison 40% of the teachers called in sick today, and all over the state workers are demonstrating against benefit cuts. If state workers will not give back some of their benefits, there's no solution to the fiscal crisis anywhere - you can't raise taxes anymore because the folks are tapped out. The solution in bankrupt states is for workers to agree to some kind of giveback, perhaps over a few years; that way they can look for other jobs in the private sector if they don't believe they're being compensated fairly in the pubic arena. Talking Points believes that class warfare is about to break out in America. Union benefits are strangling not only state budgets, but also the private economy. Yes, workers need protection, but the cold truth is that federal and state workers have reached the top of their earning pyramid. Bankruptcy looms in California and other states, and givebacks are coming. The blowback to that will be nasty."

The Factor solicited views from two men on opposite sides of the Wisconsin controversy. "This is a modest pushback against the union's power to negotiate benefits and pensions," said Fox Business anchor Stuart Varney. "It asks union members to put a little more on the table for their own health and pension plans. I don't think it's an assault, it's a modest pushback on the excessive power of the unions." But Wisconsin talk radio host Sly Sylvester accused Governor Walker of union busting. "This is the latest step in Republicans trying to de-unionize Wisconsin. Public employees have been sacrificing, giving up wage increases year after year to keep their pensions and health benefits in place, and the people who are pushing this radical proposal are the same people who supported the trade policies that have shipped 100,000 manufacturing jobs out of this state. We need to shut this state down tomorrow!"
Impact Segment 
CBS correspondent sexually assaulted in Egypt
CBS News correspondent Lara Logan was assaulted in Cairo last week by scores of Egyptian men while she was covering the jubilation in Tahrir Square. The Factor asked Fox News correspondent Jennifer Griffin about the dangers faced by female reporters in the Middle East. "You have to take certain precautions," Griffin said, "and the first question you have to ask yourself is whether you are going to cover your hair. But I don't see how this will stop women from covering news in the Middle East." Former foreign correspondent Asra Nomani described her own harrowing experience in Afghanistan. "I was going to be escorted by some young men, but I looked in their eyes and thought that I was going to be either raped or killed, and I literally had to walk out of Afghanistan. We face a battle in our Muslim community in which an interpretation of Islam is being used to treat women as second class citizens." The Factor contended that Muslim/Arab nations have a particular problem: "I've been to these Middle Eastern countries and I know that women, in general, are not treated very well."
Factor Follow Up Segment 
The debate over Planned Parenthood funding
Many Republicans want to end federal taxpayer subsidies to Planned Parenthood; some on the left contend that women's health would be adversely affected. Conservative radio talk show host Laura Ingraham scrutinized the organization's finances. "Planned Parenthood gets about a third of its budget from U.S. taxpayers," Ingraham said, "and we know that Planned Parenthood makes most of its money from the abortion procedure - about 324,000 abortions a year. So American taxpayers see this country sliding into a fiscal abyss, and the idea that taxpayers should underwrite this is ludicrous. To be brutally honest, Planned Parenthood is overwhelmingly in business to do one thing, to perform abortions. I have one suggestion - if Planned Parenthood wants federal funding, just stop doing abortions."
Culture Warriors Segment 
School tries to ban dirty dance moves
Actress Janeane Garofalo, a radical leftist in her politics, has been earning her daily bread by playing FBI agents on TV. The Factor asked Culture Warriors Margaret Hoover and Cathy Areu whether Garofalo is being hypocritical. "It's not hypocritical," Hoover declared, "she's acting, which is pretending you're someone else. Some people would say this shows the extent of her professional abilities to credibly portray an FBI agent." The Warriors turned to the Minnesota high school that is discouraging its students from dancing in a sexually suggestive way. "This is much ado about nothing," Hoover groused. "Just because they're grinding doesn't mean they're having sex." But Areu applauded the school officials for clamping down on dirty dancing. "It's up to the school to keep things reasonable and appropriate, and it's completely inappropriate for them to endorse that kind of behavior. If it's happening on school property it's completely inappropriate."
Kelly File Segment 
Judge tosses out Beckham case
In Touch magazine paid a prostitute $50,000 and published her claims that soccer star David Beckham was a customer. Beckham sued, but his case was dismissed by a judge who claimed the magazine had not shown "malice." Fox News anchor and attorney Megyn Kelly concurred with the ruling. "It is absolutely correct," Kelly pronounced. "Beckham has no case against this magazine because you have to prove actual malice against a defendant who publishes something defamatory when you're a public figure. That means they had to know it was false or act with a 'reckless disregard' for whether it was false. And there was corroborating evidence that In Touch published." The Factor complained that the judge's decision invites more slander of public figures: "That's really frightening, because if you're paying somebody $50,000 to say something, they're going to say it."

Great American News Quiz
Martha MacCallum and Steve Doocy faced off in the Great American News Quiz. Among the questions: "Michelle Obama will not allow her daughters to have what?" ... "What young celebrity appeared with Dana Carvey in last weekend's 'Church Lady' skit on SNL?" ... "Who was on the cover of the best-selling 1989 Sports Illustrated swimsuit edition?" After trailing for most of the match, MacCallum nailed the final question to salvage a tie.
