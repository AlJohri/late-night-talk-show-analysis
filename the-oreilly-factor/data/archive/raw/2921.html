Talking Points Memo 
What the debt deal means to you
"The House has voted to approve the debt deal, and here's basically what it is: The government will define at least $2.1 trillion in spending cuts and the feds will also raise the debt ceiling by $2.1 trillion. This is not nearly enough to bring expenditures under control, but since government is divided between big-spending liberals and small government conservatives, the deal at least lessens the chaos for now. The most important thing is that it challenges you, the American voter. If you want less spending in the future you'll vote for Republicans; if you want to continue with the big government philosophy you'll vote Democrat. The truth is, we need more revenue and the far right should understand that. We desperately need a flat tax that would maximize revenue and simplify the tax code so people would pay a fair amount and unfair loopholes would be closed. We also need a small national sales tax to tap into the underground economy. A sales tax would raise an enormous amount of cash, which could then be used to fund Medicare and other social programs, most of which need to be reformed. In the face of all the madness, it is important to understand the mindset of President Obama and the political left. Even though the country is in dire economic trouble, largely because of liberal policies, they simply will not acknowledge that. The far-left is accusing the Tea Party and others of extortion, which was articulated by CNN's Fareed Zakaria, who accused it of threatening to 'blow up the country.' The bottom line is this: Massive pressure had to be put on the Obama administration and the Democrats in order to get anything done on spending cuts. You can call it extortion, but the truth is that we need far more discipline in federal spending and total reform of the tax code."
Top Story 
Compromise reached on debt ceiling?
The Factor asked Republican presidential hopeful Newt Gingrich to evaluate the debt compromise. "The Tea Party members should feel really good," Gingrich said. "They were effective and we just had an extraordinary moment when a very left-wing President blinked. That would not have happened without the Tea Party. Second, Washington has to shift and focus on the economy. We are in very grave danger of sliding into an even deeper depression and they had better put a lot of attention into repealing Dodd-Frank and fixing the bureaucracies." Gingrich portrayed President Obama as a misguided ideologue. "He actually believes in left-wing economic ideas, but the problem is that they don't work. I think he cares, but all the ideas he has and all the advisors he has are wrong." The Factor contended that President Obama is well aware of his current dilemma: "I'm sure the President is doing internal polling that shows his reelection is in jeopardy if the economy doesn't improve."
Impact Segment 
Latest approval ratings for Pres. Obama show dip
How will the debt battle affect President Obama's job approval rating, which is now in the mid-40's? The Factor posed that question to political scientist Larry Sabato. "This has been bad for the President," Sabato replied. "Independent and swing voters saw a President who was disengaged and who appeared irrelevant for much of the last week. The Republicans control one-third of the national government but they got two-thirds of this deal, so it looked like President Obama caved." Pollster Scott Rasmussen dissected the President's numbers. "He still has strong support among the Democratic base," Rasmussen said. "They're less enthusiastic than they were before, but that's what's propping up his numbers. We also see that he's lost ground in the past few weeks against a generic Republican opponent and this debt ceiling debate made him look weak. But this downward blip will disappear if the economy improves."
Unresolved Problems Segment 
Twists and turns in the debt compromise
Fox News correspondents James Rosen and Carl Cameron entered the No Spin Zone with the latest on the last-minute debt ceiling drama. "You can count on one thing in Congress," Cameron stated, "and that is that they will expand their work to the available time and bring it right up to the deadline. The Senate will pass this tomorrow, President Obama will sign it quickly, and then it will take a day or two to find out what the surprises are." Rosen explained which House members opposed the compromise bill that passed Monday. "The people who voted against this were predominantly on the two wings. There were those on the far left who were upset that it didn't include tax hikes on the wealthy, and those on the far right who feel it didn't go far enough in spending cuts. There were also some moderate Republicans who voted against it because they felt the Department of Defense will bear an undue burden."
Weekdays with Bernie Segment 
The right vs. the left in the debt debate
The Factor asked Bernie Goldberg to opine on the left-wing pundits who are blithely comparing Tea Party Congressmen to terrorists. "There is one thing funny about this," Goldberg pointed out, "which is that these are people who don't call real terrorists 'terrorists.' Beyond that, I see two story lines. One is liberal commentators are saying Barack Obama is a weak president who is letting Tea Party Republicans push him around. The second story line is that a journalist could portray the Tea Party people as principled patriots who care about their country or as dangerous, scary, stupid whackos. A lot of the liberals have portrayed them as the latter, and to compare them with suicide bombers is just wrong." The Factor pointed to another irony in the left-wingers' accusations: "What is amusing is that some of these people are so radical in their thinking, but they're calling the Tea Party crazy people. The Tea Partiers don't want more spending and these guys want trillions more spending. Who's crazier?"
Back of Book Segment 
Juan & Mary Katharine on the debt debate
The Factor invited Juan Williams and Mary Katharine Ham to weigh in on the debt deal. "I just saw a poll," Williams said, "showing that most Americans say what's been going on in Washington is disgusting. When you talk about President Obama's poll numbers, you should also mention that Republican numbers are even lower. We're going to have an election in 2012 where people will argue about who's protecting Medicare, and I think Democrats are well positioned." But Ham portrayed Democrats as spending addicts in constant need of a fix. "This has illuminated the remarkable ability of Democrats to deny that there's an actual issue with the amount of money that we're borrowing. I think President Obama genuinely believes liberal policies are going to fix the country even though the evidence says no. He's a liberal college professor and he believes this." The Factor concluded with a sobering stat, pointing out that "under President Obama, every day the United States owes another $4.1 billion."
