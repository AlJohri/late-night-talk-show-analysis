Talking Points Memo 
Americans and the airlines
"Critical mass has been reached. The country's airline system melted down in the face of bad weather on the east coast Wednesday. Thousands of passengers were held hostage on runways for up to ten hours. There's no excuse for this kind of incompetence by the airline companies. The performance of the airlines has dropped dramatically - they've cut employees and salaries, cut services, and even cut civility. When they go bankrupt, we taxpayers pick up the tab as the airlines skip out on debts and reorganize to come back even worse than before. It's not the fault of the individual airline worker, who is often under siege. It is management that is abusing people, and the government must step in and force standards of decent behavior. It should be against the law to keep anyone on a non-flying plane for more than two hours. Enough's enough! The system's broken and if the federal government will not demand performance, then we the people must by suing the airline companies until they can't see. No more airline abuse!"
Top Story 
Who gets Anna Nicole Smith's body?
Guests: Fox News correspondent Steve Harrigan & attorney Michelle Suskauer

There is now a feud over Anna Nicole Smith's body and where she will be laid to rest. On Thursday Judge Larry Seidlin declared that her body "belongs to this judge" and will remain in Florida until he orders otherwise. Attorney Michelle Suskauer, who was in the courtroom, described the scene as chaotic. "It was basically a free-for-all. This judge really wanted to decide the paternity issue, even though this was supposed to be about who would get control of Anna Nicole's body for burial." FNC correspondent Steve Harrigan reported that this is now a major scandal in the Bahamas. "There are front page pictures showing Anna Nicole with the immigration minister. They have their clothes on, but they certainly look intimate. There are even criminal accusations that she gave him a $25,000 Rolex watch so she could get residency."
Impact Segment 
Injustice in Mass. child rape case
Guest: Attorney Ed Ryan

A 9-year old girl Massachusetts was brutally raped while her mother and a man named Patrick Doyle stood by and watched. The rapist received 15 years, but Doyle, who could have stopped the crime, was sentenced to just one year by Judge Leila Kern. Judge Kern would not comment, but attorney Ed Ryan defended the sentence. "Doyle is a crack addict with no record, he was in bed with two women when the rapist came in. This guy was not in any shape to save anyone, and he is not a sexual offender." The Factor angrily argued that Doyle, crack-addled or not, should have received a much harsher sentence. "Does a human being who stands there and watches a 9-year old girl get raped deserve one year? I would have given the guy ten years!"
Personal Story Segment 
Mitt Romney on the White House race
Guests: Former Governor of Massachusetts Mitt Romney

GOP presidential hopeful Mitt Romney entered the No Spin Zone and laid out his position on the war in Iraq and President Bush's troop "surge." "As long as there's a pathway to a successful resolution," Romney said, "that's a pathway we have to pursue. If that's going to require an extra 20- or 25-thousand personnel, my view is that is something we ought to try. Simply walking away could lead to terrible outcomes that could require more troops and lead to more casualties." The Factor posed a question about Iraq's neighbor to the east: "Would you go to war if the Iranians said we're going to develop nuclear weapons?" Romney made it clear that Iran can not go nuclear. "No one who is running for president should ever take the military option off the table. Having Iran, which has countenanced genocide, with a nuclear weapon is unacceptable."
Unresolved Problems Segment 
Airlines on the verge of collapse?
Guest: Fox News chief judicial analyst Judge Andrew Napolitano

As detailed in the Talking Points Memo, passengers on some JetBlue flights were stranded on the tarmac at New York's JFK airport for up to ten hours. Fox News analyst Andrew Napolitano laid out the legal rights of airline passengers. "You do not have the right to leave the plane. Since the Patriot Act was enacted, it's a federal crime to leave the plane. But you have the right to sue the airline for the misery they inflicted on you. The airline is responsible for your comfort and safety, and they would be hard-pressed to persuade a jury that ten hours of misery and horror is reasonable." The Factor commended Senator Barbara Boxer for introducing a bill that will require airlines to let passengers off any non-flying plane after three hours.
Policing the Press Segment 
Big media in trouble?
Guests: Fox News analysts Jane Hall & Bernard Goldberg

NBC News, which employs anti-military analyst William Arkin, was overtaken by ABC in the ratings last week. The Factor reported that NBC has refused to turn over a full list of its contributors. "We asked the networks to provide us with a list of paid analysts - NBC and CBS would not, but ABC and CNN did provide us with lists." FNC analyst Bernard Goldberg reacted to NBC's refusal. "News organizations expect transparency from everybody else. They look down the throat of business people, politicians, church people and entertainers. But then you ask them who they pay for their 'expert' opinions, and they say it's a secret." But Fox News analyst Jane Hall accused The Factor of cherry-picking quotes from NBC. "You take things and extrapolate what you think is true, and you've decided the whole network is to the left. You don't put on the things that are fair to Bush and very objective." The Factor reminded Hall that NBC is the network that hired William Arkin. "This all started with Arkin, who said U.S. soldiers are 'mercenaries.' We introduced the story and then NBC didn't disassociate itself from this guy. And there are other far-left loons on NBC's payroll." Moving to another media story, Goldberg contemptuously rationalized the media attention given to Anna Nicole Smith. "First, we're sick of watching people getting blown up in Iraq. Second, as a culture, we're fascinated by crap. And the third reason, the most important reason, is that she has big breasts."
Back of Book Segment 
NBA star Tim Hardaway bashes gays
Guest: Brian Levin, Center for the Study of Hate and Extremism

When asked how he would feel about having a homosexual teammate, former NBA star Tim Hardway minced no words. "I hate gay people," Hardaway said, "I'm homophobic." Brian Levin, who studies extremism, tried to explain the causes of bias. "Haters are made, not born. If people are conditioned in a subculture of prejudice, they will respond accordingly. We all have prejudices, but the hardened bigot is locked in a belief system that is rigid and inflexible." The Factor lamented the apparent prevalence of intense bias. "There are a lot of people who irrationally hate entire groups of people. Tim Hardaway is a big guy and nobody is going to bother him. I can only say to him, 'why do you care?' I guess it's some kind of a disorder."
