Election 2016 
Political Sniping
Watch more video in the Video Center...
Republican front-runners Donald Trump and Ben Carson have engaged in a war of words about their faith and professional abilities. Eric spoke about the verbal jousting with Democratic strategist David Goodfriend. "I can't sit here and gloat," Goodfriend began, "because there is a lot of uncertainty and changes on our side as well. On the Republican side Donald Trump has emerged as the front-runner and it's hard to see who is strong enough to take him down. Every time he engages in a full-on fight with another Republican, his poll numbers go up, so it's hard for me to imagine anyone taking him down." Goodfriend also defended his own front-runner, saying, "Those of us who have seen Hillary Clinton in a small setting see a warm, fun-loving, interested, and interesting person." Republican strategist Mercedes Schlapp attempted to frame the sniping as good for her party. "We should get the dirty laundry out now, this is what we need to do. People want to know the character of the candidates, and Donald Trump is a brash personality with rhetoric that is connecting with voters who are angry with what's going on in Washington. Republicans are talking from the gut, they're trying to connect with the voters emotionally."
Impact Segment 
Trouble Abroad
Eric turned to the Middle East, which is more chaotic than ever with refugees fleeing and terrorism growing. Liberal analyst Simon Rosenberg and conservative Rick Grenell examined the overwhelming problems. "We have a responsibility from a humanitarian standpoint," Grenell said, "to make sure that the refugees who have been created by this Syrian crisis are taken care of. We can do that by not bringing them to the United States, we could have safe zones in Turkey or Syria. Our duty is to feed them and give them temporary relief, our duty is not to give them permanent residence in the United States." But Rosenberg contended that the USA can easily absorb at least 10,000 refugees. "Everyone must go through detailed screening, and I think that of the millions of refugees we can find 10,000 and get them through the system." Eric warned that we could be unwittingly admitting potential terrorists, saying, "The people coming off the boats and trains are almost exclusively young male Muslims."
Factor Followup 
New Troubles for Hillary
Watch more video in the Video Center...
Eric welcomed Fox News correspondent Ed Henry, who has been reporting from the Hillary Clinton campaign that continues to lose ground among Democratic voters. "Late this week Hillary Clinton had a couple of events in Ohio and Wisconsin," he reported. "I've been to Columbus many times and President Obama always gets thousands of people, many of them college students who are excited about Democratic candidates. Hillary Clinton had only a couple of hundred of people - she is trying to show strength, but this was a pretty sparse crowd. When I posted some pictures on Twitter, the campaign was not happy. She is the front-runner, she should be getting a lot more people right now, and her poll numbers are dropping."
Unresolved Problems 
A Look at the Polls
Veteran political observers Larry Sabato and Caitlin Huey-Burns examined the latest polls on both sides of the presidential race. "The headlines for me," Sabato said, "are that Hillary is dropping like a stone and Trump is maintaining altitude. And Hillary Clinton's biggest problem may not be on the plate yet, which would be Joe Biden's entry into the race. If he gets in, establishment Democrats have a real alternative." Huey-Burns turned to Donald Trump and his continuing domination of the GOP race. "He's actually doing well with women, his numbers have improved. We have tried to predict where his support will go if it falls, but it really hasn't. Meanwhile, you have two candidates waiting in the wings - Ben Carson has been creeping up and Ted Cruz is hoping to inherit a lot of support in Iowa."
Election 2016 
Going Toe to Toe with Trump
Watch more video in the Video Center...
Eric spoke with Republican candidates Bobby Jindal and Carly Fiorina, both of whom have engaged in verbal fisticuffs with Donald Trump. "America is slipping away from us," Jindal complained. "We have $18-trillion in debt, Planned Parenthood selling baby parts, and a president who is allowing Iran to become a nuclear power. The left is running the country into the world and we have a once-in-a-lifetime opportunity to get our country back. So do we turn to our conservative principles or do we turn to a man who believes in nothing but himself? Donald Trump is not a liberal, a conservative, a Democrat, or a Republican - he has no substance! He's entertaining, but there is a lot of foolishness and nonsense there. America must do better." Fiorina, whose appearance was ridiculed by Trump this week, handled the apparent insult with humor. "When I was in 3rd grade," she quipped, "there was a little boy who said mean things about me but he really liked me. So I think I know when somebody's flirting with me." Fiorina also looked ahead to next weekend's second Republican debate. "There are many fine candidates on that stage, and my first objective is to introduce myself to the American people. I want people to know what I have done, that I have a track record of leadership and problem-solving and challenging the status quo. This isn't entertainment, this is very serious because our nation is at a pivotal point."
Personal Story 
Emotional Interview with Joe Biden
Vice President Joe Biden was overcome with emotion when talking about his late son Beau during an interview on CBS. Eric spoke about Biden's prospective candidacy with political reporter Robert Costa and professor Jeanne Zaino. "This was a powerful moment," Costa said, "and you could see the vice president struggling publicly with this decision. He's trying to see if there is a lane in this Democratic primary for him while also thinking about his family considerations." Zaino contended that Joe Biden's personal qualities vastly outshine those of Hillary Clinton. "If you compare his performance on Colbert with her performance on Ellen and you look at questions of trustworthiness and authenticity, you can see that he really does have the upper hand. That doesn't mean he'll get into the race and knock her out, but I do think there is a path forward for the vice president. Hillary Clinton's poll numbers are going in the wrong direction, but it is still an uphill battle for Joe Biden if he decides to get in."
Back of the Book 
The Evangelical Vote
Why is thrice-married tycoon Donald Trump garnering wide support from the religious right? Eric posed that question to Pastor Robert Jeffress. "I'm not endorsing Donald Trump," Jeffress testified, "but I'll explain why he is so popular among evangelicals. There is a feeling among many that this nation has been in a downward spiral for the last seven years and they are willing to think outside the box for the leader they want to choose. The same-sex marriage decision by the Supreme Court was a watershed issue for evangelicals, causing them to think they've lost the culture war at the government level. They're now thinking that the government should solve practical problems and let the church take responsibility for spiritual problems. The question now is, when some of these candidates inevitably drop out, will religious voters coalesce around other evangelical candidates or will they move toward Donald Trump in frustration? I say this as reverently as possible - only God knows what will happen."
