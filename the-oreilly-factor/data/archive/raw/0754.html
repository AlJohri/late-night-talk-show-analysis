Talking Points Memo & Top Story 
Immigration debate... out of control
Guests: Fox News analysts Michelle Malkin & Linda Chavez

"A coalition of sympathetic media and open border activists has counter attacked effectively, as Congress tries to hammer out a bill that would get illegal immigration under control. What's happening in the debate is deplorable. Number one, if you want to secure the border and hold illegal aliens accountable for breaking immigration law, you're inhumane. Americans seeking strict enforcement of immigration laws are bad people, and even worse, conservatives. But the open border crowd is humane and holistic. Just read the New York Times and other left wing outlets. 'Talking Points' is fed up with the bull. So here's the truth. The Kennedy-McCain bill would not secure the border or stop rampant illegal entry. The bill provides for more border patrol agents, but little else on the security side. The Frist bill is a bit tougher, but not much. So at this point, the Senate simply doesn't want to secure the border, period. And the press in general applauds. For example, writing in The Denver Post, our pal Cindy Rodriguez says, 'When I think of all that undocumented workers do for us, I don't feel fear. I feel gratitude.' And don't you dare disagree with Ms. Rodriguez. If you do, you're a bigot. She writes, 'Hate groups like the Aryan Nation spew vitriol about the "illegal invasion" in e-mail blasts. Bill O'Reilly and Lou Dobbs drone on about `illegals' every night. These distinct groups use the same language. It's an orchestrated effort designed to instill fear in Americans.' So now, Mr. Dobbs and I are simpatico with the Nazis, according to Miss Rodriguez. It is long past time for Americans to reject that kind of tactic and demand their government enforce current immigration laws or make new ones, but don't make the mistake of directing your ire toward individuals. Illegal aliens are basically honest hard-working people, but again, they have no right to be here."

Fox News Video: FoxNews.com

Millions of people nationwide rallied in our city streets over the issue of illegal immigration. The Factor asked Fox News Analyst Linda Chavez if a Guest Worker Program would solve the problem, "Many of the 6.5 million people who have been here more than five years actually already have their families here ? if we create a guest worker program, a lot of the Mexican and Central American men would like to come here, leave their families back at home if they could go home to visit them, if they could go home during holidays. They don't have a way of doing that now. If we give them a green card, and the ability to go home, many of them would in fact do so." Syndicated Columnist, Michelle Malkin, disagreed and fired back, "Our house is a mess. If you look at the Department of Homeland Security from top to bottom, and you want to talk about comprehensive immigration reform, let's talk about reforming that bureaucracy first. The deportation system is a mess. The detention system is a mess ? you cannot have any kind of new guest worker program until we see that the borders can be enforced, until interior enforcement takes place, until information sharing is actually happening between the FBI, the Department of Homeland Security, the CIA." The Factor points out the legislation in the Senate fails to address this fundamental issue, "The Senate bill does not address border security in any meaningful way. Neither the McCain-Kennedy, nor the Frist versions. They add 2,500 border patrol a year and that's it."

Top Story II 
The so-called "open border policy"
Guest: Gabriel Camacho, American Friends Service Committee

Does everybody have a right to come to the United States? Or does the United States have the right to determine who can cross its borders. The Factor asked Gabriel Camacho, a member of the Boston American Friends Service Committee, "The idea of America is for people to come here, start anew, and make a living and create something ? And when we ask questions about immigration, we have to ask why do people leave their families, leave their homes, leave their communities, and make a perilous journey to the north, and start anew? That's a fundamental question that a lot of." The Factor challenged Comancho and said, "But poor people the world over do not have a right, a fundamental human right, to come to any country and make a living. They don't have that. Yet the open border crew believe they do." The Factor went even further, stating, "Mexico has never been able to support its population, despite having an enormous amount of resources and a big tourism industry. It is an out of control country. We have no responsibility to take Mexicans who cannot earn a living because their government is inept."

Impact Segment 
French labor law is dead
Guest: Michael O'Hanlon, Brookings Institution

French President Jacques Chirac surrenders. For weeks, thousands of young French citizens, have been demonstrating against a proposed new law that would allow French companies to fire them within the first two years on the job. The young French workers want job guarantees. Chirac caved to their pressure and the new law is dead. In Venezuelan, President Hugo Chavez is encouraging demonstrators to throw eggs at America's Ambassador. The Factor spoke to Michael O'Hanlon, senior fellow of Foreign Policy Studies at the Brookings Institution who said in France, Chirac's change of heart is "being portrayed essentially as a capitulation ... basically, the law is withdrawn." O'Hanlon had a harsher assessment of the Chavez situation, "[Chavez] says is that the U.S. ambassador did not give any notice of where he was going, and therefore, this kind of a mob which doesn't like the United States to begin with, couldn't be easily stopped by police." O'Hanlon didn't buy that story and added, "Of course, that's nonsense because the police were there and just chose not intervene. And Chavez, instead of doing the responsible thing and saying 'it won't happen again, I apologize,' decided to go after the United States. It's obviously a very childish sort of maneuver." The Factor revealed Chavez's true agenda, "He obviously wants a confrontation with us. He's threatened to expel the ambassador now. He wants a confrontation to the USA." Our guest said Chavez thinks he's powerful because of Venezuela's resources, "He believes that his oil is so needed by the world economy in a world where we have no spare capacity and $3 a gallon gasoline here, we're not going to do the one thing that could really hurt him ... So he basically feels like, you know, he can have this one for free. We're not going to come invade him because this kind of insult doesn't justify a military operation."

Unresolved Problems Segment 
Developments in Duke lacrosse case
Guest: Fox News chief judicial analyst Judge Andrew Napolitano

A stripper accused three Duke lacrosse players of sexually abusing her at a party. Authorities took DNA from most of the team. And now the DNA results have come back. Defense Attorney Wade Smith announced, "No DNA material from any young man tested was present on the body of this complaining woman. Not present within her body, not present on the surface of her body, and not present on any of her belongings." So what does this mean for the case? The Factor asked Judge Andrew Napolitano, FOX News chief judicial analyst. He said, "these guys are completely off the hook now ? They can [still] prosecute the case. In this country you can prosecute a case just on the basis of an accuser. But with this kind of evidence supporting the defense, this guy would be voted out of office for dragging a jury through this and forcing them to find these guys not guilty. The DNA evidence here is unassailable." The Factor wants to know what really happened and asked, "So this was a setup, in your opinion, by this woman, who's angry about something?" Napolitano replied, "It was a vendetta that they probably didn't pay her what she thought she was going to make for performing." But The Factor wants to know if the accuser will be prosecuted if it was all a set up. "I think they have to -- they have to justify -- they have to prevent this from happening again," Napolitano said.

Personal Story Segment 
Is the press destructive?
Guests: Fox News analysts Juan Williams & Tony Snow

Baseball superstar Barry Bonds breaks down in an interview on ESPN. Is the media intentionally hurting Bonds? Fox News' Tony Snow says, "Yes, absolutely ? he's not the most lovable guy. Reporters don't like him; teammates don't like him. But I think he's getting a raw deal in the way this has been covered and, frankly, I don't know if he did or didn't take steroids. But did he take them when they were illegal?" Fox's Juan Williams disagreed and said Bonds brought this upon himself, "I think that Barry Bonds is a cheater ? this is a guy, who I believe, according to the books that have come out, the documentation now, you know, you know, the article that was printed in "Sports Illustrated," demonstrates clearly Barry Bonds has a radical change in his physical body, in his performance, and it looks like something intervened, something synthetic. It looks like he had chemical enhancement. And he refuses to talk about it. He's arrogant; he's insufferable. And he has now a pity party going on, on ESPN, which I think, you know, there's a problem there. They've given this guy free air time. So how can you say that the press is hard on Barry Bonds when they give him free time?" The Factor challenged both guests and said the problem is much larger than just Barry Bonds, "They all cheated. Sosa, McGwire, Bonds, the biggest names in baseball, they all pumped up with steroids. As Tony pointed out, it wasn't illegal then, because the Major League Baseball wanted them to pump up so they could hit home runs and people would come to see them. They are absolutely culpable, absolutely. But you have a press in this country. I don't mind them criticizing Bonds, all right? I don't mind them -- look at Pete Rose. Pete Rose bet on baseball games when he was a manager. Yes, they worked him over, but nothing like this. I think it is personal, but I also think that the press is out of control. I think that the press has totally lost it on almost every level."

Back of Book Segment 
Mail-order bride horror story
Guest: Mail-order bride Katerina Brunot & detective Debbie Hollan

Katerina Brunot grew up in Russia. At 24 years old she married 46-year old Frank Sheridan of Atlanta in a mail-order bride deal. She was then subjected to abuse, which eventually led to Sheridan being shot dead by police. The Factor asked Brunot asked how Sheridan was able to lure her into his home, "I did speak a little English, but I could hardly understand some things, especially. I -- I wasn't allowed to have a lot of friends or friends at all. I was basically a prisoner in the house ... I asked myself -- I always ask myself why that happens to me. But I don't know why; I have no idea. I have no idea what kind of man he was." Cobb County Detective Debbie Hollan told The Factor, the authorities got involved "when he had her arrested for aggravated assault. She spent 18 days in the Cherokee County jail for trumped up charges for an injury he actually inflicted on himself. Then the Cherokee County domestic violence shelter got involved and brought me into it. We got a protective order, and she moved to Cobb County so she would be under my jurisdiction and we then tried to build a case against Mr. Sheridan ... we arranged for him to actually bail her out of jail with the understanding that she was coming back to him. And when he made her bond, they took her out the backdoor of the jail to the domestic violence center." The Factor complimented the good crime work, "That's very aggressive action you took. Congratulations, because that's what's needed in this country."
