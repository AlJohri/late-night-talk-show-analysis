Opening Remarks 
Remembering Tony Snow
"Early this morning, Tony Snow died from cancer at Georgetown hospital in Washington, DC. I knew he was very ill, and I knew he didn't want a lot of publicity about it. Which is why for the past couple of months, we didn't mention much about Tony. Tony Snow was born in Berea, Kentucky on June 1, 1955. He was raised in Cincinnati. His father was a high school teacher, his mother a nurse. She died in 1973, when Tony was 17 years old, from the same insidious cancer that Tony had. After graduating from Davidson College in 1977, Tony Snow began a newspaper career. As with most everything else in his life, he succeeded, rising up to become a syndicated columnist and national media commentator. In 1991, Tony took a sabbatical from journalism to write speeches for President Bush the elder. He worked in the White House for two years. In 1996, I first met Tony Snow because he was hired as the host of Fox News Sunday. At first we sparred on the air, but we shared a traditional point of view and a sense of how absurd life can be. As many of you know, Tony succeeded here at Fox News in a big way, and then in 2006 he re-entered the political world as the Press Secretary for President Bush. I talked with him before he made the decision, and I was actually mocking him for getting back into politics, a career we were both very skeptical about. But he said this: there's no better way to learn about the world and what really happens in it than traveling with the President of the United States. Again, Tony succeeded greatly at the White House, although he was often under siege. On Monday we'll get into some of the situations Tony had to deal with. But for tonight, I can tell you that Tony Snow never misled me when I asked him a direct question, no matter how tough the question was. If he couldn't tell me something, he told me why. Even most liberal democrats admired how Tony handled the White House media. He was one of President Bush's best hires. Tony Snow converted to Catholicism. He and his wife, Jill, had three children, Kendall, 16, Robert, 12, and Kristen, 11. I can tell you those kids are bright, respectful, and will do great things for this country. Just wait and see. This evening we will talk to a number of people who knew Tony Snow very well. They will share some of their private moments with him. For me, Tony Snow was a great man, a patriot, an American to be admired. We will miss him tremendously and our prayers are with his family tonight."
B Block 
Condoleezza Rice, Secretary of State
Bill spoke on the phone with Condi Rice, who reminisced about her initial contacts with Tony Snow. "I first met Tony," Secretary Rice recalled, "as one of the White House folks around George H. W. Bush. We were a part of the group that supported that administration and saw great things happen in the world. Over the years our friendship evolved, and I got to know him again from Fox News. He was a pretty tough interview for me, but I always loved being with him." Secretary Rice elaborated on Snow's influence on the Bush White House. "2006 was a pretty rough year in Iraq, a time when a lot of people were losing faith in our ability to win there. Tony believed that we needed to get the truth out about the difficult things that were happening, but he also believed that there were good things happening and we spent a lot of time talking about that. And he could just be very funny. There are long hours and long plane rides, and Tony was always fun to be around." Bill added that "even in the throes of this awful illness, I never saw him depressed. The guy was just unbelievably optimistic. And he was a guy who loved his country. It was an amazing relationship that he had with the United States of America."
C Block 
Joshua Bolton, White House Chief of Staff
Bill then spoke with Josh Bolton, who described Tony Snow's camaraderie with President Bush. "He saw him every day," Bolton said, "and it was a very natural, very personal relationship. It was impossible not to get along with Tony Snow, and they shared a kind of optimism that forged a bond between them and made them natural partners. The president loved Tony and his wife Jill." Bolton added that Snow was far from a mere mouthpiece. "He knew how to deliver a message without making himself the least bit disagreeable. He was an active participant in our policy debates." Bill called Tony Snow "the best press secretary President Bush has had."
D Block 
Andrew Card, Former White House Chief of Staff
Next, Andrew Card spoke about his first and unsuccessful attempt to hire Tony Snow for the Bush White House. "I'd known Tony since 1989," Card said, "when he was a writer for the Washington Times. He was very insightful and he had good, strong conservative credentials. He could be combative without being cutting, he could be feisty and respectful, and he could listen well to other people." Card effusively praised Tony Snow's decency and humanity. "He was kind when he didn't have to be, and he did good things when no one was watching. One time I was looking out the window and I saw some litter - Tony went over and picked up the litter and went on his way. He did random acts of kindness all the time."
E Block 
Ari Fleischer, Former White House Press Secretary
Bill welcomed Ari Fleischer, one of Tony Snow's predecessors at the White House, who recalled a telephone conversation when Tony was considering taking the job. "What he wanted to know," Fleischer said, "was whether he would have time on the weekends to be with his children. That's what he wanted to know the most, and then we talked about the job." Fleischer explained what made Tony an exceptional spokesman. "As White House Press Secretary you are an advocate for the policies and the person in the Oval Office, and Tony knew how to be an effective advocate with grace and a smile. That's why people liked him so much." Bill pointed out that Tony Snow, unlike many other press secretaries, "looked forward to the joust."
F Block 
Media colleagues of Tony Snow
One of the correspondents who regularly sparred with Tony Snow is Bill Plante of CBS, who praised Snow's effectiveness. "He was right up there near the top," Plante said, "or at the top. Tony did his job with panache, style and a sense of humor. And most of all, he was candid. He did not try to hide any obvious difficulties for the administration. He was a very honest man." Plante also touched on Snow's priorities. "We had a dinner one night over some good red wine, and he talked about faith and family, those were his priorities. This was a guy who would leave the White House at 5:00 if one of his children had a soccer game."

Bill welcomed FNC contributor Cal Thomas, who described his relationship with Tony Snow. "I really got to know Tony at a deep level when he came here to Fox. Tony knew that he was ill and that he had this genetic inheritance of colon cancer, and we began to talk about spiritual things. Tony came to have a deep and abiding faith in Jesus Christ - he began to read the Bible and pray and read devotional books that I recommended. He said one of the best things about having cancer is that it allows you to get closer to God." Democratic strategist and FNC contributor Bob Beckel added his own personal recollections of Snow. "We were co-hosts of a Fox broadcast show called 'Off The Record.' The thing that struck me about Tony Snow is that the guy never had a bad word to say about anybody. He just wouldn't do that. He valued your opinion and he would never look down on me because I was a liberal Democrat." Bill added that Tony Snow, unlike nearly everyone else in Washington, had an "absence of malice."
G Block 
Bill's closing thoughts
Bill ended the program with a montage showing Tony Snow as a television host and fielding questions in the White House briefing room, combined with some final remembrances. President George H.W. Bush: "We could all learn from this good man. It's hard to describe the number of times we would say 'thank God we have Tony Snow.'" Barbara Bush: "He was loyal to the core, and to a mother that made a huge difference. He didn't have a mean bone in his body." FNC's Bret Baier: "My son had to have two open heart surgeries. Tony e-mailed almost every day. That is a true gentleman. I'm really going to miss him." The segment ended with these words from Tony Snow himself: "Why sit around and bemoan your fate? Go ahead and get in there and, while you're at it, enjoy every moment you're alive."

Bill concluded: "We put together this program to tell you a little bit about Tony Snow's life and how he conducted it. We hope this presentation will remind people that this country has lost a great patriot. We also hope it will bring some comfort to Tony's family. The measure of any person is how much good that person does on this Earth. There is no other calculation. I met Tony Snow when he was 39 years old. I'm sorry I missed the earlier part of his life. The guy was such a good companion, with a great sense of humor and great insights. But the big thing for me was Tony's courage. Never once did he complain about the cancer, and I talked to him about it all the time. He always recognized the gifts he had been given and never gave into that disease or the anguish and uncertainty it brought. Tony Snow fought the good fight, he lived a good life. There's little else that matters."
