Talking Points Memo & Top Story 
A media war breaks out in America
"There is now an open media war in play. And that situation extends from the White House to your living room. On one side is the liberal establishment media led by NBC News and The New York Times. These pro-Obama vehicles actively promote progressive policies in their hard news coverage. On the other side is the new media that leans more traditional than the urban lefties, who have dominated the press for decades. The new media, which includes Fox News and a bunch of Internet concerns, is skeptical about President Obama and liberalism in general. The establishment media used the Shirley Sherrod story to attack Fox News, which after almost 13 years on the air, has emerged as the dominant media force in America. We set the daily discussion. And I don't think there's any doubt about it, certainly not in Ms. Sherrod's mind. She told CNN she was fired because the White House feared FNC would run with her story. And so, the liberal media sensed an opening to weaken Fox News by using Ms. Sherrod's situation as a hand grenade. But the attack failed. MSNBC and CNN on Wednesday received terrible ratings, while Fox News dominated as usual. Also, Washington Post TV critic Howard Kurtz reported that from the beginning, Fox News executive Michael Clemente ordered his troops to be cautious in reporting the Sherrod story. That fact made liars out of the far-left media people, who told Americans Fox News had ginned up the story in order to hurt black Americans. Once Mr. Kurtz's report became known, the tide turned. And a number of liberal media people became more circumspect. Now the sad truth is that journalism in America is on the verge of collapse. The Factor has documented dozens of important stories ignored by establishment press and hundreds that have been slanted. And those people accuse us of doing the same thing. For you, the honest American, this is very bad news. The folks need accurate information, because we're living in dangerous times. We must elect people who can solve complicated problems and truly look out for us. The press was given constitutional privileges by the founders to serve the folks. But right now, many in the media are abusing those privileges."

Continuing with the top story, The Factor brought in Fox News analyst Bernie Goldberg. Bernie thought that Internet journalist Andrew Breitbart owed Shirley Sherrod an apology for getting the initial story wrong. But he also saw the need for someone else to say sorry: "You know who else owes an apology? Shirley Sherrod. She is a racial bomb thrower of the first order. She accuses people who don't like Barack Obama's policies and people who don't like the health care plan that he put through as racist." The Factor, meanwhile, was surprised at the media's infighting: "I have never seen this level of vitriol between and among media companies. And we're talking about huge companies here. General Electric, News Corporation, New York Times. I mean, this is just savage hand to hand fighting." Bernie thought that the other networks were coming after Fox because of Fox's success: "Fox is doing so well. If Fox weren't doing well, they wouldn't be attacking Fox.

The Factor kept the top story going with the next segment. The guests were Fox News analysts Leslie Marshall and Dana Perino. Perino, who served as press secretary for Pres. George W. Bush, acknowledged that the Obama White House jumped the gun when they fired Shirley Sherrod, but also said she understood why they acted so quickly: "The pressure to respond to reporters when you're in the press office is incredible. You want to get it done fast so you can squash the rumors." Marshall said she saw courage in conservatives who admitted that they were wrong to have prejudged Sherrod: "Bill, I'm going to include you in that. You were a real man. You manned up and you apologized. And I respect you even more after that." The Factor said that he had no choice but to say sorry to Sherrod: "I had to. I made a mistake. If I didn't apologize, I'd be an idiot."
Impact Segment 
What happened to 'post-racial America?'
Despite Pres. Obama's wish for a post-racial America, racial division is once again center stage. The Factor asked former San Francisco mayor Willie Brown if Obama was avoiding unpleasant realities about race. Brown agreed that the president was going out of his way to appear post-racial: "I think that for a long time, President Obama's whole political operation has been geared to making sure people saw him as the president, rather than as the black president." The Factor was sympathetic to the president's dilemma: "I'm not criticizing President Obama for trying to avoid the discussion of race. I don't want to have it anymore. Every time I have it my words get taken out of context and I'm branded a racist. It doesn't do me any good personally to have the discussion because the dishonest reporting in America is just through the roof."
Personal Story Segment 
Will Newt run for president in 2012?
Fox News analyst Newt Gingrich said recently that he was considering running for president in 2012. The Factor asked him why he would put himself and his family through all that stress. Gingrich said that if he ran, he'd have to accept that winning would be ultimately out of his hands: "We have to accept from day one that it's up to God and the American people, not up to us. And we have to be prepared to win or to lose and to be equally happy, because we'll have done our citizenship job by running. We can't guarantee victory." The Factor saw at least one problem for Gingrich's possible candidacy: "The big downside for running for president is that you can't be on Fox News anymore. I mean, could you live with that?" Gingrich said that he'd still be able to come on FNC as a guest. "Maybe, maybe not," The Factor joked. "Depends on if we like you."
At Your Beck and Call Segment 
Glenn Beck on Shirley Sherrod
The Factor asked Fox News host Glenn Beck if the White House's fear of him led them to throw Shirley Sherrod overboard. Beck was flabbergasted: "Do I look like a scary guy? Seriously?" The Factor said he thought so: "Yes, yes, you do. I would never get in the car with you alone. No way." Beck said he wasn't buying the rumor that he was the impetus for Sherrod's firing: "I think this administration is out of control from top to bottom. I think this administration is lying when they're saying they're disturbed by me." The Factor wanted to talk about their recent outing to a Yankees game, along with Geraldo Rivera. Beck said that he got some interesting reactions on his way out of the stadium: "One lady was screaming, 'I love you. You're the best.' And another guy behind her was making hand gestures that you don't normally see in the street of New York." The Factor speculated that Beck may have cost the home team the game: "The Yankees lost 10-2, because they were all staring at you. Couldn't catch the ball."
Back of Book Segment 
Dumbest things of the week
"Red Eye" guy Greg Gutfeld and Fox News correspondent Arthel Neville joined The Factor to run down the dumbest items of the week. One of the items was a clip of 80's pop star Cindi Lauper bashing Pres. Bush. Gutfeld had a diagnosis for Ms. Lauper: "She suffers from what's called liberal Tourette's Syndrome. When you have nothing to say, you say, 'Bush is evil. Religion sucks, and O'Reilly is Satan.' In this case, she's right about O'Reilly." Arthel made a distinction between Lauper and her comments: "I don't think Cyndi Lauper is an idiot. But what she said may have been idiotic." The Factor's "dumbest thing" was a New York Times article that claimed he had been "humiliated" by the Shirley Sherrod story. The Factor thought the Times got it wrong: "It wasn't humiliating, New York Times, you pinheads. I just made a mistake, all right? Everybody makes them. You guys make them. You guys make 100 more than I make, all right? Because you're corrupt and dishonest. I'm honest and not corrupt."

Pinheads and Patriots 
Who's helping and who's hurting?
It's another "You decide" situation -- The Factor played a clip of MSNBC's Luke Russert, son of the late Tim Russert, asking questions to Rep. Charlie Rangel (D-NY) who is in trouble for ethics violations. Russert asked Rangel if he was worried about losing his job. Rangel got mad, telling the reporter "It doesn't sound like NBC, asking these dumb questions." The Factor didn't render a judgment on the situation, but did say this: "You can decide if the congressman is a pinhead. I thought the questions were absolutely legitimate."
