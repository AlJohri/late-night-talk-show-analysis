Talking Points Memo & Top Story 
How Weiner has botched the 'Twittergate' story
"We've known Congressman Anthony Weiner for years and believe he has totally botched the 'Twittergate' story. Weiner's a smart guy, but telling the press he doesn't know if the picture in question is him is eye roll material. Whether or not Weiner sent an inappropriate Tweet to a young woman is still to be determined, but there's no question the Congressman is looking very bad trying to explain the situation. He continues to assert that someone hacked into his Twitter account and sent out a photograph of a guy in his undershorts to a 21-year-old woman. As we've stated, the FBI should be involved in the Weiner story, but they will not confirm anything to us. That's wrong because national security is involved. The worldwide hacking situation is so serious that the Pentagon says computer sabotage coming from another country can constitute an act of war. The USA can not simply stand by and allow members of Congress to be attacked by cyber-criminals. Weiner's case is dumb, but if he was hacked that's serious. High tech sabotage is a danger to all Americans and the Justice Department must get involved now."

The Factor was joined by Republican Congressman Cliff Stearns, who wants a federal investigation into the Weiner "hacking" incident. "I'm asking Homeland Security chief Janet Napolitano to look into cyber-security," Stearns said, "and to assure members of Congress that our BlackBerrys and iPhones are safe, as well as our computers. We want to make sure that whatever happened there is not replicated." The Factor complained that federal law enforcement authorities are not being responsive: "We called the FBI, which won't tell us anything. Someone could blackmail a member of Congress on this kind of stuff, and the FBI won't tell us if they're involved! There's something wrong here. And if Weiner is lying and he wasn't hacked into, he's committing a crime."
Impact Segment 
Analyzing the Palin bus tour
As Sarah Palin continued her bus tour through the Northeast, The Factor asked strategist Dick Morris to analyze Palin's motives. "She has a novel form of attention deficit disorder," Morris quipped. "When she doesn't get enough attention, she gets a disorder. I think she's trying to have one last fling of tremendous national publicity before the presidential race starts. I don't think she's running because I don't think she can win and her pollsters tell her that. But she is a very strong and effective voice for the conservative wing of the Republican Party and I think she wants to freshen up her credentials." The Factor took a playful jab at Morris' track record: "You come down with Charles Krauthammer, Brit Hume and me, who don't think she's going to run. That gives me pause because you've been wrong so many times before."
Personal Story Segment 
Is network news too pro-Obama?
In 2008 the broadcast networks provided then-Senator Obama with overwhelmingly favorable coverage. ABC World News anchor Diane Sawyer entered the No Spin Zone and zealously defended her network, which was the least biased of the "big three." "We are proud of our team," Sawyer said. "Don't forget that you are talking to the network that broke the Jeremiah Wright tapes, and I went down personally to cover the tea party. We're planning fantastic political coverage and we're even talking about having two people cover some of the big issues so we give you two ways of looking at a story." The Factor reminded Sawyer of studies showing that the networks were exceedingly biased in 2008: "Last time around Barack Obama got kid gloves treatment. I am concerned as an American that the three network news nightly broadcasts are in the tank for the Democratic candidate, and I don't think that's fair."
Unresolved Problems Segment 
WHO study: Brain cancer possible from cell phones
The World Health Organization says cell phone use may raise the incidence of brain cancer. The Factor explored that claim with Marty Makary, a professor of public health at Johns Hopkins. "Researchers play this game," Makary said, "where they say it doubles the risk when it goes from super-rare to still super-rare. We're talking about the risk going from 1 in 50,000 going to 1 in 25,000." Nevertheless, Makary maintained that cell phones may eventually prove to be harmful. "Things come to market a lot faster than we can study them in public health. It may take a while to understand the risks, and no one asks those questions when a new technology is being adopted." The Factor reminded everyone that cell phone technology is still relatively new: "It's possible that ten years from now this could be a serious problem if the science is legitimate. A lot of people are pooh-poohing this study, but I am not."
Miller Time Segment 
Dennis Miller on Sarah Palin
The Factor invited Dennis Miller to opine on Sarah Palin's magical and mysterious bus tour. "The bus looks like fun," Miller said, "and Sarah Palin and Todd seem like my type of people. I know they're going tell me she's not a genius, but do I want to be on the bus with a genius? Maybe Jay Carney and I can get in a Winnebago and he can continue to sound like a bingo caller at a VFW. But you know what's great about Palin? She looks cool eating pizza and riding a Harley, and Barack Obama looks like the squarest cat in the world. He plays golf more than Dwight Eisenhower, I'm surprised we don't hear he's a ham radio operator." Miller also expressed hope that the Supreme Court will declare Obamacare unconstitutional. "Don't shoot for the European model in health care, it's not going to work. Give Bill Clinton this - at least when he told you he dug 'European models,' he actually meant European models."
Back of Book Segment 
Casey Anthony trial reaches day 7
The trial of Casey Anthony, accused of killing her 2-year-old daughter, continues in Orlando. FNC's Geraldo Rivera again expressed doubt about Anthony's guilt. "The question is whether she killed her in the way the state defines capital murder," Rivera said, "and that's not the case here. Was this aggravated child abuse, heinous, atrocious and cruel? That's what she's being tried for, but there is no cause of death and no motive. How do you explain the fact that every single witness has testified that she was a perfect mother? She loved this child, who was never neglected or abused." The Factor insisted that Casey Anthony is obviously guilty of killing her little girl: "In any narcissistic personality they have the ability to appear normal, but there's always a tipping point in a sociopathic personality. The tipping point was reached in this case. She snapped."
