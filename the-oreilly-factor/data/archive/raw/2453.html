Talking Points Memo & Top Story 
Is the military fed up with Pres. Obama?
"The liberal New York Times reports that elements of the U.S. military are becoming disenchanted with President Obama because he is taking so long to make a decision about sending more troops to Afghanistan. Talking Points believes that is true, that the military is not happy right now with the commander in chief. My opinion is based on Defense Secretary Gates, who has publicly stated that Barack Obama must make a decision on troop deployment. For his part, the president continues to say he needs 'clarity' from the Afghan government before he commits more American forces. Afghan leader Karzai now says he will allow a runoff election, but let's be honest - the Afghan government is corrupt and that's not going to change any time soon. In the end, I believe President Obama will send the soldiers and Marines, but his hesitation is teeing off the military. It is also helping the Taliban and Al Qaeda, who will fight harder if they believe they can outlast America. If President Obama sincerely believes that the Afghan government is so corrupt and the theater is so out of control that it's impossible to win, then he should say that and get out! One way or the other, the president needs to act ... now."

The Factor welcomed former Pentagon official K.T. McFarland, who responded to the Talking Points Memo. "You framed the problem brilliantly," McFarland said. "The problem is not going to get any easier and President Obama, by delaying, is putting American troops in danger. He has to make a decision and Secretary Gates is prodding him." But foreign policy analyst Chris Preble denied that the Pentagon and White House are at odds. "The talk of a rift has really been overplayed. The president has already approved a substantial increase of troops in March and in the summer, and this is in the face of a public that is quite ambivalent about this war." The Factor reiterated that President Obama should make a decision. "My contention is that this emboldens our enemy in Afghanistan because Barack Obama has taken more than four weeks to make a decision. But I don't have all the facts."
Stossel Matters Segment 
New decision on medical marijuana issued
Veteran ABC News reporter John Stossel is the newest member of the Fox News team. The Factor welcomed Stossel and asked how his media colleagues reacted to his move. "There were people who already viewed me as a nut," Stossel said. "The mainstream media views us libertarians as nuts. But there are a lot of things I want to do that they didn't have time for at ABC." Stossel also commented on reports of widespread abuse of medical marijuana, which is legal in California. "Adults should be free to take anything," Stossel averred, "and some people who are in pain get help from medical marijuana. I want to legalize all drugs for adults." The Factor challenged Stossel by focusing on child welfare: "According to some research, 75% of all the child neglect and abuse in America is caused by drug-involved adults, which includes alcohol. And you, John Stossel, want to make it easier for adults to get whacked out on all substances."
Barack and a Hard Place Segment 
Obama on private health care reform
Obama-watchers Monica Crowley and Alan Colmes evaluated the president's offensive against private health insurers. "The president is accusing companies of what he and his administration are guilty of," Crowley asserted. "The health insurance companies have bitten their lip, hoping they could get the best deal possible, but now it looks like they are buying into their own extinction. This administration sees profit as a four-letter word." Colmes argued that even a government-run system will leave room for private companies. "There should be a single-payer basic health insurance covering everyone. Private companies will not go out of business because people will buy extra insurance. The Factor warned against replacing a flawed system with something that may be worse: "Private insurance companies are not looking out for the folks, but why would you have any confidence that the government would be better?"
Factor Follow Up Segment 
Teacher acquitted on sex charges with student
26-year-old Nicole Howell, a former high school English teacher in Kentucky who has been acquitted of having sex with a 16-year-old male student, entered the No Spin Zone and recounted her ordeal. "Words can't describe how painful this was," Howell said. "I was devastated by the lies and accusations, and I can't say enough about how horrible the experience has been. I got through it with my faith in God and my family and friends, who stuck by me." Howell's attorney Eric Deters denounced the accuser and local authorities. "The prosecution mishandled this case, the investigation was terrible, and there was an overreaction by the school district." The Factor lauded Howell for persevering under intense pressure: "Congratulations to you, Nicole, you're a very brave woman. The story didn't add up and I knew what you were going through. I know your life is going to be good from now on."
'Is it Legal?' Segment 
Should child services intervene in balloon boy case?
The Factor asked legal whizzes Lis Wiehl and Kimberly Guilfoyle whether Richard and Mayumi Heene, who engineered last week's "balloon boy" hoax, should lose custody of their children. "Absolutely," Wiehl declared. "The child wasn't just hiding for those six hours, he was put there by his parents. I've been very hesitant to take kids away from their parents, but the mother and father worked on this so they could get a reality show. It's horrible." Guilfoyle advocated a more cautious approach. "There are parents who are far worse than these who have custody. An investigation is warranted - you sit the parents down and interview them and perhaps you take the children for a period of time. The ultimate goal is reunification of the family." The Factor agreed that all steps should be taken to keep the family intact: "My solution would be to have a heavy presence of social services at the home, but I don't think I'd take the children out of the home. You have to think of the kids."
Back of Book Segment 
Great American News Quiz
Martha MacCallum and Steve Doocy resumed their rivalry in The Great American News Quiz. Among The Factor's questions: "Who wrote the Oscar-winning song featured in Al Gore's documentary?" ... "Business Week recently broke a story about which scandal at General Electric?" Doocy's astounding streak of consecutive correct answers ended at twelve, and MacCallum then strolled to an easy victory. The entire GANQ can be found here on BillOreilly.com under 'Fun Stuff."
