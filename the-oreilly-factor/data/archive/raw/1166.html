Talking Points Memo & Top Story 
The rat line in Vermont
Guest: Former State Senator Wendy Wilton

"For the second time in less than a year, a Vermont child has been brutally assaulted and a sexual predator allowed to escape justice. The victim is a little boy, four years old, who told his aunt he was scared to be around 37-year-old Andrew James. The woman checked his story and learned that that James had been abusing the boy in a terrible way, too terrible to describe on television. The Manchester, Vermont police were called. And according to the affidavit, the boy told investigators and a social service agent that he had been molested 10 times by James. The little boy is poor and may have a learning disability, but his story was backed up by his older brother and his mother. The predator, Andrew James, has two previous assault convictions, [including] one felony where he attacked his wife with a screwdriver in front of their children. For that, he served 30 days. For molesting the four-year-old, James will serve no prison time. Judge David Howard sentenced James to probation and treatment. If he violates probation, he could go to prison. Prosecutor Andrew Costello asked for probation, but the judge could have rejected this ridiculous sentence. He had the power to do it. 'Talking Points' believes Vermont authorities simply don't care about this poor child and are OK with James being out on the street where he is right now. That, of course, is appalling. You'll remember that last year, Vermont Judge Edward Cashman sentenced a man who raped a six-year-old girl over a four year period to just 60 days in jail. The outcry embarrassed the state of Vermont, but still Jessica's Law was voted down by the legislature. Vermont has become a secular progressive enclave, a state that believes in restorative justice: that is healing for the criminal as well as the victim. I doubt the four-year-old boy will ever be healed. As for the criminal, he's walking free this evening thanks to Judge David Howard."

The Factor threatened the state of Vermont: "I am considering asking people to boycott the state of Vermont. Not go there, not buy their products. I don't want to do it. People that we spoke to in southern Vermont, they all knew about this case. They all were horrified. But the fact remains the legislature voted down Jessica's Law. The media continues to cover up for this stuff in Vermont. And the situation is out of control. So boycott or no?" Former state senator Wendy Wilton said, "I would say no. But I tell you that most Vermonters are outraged by these cases. They were outraged last year about Cashman and they're outraged by this case as well. What we have is a judiciary that has gone wild. The legislature last year, and I was very involved in this bill, passed a sex offender bill that at least gave us minimum mandatory sentences, a sentencing commission, but that hasn't really had an opportunity to work yet." The Factor thought some of the blame resided with the Vermont voters: "But you were voted out, Ms. Wilton. So I'm saying to myself that there is an element in Vermont, the people, who don't want this, who believe we can rehabilitate the guy. I'm saying to myself, I don't know what's going on in Vermont. I used to go there when I was a child. It was a traditional state. It has changed into a secular-progressive state."

Impact Segment 
Kidnapped boys recovered in Missouri
Guest: Greta Van Susteren

The Factor discussed the case of 15-year-old Shawn Hornbeck, discovered in the apartment of 41-year-old Michael Devlin last week, along with 13-year-old Ben Ownby. Devlin had allegedly kidnapped both boys; Shawn had been missing for four years. "The question is, why didn't he escape when he could have? There are all kinds of theories about that. The Stockholm syndrome thing, I don't buy it. I've never bought it. I didn't think it happened in the Patty Hearst case. I don't think it happened here." Fox's Greta Van Susteren argued, "First of all, we don't know all the facts. But don't forget that Elizabeth Smart likewise had an opportunity to leave and did not. For some reason, when young people are picked up and taken under the influence of adults, they are very receptive to what the adults do. So I would not so quickly dismiss the Stockholm syndrome. And remember, he was a kid when he was picked up. He was 11." The Factor countered, "The difference in the Smart case is that this guy was always hovering around the little girl. And she wasn't gone for the long period of time as this guy was. Now what we have learned-and this is why I don't believe in the Stockholm syndrome-this guy Shawn Hornbeck was gone four years, [and] authorities actually say he taunted his own parents on their website. I mean, this is a troubled kid."

Factor Follow Up Segment 
Should Durham DA Nifong be charged?
Guests: Attorney Michelle Suskauer & Fox News legal analyst Lis Wiehl

The Factor brought us up to speed in the Duke Lacrosse-rape case: "Durham D.A. Mike Nifong is finally off the Duke rape case. Nifong is under fire for holding back some vital information; they went to a DNA lab. The DNA came back. There was no DNA of the three students on the women, but there were DNA of seven other men on her. Nifong knew that and did not hand it over to defense attorneys, which is required by law. He broke the law. Can criminal charges be filed against Nifong?" Fox's Lis Wiehl answered, "They can for obstruction of justice, withholding evidence, tampering with evidence, even civil rights violations. But it's never happened before in a context like this. Prosecutors have almost complete and absolute immunity from any kind of prosecution themselves for the way they handled the case." Defense attorney Michelle Suskauer said that this case should change that: "I think that this is going to be the test case. If there ever was a case, it should be this one. If a prosecutor steps out of his role or her role as a prosecutor and becomes law enforcement, or investigator, or is trying to do something for personal gain, they lose that shield of immunity. And they can be prosecuted." The Factor made a call: "Well, I'm going to make a prediction. He's not going on be criminally charged, but I think he probably will lose his license."

Unresolved Problems Segment 
Illegal aliens: Catch & release
Guest: Jesse Diaz, League of United Latin American Citizens

A federal study of 100 illegal aliens with arrest records found that they had each been arrested and released six times, on average. The League of United Latin American Citizens believes some of the problem is that Mexican illegals are being corrupted after they get to America. The Factor said that was crazy: "Number one, the illegal aliens shouldn't be here. And number two, the culture from which they come is a lot more violent than the USA. For example, homicides in Mexico are double the average in the USA." LULAC president Jesse Diaz responded, "Well, basically what I'm saying is they have assimilated to the American culture. I was talking about family values. I'm seeing the saggy pants and of course the family break-ups. And those are some of the issues that I was talking about. And basically what I said is they're picking up some of our bad habits. They're picking up some of our good habits, too." The Factor thought the illegals could only blame themselves: "My point is this: If you sneak into this country, no matter what happens to you, it's your own fault. If I sneak into Mexico, and I don't bring my U.S. passport and I don't go through a border checkpoint and I just walk across the Rio Grande the other way, no matter what happens to me in Mexico after I sneak in is my own fault."

Culture War Segment 
Newsweek prints excerpts from OJ book
Guests: Fox News analysts Laura Schwartz & Kirsten Powers

Two months after the O.J. Simpson book was canceled, Newsweek has printed a story about the banished book. Fox News analyst Kirsten Powers said they were within their rights to print it: "I would have run it. It's newsworthy. If you read the article, if you read what he wrote, it's virtually a confession." Analyst Laura Schwartz dissented: "I wouldn't run the piece, because money or no money, O.J. Simpson craves the attention. So you're giving him what he wants. Rupert Murdoch knows the bottom line better than anyone. And he not only heard the public outcry against the disgust of this book, but he listened to it. Then you've got Newsweek, who heard the outcry, joined in the outcry and prints it anyway." The Factor said: "I don't think it's newsworthy, and I'll tell you why. This is a charade. Everyone knows this man did it. Everyone with an ounce of sense knows he did it. Now, after reading this Newsweek thing, you'll know it more. You'll know it more. Yes, that's Newsweek's defense. But is it newsworthy? No. It's designed to sell magazines." Next, the discussion turned to Senator Barbara Boxer's apparent insinuation to Secretary of State Condi Rice that because Rice doesn't have children she could not empathize with the loss of American lives in Iraq. Powers said, "I think that Boxer was grandstanding. I think she just was making this sort of generic argument, which is a stupid argument in itself. I mean, did Madeleine Albright have children fighting in the Kosovo intervention?" Schwartz agreed: "I think that she was making a very poorly made point out of context. The members of Congress that voted for the authorization of war for President Bush to have the ability to go to war, they all didn't have sons and daughters in active military. And I believe every president, when they see the face of a fallen soldier's mother, or father, or brother or sister, they can empathize [...]." The Factor thought Boxer's attack could backfire: "I think it hurts the Democratic Party that Barbara Boxer does this, because it shows once again that Democrats don't have a policy in Iraq or to fight terror."

Back of Book Segment 
Bindi Irwin controversy
Guest: Louise Pennell, Australian TV

A stingray killed "Crocodile Hunter" Steve Irwin in September, and now his daughter Bindi and wife Terry are touring the USA promoting Australian tourism. Now Australian media is raising questions about whether Irwin's daughter is being exploited. Louise Pennell is a foreign correspondent for Australian TV: "I think questions are being raised as to whether this is too much, too soon. Questions are being raised. Can we make a heroine out of an 8-year-old as we made a hero out of her father? And I don't think there's anyone who disagrees with the fact that Bindi has the same passion and affinity for nature as her father. But what these journalists in Australia are asking now is, does she actually understand? Should we sort of slow down on this sort of promotional roundabout that she's going on?" The Factor thought it best to stay away from this one: "I feel terrible for the little girl and her mom and the whole family. Whether that's good therapy for her, I don't know. So I'm not going to say anything on this one. I'm going to let the mom do what the mom thinks what the mom thinks is best. I think that's the best thing to do."
