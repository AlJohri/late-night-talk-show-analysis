Talking Points Memo & Top Story 
Analyzing Obama's health care press conference
"President Obama's intentions are good - he wants to clean up the health care mess that's hurting millions of Americans. There's nothing wrong with that, but his plan is chaotic and the expense is ill-defined. Tonight, the president faced a rather docile press corps and buried them with rhetoric. I've often said the government needs to control the medical industry with a lot of oversight. But after another hour of Barack Obama explaining his program, I still don't know what it is. The man is incapable of breaking it down so that we the people can understand it. That's the crux of the matter. If President Obama wants Americans to support an overhaul of the health care system, he's got to put it in bullet points so we know what's going to happen and who is going to pay for it. Otherwise, this is not going to fly."

The Factor challenged political correspondents A.B. Stoddard and Sam Youngman to explain President Obama's health care proposal. "We don't know what the details are," Youngman conceded, "but I'm going to try to make some sense of it after I get done talking with you. There was nothing new here, and I'm not sure he did enough to regain his message." Stoddard agreed that President Obama was short on specifics. "Although he makes a good case for his general principles, what he doesn't like to do is talk about how he'll pay for it. I expected him to show some leadership tonight, but he wants Congress to figure this out." The Factor urged the president to step up and delineate a concrete proposal: "People just gave an hour of their time to watch President Obama go all over the place, but I still don't know what he's going to do. People are starting to lose confidence in his leadership because this is his signature issue."

Political strategist Dick Morris was next up in the No Spin Zone with his analysis of the president's health goals. "You have to read into his vocabulary," Morris said. "Obama's plan will take away your doctor's ability to decide what care to give you; a federal health board will decide whether or not you can get a hip replacement. And your health insurance premiums will go up because you will have to pay the costs, one way or another, of insuring another 50-million people who aren't covered." Morris also ridiculed the notion that health care reform will not add to the deficit. "Nothing in health care has ever paid for itself, and if you're cutting medical incomes, it will drive doctors out of medicine."

For one more viewpoint, The Factor welcomed Obama supporter and professor Marc Lamont Hill. "The president gave more specifics than ever before," Hill contended. "There is a lot of fear-mongering going on from Republicans who are saying this will crumble the American economy, so he responded to the broad public discourse. I agree that he should have given more specifics about where the money is coming from, but he did say that two-thirds of the money is already in the system, which reassured the American people." The Factor raised the specter of deteriorating privacy. "It worries me that my medical history and your medical history are going to be on a computer in Washington, which is unlike the doctor-patient confidentiality we have had in this country."
Barack and a Hard Place Segment 
Colmes & Crowley face off
The Factor was joined by FNC's Monica Crowley and Alan Colmes, who analyzed the highs and lows of President Obama's past week. "I've noticed a trend," Crowley griped, "in how President Obama goes after honest opponents of his policies. He uses the term 'scare tactics,' and he tries to paint Republicans and moderate Democrats as trying to scare people. Meanwhile, he's saying that unless we adopt his nationalization of health care, things are going to get so much worse." Not surprisingly, Colmes had a ready response for Crowley: "It's laughable to hear 'scare tactics' and 'Obama' come out of the mouths of conservatives who supported Bush. It didn't bother them when Bush did it." Colmes also lauded President Obama for giving a "tough love" message to a black audience. "He said African Americans have to be known for things other than sports and music. A white president could never have given a speech like that, which was inspirational." The Factor agreed with Colmes' appraisal, with a caveat: "Sometimes the president's rhetoric is very effective, as it was there, and sometimes his rhetoric is just b.s., and health care is the best example."
Miller Time Segment 
Dennis Miller on Barbara Boxer
Dennis Miller trained his eye and wit on Senator Barbara Boxer, who was accused of racial condescension by Harry Alford, head of the Black Chamber of Commerce. "This was the most cathartic thing I've seen in the last ten years," Miller opined. "I haven't seen a Boxer bloodied like that since Chuck Wepner, and it's about time it caught up to these people on the left." Miller moved to Prince Charles, who claimed we have precisely 96 months to avoid an ecological Armageddon. "That's good," Miller quipped, "because I won't have to listen to this mindless crap anymore. The only reason Prince Charles' head isn't completely up his arse is because his ears are so big." Miller also took a moment to solve a grave national crisis: "Paula Abdul should leave American Idol, and Sonia Sotomayor should take over as judge. She won't judge people on singing, but will have a system of prearranged quotas. Contestants won't even have to sing, and they'll change the name of the show to 'American Idle.'"
Back of Book Segment 
Did You See That?: Erin Andrews videotape
The Factor introduced a new segment called "Did You See That?", which looks at stories involving video footage and social issues. First up, the news that comely ESPN sports reporter Erin Andrews was surreptitiously taped while naked in her hotel room. The Factor explored the breach of privacy with FNC's Jane Skinner and Courtney Friel. "Erin Andrews was in a hotel room," Skinner reported, "and some sicko set up a tiny camera. She was naked, doing her hair and makeup, and what did they do with the video? The put it on line." Friel worried about the effect the incident will have on others. "This is a big deal because women everywhere are going to be so paranoid, and there are cameras everywhere. I guess you have to either keep your clothes on or stay in shape." The Factor called for new laws to cover these kinds of high-tech crimes. "This should be a felony, and states should introduce laws covering cyber-stalking."
Pinheads and Patriots 
Tyler Perry & South Korean legislators
Wednesday's Patriot: Comedian Tyler Perry, who is sending 65 young kids to Disney World. And the Pinheads: The South Korean legislators who engaged in a mini-brawl while debating a bill.
