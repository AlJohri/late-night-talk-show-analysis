Talking Points Memo 
A Bad Day for America
Watch more video in the Video Center...
"21-year-old Dylann Roof shot nine people dead last night inside a Charleston, South Carolina church. He was caught this morning in North Carolina, about 250 miles from Charleston. He surrendered without a struggle. Roof dropped out of high school in the 10th grade and has been arrested at least twice, once on a drug possession charge. Apparently he's a longtime racist, wearing anti-black patches on his clothing. Those who know him say he often made inappropriate statements about African-Americans. No question that was an act of terror, no different than what ISIS and al Qaeda do in targeting civilians because of who they are. In this case skin color seems to be the motivator. This was an execution designed to terrorize, just like the Boston Marathon bombing and the mass-murder at Fort Hood, Texas. Terror acts have sadly become common the world over, as thousands of human beings are being slaughtered by insane fanatics. Having just finished investigating John Hinckley who nearly assassinated President Reagan, I can tell you that troubled people often act out against innocent strangers. That kind of pathology can never be stopped, it has been with us since man began walking the planet. Pundits who say they can prevent individual acts of terror are misleading you; no one can do that. Unfortunately, President Obama strayed into the theoretical today, saying 'this type of mass violence does not happen in other advanced countries.' Of course mass murder happens in other countries, even those with all-out bans on firearms. Preventing random mass murder is impossible, as is stopping the deranged acts of terrorists. The best we can do is slow them down. There are those who condemn America as being a violent place where racial animus is the rule, not the exception. But that is a lie. This is a nation of 320 million people who have freedoms beyond those of most other countries. Most places in America are safe and secure; most citizens are decent, fair, and law abiding; most police officers are honest and dedicated to helping people; most civil servants, teachers, firefighters, and local officials take lower pay to look out for the folks. But now an ignorant terrorist like Dylann Roof damages the image of America by a disgraceful act of violence. Decent people mourn the lives of the nine Americans shot dead, and we also mourn for our country. Evil is present in the United States, as it is everywhere."
Top Story 
Race Relations in America
Watch more video in the Video Center...
The Factor welcomed Martin Luther King III, who spoke about the South Carolina murders and race relations. "My first response was, 'Oh my goodness, not again,'" he said. "If one can not be safe in the sanctity of places of worship, what has our society come to? From time to time we have seen these terrible incidents and we must find a way to examine our nation in a different way. Some have accepted a climate of violence - look at our video games and our movies. Should Americans demand something else?" The Factor agreed that societal pressure against violence can be invaluable: "We can't censor things, but you're absolutely right that there is no pushback against deriving pleasure from violent pursuits. We should try to change our mindset in America."
Impact Segment 
Tragedy & Politics
Watch more video in the Video Center...
Almost immediately after the Charleston murders, Hillary Clinton and some other politicians used the tragedy to call for stricter gun control. The Factor asked TV host Montel Williams to opine. "This is not a gun issue," Williams declared, "and I am a 100% supporter of the Second Amendment. But I also understand that laws need to be in place to stop nuts like this. You said that we can't stop somebody like this guy, but we can. Two weeks ago we stopped a radicalized young man who was trying to recruit people to go over to ISIS. We need to look at this man Roof as if he's a radicalized separatist and apply the same treatment, watching what he does on line." The Factor reiterated that lone wolf psychopaths are nearly impossible to stop: "The killers in Connecticut and Colorado, John Hinckley, and this guy Roof are the same person. They are sociopaths and losers who are lashing out. You can't stop them."
Impact Segment 
More in South Carolina
One of those killed in the Charleston massacre was Reverend Clementa Pinckney, a minister and state senator. The Factor welcomed South Carolina state representative Todd Rutherford, who spoke about his late colleague. "Senator Pinckney was called to the ministry when he was only 13 and ordained when he was 18," Rutherford said, "and he was one of the kindest and gentlest people you'd ever meet. He had a booming voice and a huge presence, but he never said an angry word." Earlier in the day, during an appearance on CNN, Rutherford had implied that Fox News' "hate speech" was somehow to blame for the shootings. The Factor invited him to clarify and elaborate. "It is disturbing to most African Americans to watch," Rutherford said, "as Fox News continues to question whether the president is really a citizen of the United States." The Factor disputed Rutherford's facts and veracity: "I don't know anybody on this network who does that, I don't believe that's reality and you'd have to show me. You are equating what he did in that church with our commentary people? I don't think Senator Pinckney would have embraced that kind of rhetoric."
Weekdays with Bernie 
Responsibility in the Media
Watch more video in the Video Center...
Bernie Goldberg entered the No Spin Zone to assess coverage of the South Carolina carnage. "There are some people in the media," he said, "who will use innocent people being killed in a church as an opportunity to bash conservative media. It has a long history - it happened after the massacre in Arizona and after the Oklahoma City bombing. Today Salon magazine had a writer who claimed Fox News and right-wing media encourage violence. And the guest you had on earlier made those same ridiculous assertions on CNN. The anchor let it go, he didn't challenge it! The first reaction of some on the left is to blame right-wing rhetoric without any evidence."
Back of the Book 
Live on the Scene in Charleston
The Factor wrapped up Thursday's show with live analysis from Geraldo Rivera in Charleston. "The headline here," Rivera said, "is that a racist Judas inculcated himself with faith-based people and pretended to care about their prayers. He entered their church only to cut them down. At first it reminded me of the four little girls who were blown to smithereens in Birmingham in 1963, but it now reminds me of Charles Manson, who wanted to ignite a race war. I think this racist psychopath is like Manson in that sense." Rivera concluded, "Here in Charleston everybody, white and black, is on the same page and will get through this together."
