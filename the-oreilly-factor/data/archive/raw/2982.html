Top Story 
Rubio's Factor debut!
Watch more video in the Video Center...
Guest: Marco Rubio

The Factor began Monday's show by welcoming Senator Marco Rubio, a rising star in the Republican Party. Rubio reacted to a Washington Post story that implied he has lied about his parents' being "exiles" from Castro's Cuba. Some excerpts:
O'Reilly: "Did you mislead anybody about your family history?"
Rubio: "Absolutely not. I was always under the impression that my parents came in the late '50's, but they came earlier as legal residents. But that's irrelevant as to whether they're 'exiles' or not. On the day that I was born in 1971 my parents could not return to the nation of their birth unless they were willing to live under communism. There is not a single credible Cuban voice in Miami who will dispute that my parents were exiles."
O'Reilly: "Did they call you before they published the article?"
Rubio: "They actually published the article and then they interviewed me. The bottom line is that I don't need to embellish my narrative, which is very simple: I am the son of exiles and immigrants and that has framed my political thought."
The conversation turned to widespread speculation that the eventual Republican nominee will ask Marco Rubio to join the ticket.
Rubio: "I'm honored, but I'm focused on my job in the Senate. I haven't even been there a year yet.
O'Reilly: "Are you ruling it out right here?"
Rubio: "Yes."
O'Reilly: "So no matter who the nominee is, if they ask you, Senator Rubio, to consider being vice president, you're going to say absolutely not? It's off the table?
Rubio: "It is."
O'Reilly: "That's almost shocking. You're made for this. You're good on TV, you're absolutely a conservative, and you represent Hispanic Americans in a fine way. If you could elevate the Republican Party into the White House, you're telling me you wouldn't!"
Rubio: "I believe I can do just as much good in the Senate."
O'Reilly: "I can see you in ten years, maybe even sooner, running for president yourself."
Rubio: "Or having a show here on Fox!"
O'Reilly: "The Rubio Factor! Well, I've got to get out of here sometime and you could slide right in."
Top Story II 
Will Rubio be on the VP choice in 2012?
Guest: Brit Hume

Fox News political analyst Brit Hume assessed Marco Rubio's declaration that he will not be on the Republican ticket. "That's what you say," Hume surmised, "because if he would have suggested that he was willing, the word would go out that he's running for vice president. I think he's not going to campaign for it, but when the party selects a nominee he could still do it. Rubio is a very big hero to American conservatives because he articulates their case so well, and he has all the other stuff going for him."
Impact Segment 
More chaos and violence from Occupy Wall Street
Guests: Juan Williams & Mary Katharine Ham

With violence increasing at "Occupy" protests around the country, FNC analysts Juan Williams and Mary Katharine Ham assessed the protesters and their tactics. "This is Halloween season," Williams began, "and Republicans are in a fright over 'Occupy Wall Street.' You can judge the effectiveness of it by how scared Republicans are that it's activating the Democratic base and changing the narrative." Ham put forth a radically different analysis. "Part of the movement recognizes that Obama and the Democrats have gotten a lot of donations from Wall Street, so this is not necessarily a partisan movement against Republicans. They love their civil disobedience and they will continue to do it because they don't have to pay a price for it in the media, but they're not using this movement to move national politics or to register voters." The Factor theorized that most citizens are turned off by the "Occupy" excesses: "90% of independent Americans don't like them, don't trust them, and think they're radicals."
Personal Story Segment 
What does it mean to be a man?
Guest: Bill Bennett

The Factor welcomed former Education Secretary William Bennett, whose new book examines manliness in modern America. "The central theme is that we've gotten off the path," Bennett said. "It takes an effort to turn boys into men and you have to have an idea of what it means to be a man. You need to shape them - you point out men and you say 'be like those men.' You point out role models who embody the real virtues of work and marriage and faith." Bennett argued that even feminists want men to be strong masculine figures. "Men are falling behind in achievement and work and ambition and feminists are not celebrating this because they're looking for men who are responsible and who will take the role of protector."
Weekdays with Bernie Segment 
New media spin: economy isn't Obama's problem
Watch more video in the Video Center...
Guest: Bernie Goldberg

The Factor invited FNC's Bernie Goldberg to opine on mainstream media folks who are suddenly saying the economy is out of President Obama's control. "The media can't possibly be as enthusiastic this time around as they were last time," Goldberg said, "because if they slobber over him as much this time, the media and the President would have to get a room. But will they be enthusiastic again? I think the answer is yes. Four years ago the media picked up their pom-poms and cheered him because Barack Obama was an historic figure, and once you fall madly in love with somebody it isn't easy to fall out of love just a few days later. Once Obama is facing a real opponent, the media will be enthusiastic for him again."

Reality Check
Bernie Madoff's daughter-in-law has revealed portions of her correspondence with the imprisoned thief. The Factor's Check "One thing I've learned in this life - people rarely change and evil stays evil. Stephanie Madoff Mack tried to make Bernie Madoff feel guilty about his deeds in correspondence, but it didn't work. Narcissists like Madoff will never show remorse." Meanwhile, Mitt Romney's campaign is running a tough new anti-Obama ad in Nevada. The Factor's Check "Nevada is one of the states the Republicans must take back in order to defeat the President."
