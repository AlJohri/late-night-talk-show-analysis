Talking Points Memo 
Who wants to influence your vote?
"While in Washington Thursday, I was able to talk with some very well-informed people, as well as read the latest dispatch from stratfor.com, perhaps the best information web site in the country. This may surprise you, but the Iraq situation, the Foley scandal, and the Woodward book all have one thing in common - November's election. The war in Iraq has dramatically changed - no longer is the primary enemy Al Qaeda and the Baathists, we are now fighting Iran. Iran wants to keep the US pinned down in Iraq so America can not rally opposition against Teheran for its nuclear ambitions and other things. So now Iran has ordered its killers to up the violence in Iraq for the next month, believing Americans will hold President Bush responsible and vote in the Democrats, who the Iranians believe are not as aggressive on foreign policy. On the Foley front, it's now clear the scandal was timed to put the GOP on the defensive, and it has. Finally, the Bob Woodward book was released before the election to give it maximum impact. Talking Points believes voters should have as much information as possible before going to the polls, so I have no problem with the book. By far the most distressing part of this analysis is the Iranian connection. These brutal people are now driving the death and destruction inside Iraq, and are directly trying to influence our elections here. That is outrageous."
Top Story 
Outrage at Columbia University
Guests: Avi Zenilman, The Blue and White & Chris Kulawik, Columbia University Republicans

Members of the Minuteman Project, the border protest group, were shouted down and harassed at Columbia University Wednesday night. Chris Kulawik of the college Republicans, who invited the speakers, expressed disappointment with his fellow students. "Some people are saying it's the speakers' fault for antagonizing the crowd. And people are taking shots at me, saying I was trying to instigate this and cause trouble. I brought them because they offer something different for the campus." Columbia junior Avi Zenilman agreed that the protestors were out of line. "The Minutemen should have gotten a chance to spew what they were spewing, and it was embarrassing for Columbia. But I think this can be seen as an isolated incident. Most students at Columbia do not want chaos and protesting." The Factor disputed the notion that this was an isolated incident. "If you get groupthink, which you have at Columbia, this kind of thing is inevitable. I am convinced that there is a left-wing jihad that holds power at Columbia University. This would never have happened to a left-wing speaker."
Impact Segment 
Schwarzenegger criticized
Guests: Nativo Lopez, Mexican-American Political Association

California Governor Arnold Schwarzenegger is being attacked for urging Mexican immigrants to "assimilate" and become "part of the American fabric." Mexican-American activist Nativo Lopez explained why he finds that advice offensive. "It demonstrates the ignorance by the governor. Immigrants are integrating and assimilating faster today than ever before." Lopez also excoriated the Minutemen as a hate group. "The Minutemen are the modern version of the KKK. They've assaulted with their language and they've assaulted physically." The Factor defended the Minutemen and issued a challenge. "The KKK were criminals who used violence to intimidate black Americans. You have no proof that the Minutemen did anything violent, yet you compare them to the KKK. I'll have you back on this broadcast next week if you can give me one documented hate incident involving the Minutemen."
Unresolved Problems Segment 
Partisan divide over Foley case
Guest: Congressman Patrick McHenry (R-NC)

Republican Congressman Patrick McHenry, who is calling for Democrat leaders to testify under oath about the Foley scandal, explained why. "We know that Mark Foley perpetrated the sick acts. The only issues remaining are what person or group had access to these instant messages and e-mails? I've asked Democrats to testify about what they knew and when they knew it. Democrats are accessories to this crime because instead of taking this information an turning it over to law enforcement to protect children from further harm from this predator, they kept it for political gain." The Factor reminded McHenry that there's one primary villain. "There's a chance this was orchestrated by the Democrats, but they didn't force Foley to do what he did."
Factor Investigation Segment 
Objections to drunk driver's murder charge
Guests: Parents Jennifer & Neil Flynn

In July of 2005 drunk driver Martin Heidgen smashed into a car on Long Island, killing 7-year old Katie Flynn. Because his blood-alcohol limit was three times the legal limit, Heidgen is being charged with second-degree murder, which some have called excessive. Katie's parents told The Factor why the murder charge is warranted. "I want him dead," Neil Flynn pronounced. "He murdered my daughter. His actions clearly indicate depraved indifference toward the lives of everyone on that road. He won't look at me and neither will his attorney." Jennifer Flynn agreed. "I'm looking forward to him going to jail as a murderer. He murdered her and he should get the maximum of twenty years." The Factor predicted that Heidgen will receive a stiff sentence. "He has a previous drunk driving conviction. It's a Long Island jury and I think this guy is going to do some serious time."
Personal Story Segment 
Dick Morris on the election
Guest: Analyst Dick Morris

With Republicans running scared and pundits predicting a Democrat takeover of one or both houses of Congress, Political analyst Dick Morris weighed in with his opinion. "Unless Bush changes the subject with presidential leadership, the Foley scandal won't go away. It's not Foley, it's Congress. They haven't passed anything, spending is out of kilter, and all of this is epitomized by Foley. If this election is defined by Foley and Iraq and ethics, they're going to get clobbered. If the elections were held now the Republicans would lose both houses."
Back of Book Segment 
American TV icons: Mr. T
Guest: Mr. T

The Factor has been interviewing TV legends, and Friday was Mr. T's turn to enter the No Spin Zone. The former "A-Team" star said he is one actor who is glad to be typecast. "If people typecast me, that's good - I'm a tough guy with a tender heart, and the people know that. I want to feed the hungry, I want to build a community center for less fortunate people. A lot of people come and go, but my career, by the grace of God, is still here because I use my platform for the good of humanity." In Mr. T's upcoming reality series, called "I Pity the Fool," he tries to motivate and encourage everyday Americans. As Mr. T put it in his inimitable style, "there's no nudi-T, there's no obsceni-T, there's some chari-T, and much humili-T."
