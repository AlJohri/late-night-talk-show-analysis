Talking Points Memo & Top Story 
The liberal establishment on the attack
"Sensing they could soon control the federal government, some liberals are on the march. First, Senator Sherrod Brown, a Democrat from Ohio, trying to rev up the base at a Biden rally, said this about election night: 'They're going to pan over to Bill O'Reilly and you're going to see this little tear coming out of the side of his eye.' Here's the problem - The Factor has been very fair to Barack Obama. We sent producer Griff Jenkins to ask Senator Brown to cite 'one example' of unfairness, but he could not. Also, the New York Times ombudsman Clark Hoyt, who is supposed to keep the paper honest, actually wrote an article disputing that the Times is a liberal enterprise. 80% of the columnists at the Times are committed left-wingers, and if you saw the nasty piece on Cindy McCain over the weekend, please send it to Mr. Hoyt. The main point here is that fair-minded Americans may have something to fear if hard-core leftists gain control of the government in two weeks. They already control the Times, NBC News, and other powerful media, and you can see for yourself what is happening."

The Factor was joined by FNC media analyst Bernie Goldberg, who explained the hatred many leftists have for Fox News. "These people have a mental disorder," Goldberg said, "called 'FDS,' Fox Derangement Syndrome. They don't watch Fox but they know they hate Fox. Sherrod Brown and the people who boo Fox News want a cable television network where the anchor says 'I have a thrill running up my leg' when Obama speaks. And if Barack Obama wins, they're not going to want everything to be down the line the way they see it." The Factor reported that many on the left would like to pull the plug on talk radio and Fox News. "Sherrod Brown doesn't care about the truth, he just wants to knock us off the air. But I don't think that will ever happen and I don't think Barack Obama would get behind that."
Impact Segment 
Colin Powell endorses Barack Obama
Former Secretary of State Colin Powell has crossed party lines to endorse Barack Obama, but Fox News analyst Tammy Bruce downplayed the importance of Powell's move. "This is an emotional endorsement," Bruce said. "He used terminology not based on specifics - 'inspirational' and 'transformational.' Most people are looking at this more as a slap at the Bush administration than an embrace of Barack Obama. This is effectively a 5-year-old, Barack Obama, asking people to vote for him because he finally has his booster seat of Colin Powell." Retired Air Force Colonel P.J. Crowley disputed Bruce's assessment. "What Tammy said is silly. Colin Powell is a transformational figure who values statesmanship and bipartisanship, and he says he's voting for Barack Obama because he feels he's the kind of president we need right now. It's a powerful statement." The Factor theorized that Powell endorsed Obama for at least two reasons. "Barack Obama cultivated General Powell over a period of time, asking for his input on policy issues. And I think Powell was angry with the Bush administration."
Personal Story Segment 
Body language: Sarah Palin on SNL
Ace body language interpreter Tonya Reiman analyzed Sarah Palin's high-profile appearance on Saturday Night Live. "She looked very playful," Reiman said, "self-deprecating, and likeable. She's very comfortable in her own skin." Reiman also watched the newly-famous Joe "the Plumber" Wurzelbacher as he was interviewed on Fox News Channel. "I saw his eyes darting around a little bit, and when someone does that it tells me they can't wait to get out of that place. Even when you're attracted to the spotlight, it can still be intimidating." Finally, Reiman assessed both presidential candidates as they played it for laughs at the Al Smith Dinner. "John McCain was in his element, he was on, and Barack Obama was relaxed and smooth."
Campaign 2008 Segment 
Missouri state polls neck-and-neck
The Factor was joined by Missouri Governor Matt Blunt, a Republican whose state could be pivotal in the presidential election. "I'm confident that Senator McCain will carry Missouri," Blunt said. "His values are the values of Missourians, whether it's protecting innocent life or protecting Second Amendment rights. Senator Obama can attract large crowds, but often those crowds don't turn into votes. We have thousands of volunteers working across the state." The Factor asked Governor Blunt if he is worried about voter registration fraud. "We've had thousands of troublesome registrations," Blunt responded, "and ACORN has a bad record in our state. I'm urging our local election authorities to keep a sharp eye out to ensure we have an election that's free of fraud and corruption."
Unresolved Problems Segment 
Politicians and comedy: Good or bad?
Sarah Palin helped Saturday Night Live rack up huge ratings last weekend, but did the appearance help Governor Palin? The Factor posed that question to FNC analysts Mary Katharine Ham and Juan Williams. "I think it helped her," Williams replied. "A lot of people want to demonize Governor Palin, but there she was - human, self-deprecating with a sense of humor. She came across as a real human being having some fun." Ham added that Saturday Night Live has a large and youthful audience. "SNL has shown it can have an impact on this election and Sarah Palin really pulled it off. She came off as very funny and her performance was spot on. But people who hate her will continue to hate her." The Factor argued that "it is harder to hate somebody who is that funny and that engaged."
Back of Book Segment 
Reality Check: The financial meltdown
Factor producer Jesse Watters tracked down former Merrill Lynch boss Stanley O'Neal, who led the company to ruin but exited with a severance package valued at more than $150 million dollars. O'Neal refused to answer any of Watters' questions about his golden parachute or the people he harmed. The Factor put forth this reality check: "O'Neal maintains lavish homes in Westchester County, New York City and other places. He should be very ashamed." On the campaign trail, Sarah Palin spoke about 'very patriotic, very pro-America areas of this great nation.' Her rival Joe Biden took umbrage at that, saying "We are all patriotic, we all love our country in every part of this nation." The Factor's reality check: "We don't all love America, we aren't all patriotic. Not for a second."
