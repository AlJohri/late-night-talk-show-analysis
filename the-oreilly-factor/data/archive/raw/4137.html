Talking Points Memo 
Donald Trump and Violence
Watch more video in the Video Center...
"One of the biggest political stories in years unfolded in Chicago Friday as hundreds of left-wing protesters shut down Donald Trump's rally at the University of Illinois. If you were monitoring the national media coverage, you did not get the entire story. Here's what really happened: One week ago about 180 University of Illinois faculty and staff sent a letter to the university chancellor expressing concern about the rally from a liberal point-of-view. The same day, the far-left website MoveOn.org posted a petition opposing the Trump rally. Throughout the week, far-left people ramped up a campaign to deny Donald Trump the forum. Thus on Friday it was no surprise that disruptive protesters entered the pavilion to shut Trump down and confront his supporters. Far-left agitators who do not believe in freedom of speech drove a situation that could have become violent. But the national media spun the story, demonizing Mr. Trump and his supporters, blaming the incident on 'inflammatory rhetoric' and 'racist thought.' Some Trump supporters are angry, undisciplined people, but some supporters of Bernie Sanders are angry and undisciplined. And here's an interesting footnote. The radical group Black Lives Matter actually shut down a Sanders rally last August. So there is a trend of radical leftists believing they can disrupt political events at will. Talking Points applauds honest dissent. If you don't like Donald Trump, stand outside his events peacefully. Opposition to Trump is not the issue here, true fascism is. We have seen some anti-Trump people use the fascist salute while taunting his supporters. Trump has said some provocative things when protesters have interrupted his speeches. In the beginning of the campaign his flamboyant rhetoric got him attention and support, but now he is a very viable presidential contender. If Trump wins Ohio tomorrow, he wins the nomination. That's how close he is to running against Hillary Clinton for the presidency. But winning the Republican nod is far different than winning the general election, where Mr. Trump is going to have to persuade about 65 million Americans to vote for him. It is a foregone conclusion that minority voters will not support him. He will also have trouble in states like California, Illinois and even New York, which have a lot of electoral votes. In addition, some Republicans who don't like Trump will stay home and the press, as we have seen in the Chicago case, will not cover the Trump campaign fairly. Donald Trump is an intelligent man but he has never been through anything like this before. He would be wise to tone it down a bit, fully explain his positions, hire advisors who can articulate his vision, and conduct himself with restraint. Mr. Trump has to overcome big odds to win the White House. It can be done, as Mrs. Clinton has her own challenges, but a tone adjustment will have to be made."
Impact Segment 
First Hand Account of Trump Protest
The Factor spoke with two Fox News reporters who were actually at the canceled Trump event in Chicago. "There were student groups inside the hall who wanted to disrupt the rally and take away Donald Trump's freedom of speech rights," John Roberts explained. "Trump decided there was a potential for violence and that he would not have been able to get a word in edgewise. Some of the protesters cited Trump's stand on immigration." Jeff Flock was outside the hall where Trump foes and supporters mingled. "After the event was canceled there was a lot of conflict. Many people had come to see Donald Trump and they felt that these protesters had kept them from doing that. There was a lot of back-and-forth, and most of it was non-violent."
Personal Story 
Dr. K on Trump and Violence
Watch more video in the Video Center...
Charles Krauthammer entered the No Spin Zone with his analysis of the protests. "There are two separate issues here," he said. "The far left used the time-honored tactic of shutting down the opposition and not allowing them to speak. This is what's been going on on college campuses for years and there is no defending it. The other story is that Donald Trump is running for the presidency and he says about a protester that in the old days we would have carried him out on a stretcher. He also said about another protester that he'd like to punch him in the face. Trump is responsible for not condemning another guy who sucker-punched a protester." The Factor reiterated that Donald Trump has to be more circumspect: "Trump speaks in an emotional manner and he doesn't have a filter. He doesn't think sometimes before he speaks, he doesn't understand that his words can carry threats."
Personal Story 
WWP Investigation
After reports that the Wounded Warrior Project was spending lavishly on salaries and junkets, the charity's top two executives were fired. The Factor spoke about the scandal with Capt. Anthony Odierno, who chairs the board of directors. "We conducted an independent review," Odierno reported, "and it showed that 80% of our spending goes to programs for the vets. There were allegations of excessive spending on travel, but over 99% of plane tickets were in accordance with our policies. But after looking at the totality of things, the board decided a change was necessary." Odierno refused to put forth specifics about what led to the two firings. The Factor reminded viewers, "The new leadership seems solid to me, but ultimately you guys will have to decide about the Wounded Warrior Project, which does a lot of good."
Hume Zone 
All Eyes on Ohio
Watch more video in the Video Center...
FNC's Brit Hume looked ahead to Tuesday's five primaries, especially the delegate-rich states of Florida and Ohio. "Florida has 99 delegates and Ohio has 66," he said, "so if Donald Trump wins Florida and not Ohio he'll still be 33 delegates to the good against the field. And if he does really well in Illinois and Missouri, he'll get a lion's share of the delegates. As Trump continues to gain delegates, the others have to get an ever-larger share of the remaining delegates, so the odds lengthen. But that does not mean Trump will get to the convention with the 1,237 delegates necessary to win." Considering the strong possibility of a very contentious convention in July, Hume concluded, "You can not think of a way that this turns out well for the Republicans."
Watters' World 
Politics in America
The Factor re-aired some of Jesse Watters' more memorable interviews with American citizens who just don't have much of a clue. One Bernie Sanders supporter was unaware that his preferred candidate is a United States Senator, while a young woman suggested that ISIS could be defeated by meditation. And then there was one particularly hospitable woman who invited refugees to come and stay at her house.
