Talking Points Memo 
The real race for the White House begins
"With Hillary Clinton finally waving the surrender flag, Barack Obama is now free to begin convincing independent-minded Americans that he would be a better president than John McCain. The first duel, under way right now, is about Iraq and Iran. John McCain is painting Obama as a naï¿½ve appeaser who has no idea about Iran's villainy. But Obama quickly responded, saying 'there is no greater threat to Israel, or to the peace and stability of the region, than Iran.' So the debate over exactly how to contain Iran becomes central to the election. McCain blasted Obama for not supporting a 'terrorist' designation for Iran's Revolutionary Guard. Obama's more diplomatic approach toward Iran is popular in the liberal community, but among independent voters the improving Iraq situation and an aggressive posture toward Iran may resonate. If it doesn't, McCain loses. If it does, Obama's got problems. It's a key issue."

Top Story 
Hillary as VP: Could a gender war erupt?
How will women supporters of Hillary Clinton react if their candidate does not get picked as Barack Obama's running mate? The Factor posed that question to FNC's Monica Crowley and Margaret Hoover. "49% of white women have an unfavorable impression of Barack Obama," Hoover reported. "He is going to have a lot of work to do, and they are her core constituency. But if she comes out and gracefully passes the baton over to Obama, they will follow her lead." Crowley agreed that Senator Clinton can influence millions of women. "She has a very emotional grip on these women voters, who talk about her candidacy in very passionate terms. They see that she's had to suffer a lot of humiliation, and a lot of women look on her candidacy as historic."

News Link: Female Clinton supporters reeling from Obama victory
Impact Segment 
Pat Buchanan on the race for the White House
The Factor welcomed author and former presidential candidate Pat Buchanan, who discounted the possibility of an Obama/Clinton ticket. "There's only one circumstance under which she gets it," Buchanan asserted, "and that is if Obama goes into August and finds himself eight or ten points behind and needs her to bring home the women, the white folks, and the working class. I would not be surprised if Michelle Obama is very bitter about Hillary Clinton and does not want her on the ticket." Buchanan also praised Obama as a remarkable talent. "As a political athlete, Obama is in a league with Bill Clinton, Ronald Reagan and Jack Kennedy. But his problem is that culturally, socially and ideological, he is far outside the center of American politics." Turning to the GOP, Buchanan predicted that one of three Midwesterners will be picked to run as VP. "McCain will look at Governor Tim Pawlenty of Minnesota, Mitt Romney to bring him Michigan, or Rob Portman of Ohio." The Factor narrowed that choice to one. "It's got to be Romney because he's a money-raiser and he's an economic guy who can bring Michigan. I just can't see McCain going anywhere else."
'Is it Legal?' Segment 
Obama and Rezko, more
The Factor was joined by FNC legal analysts Lis Wiehl and Megyn Kelly, who began with the conviction of Barack Obama's one-time financier and friend Tony Rezko on fraud, bribery and money laundering charges. "He put people in positions of power," Kelly said, "and he tried to use that to force bribes so people could get government contracts. The only way it's coming up in the campaign is if people say Barack Obama has a judgment problem." Wiehl reported on the Guantanamo Bay hearing of Hhalid Sheikh Mohammed, self-proclaimed mastermind of 9/11. "He won't accept lawyers. Not only does he not care, he wants to be martyred, he wants to be executed."

News Link: Obama fundraiser guilty of corruption charges

News Link: 9/11 mastermind on trial

Returning for a second segment, Megyn Kelly responded to Wednesday night's interview with gay rights activist Mikayla Connell, who accused Kelly of misrepresenting the California Supreme Court's decision endorsing gay marriage. "Mikayla is right," Kelly countered, "that 60 years ago the California Supreme Court recognized the fundamental right to marry, but not for gay people to marry other gay people. You don't have to take my word for it, just look at the opinion." Finally, the legal wizards turned to the case of Connecticut teens who are taking nude videos of themselves and sending them to one another. "I wouldn't file charges against the school," Wiehl declared, "but I would file against a kid who sent this out. It's child pornography." But Kelly vehemently disagreed. "This isn't a crime. This is the Internet version of 'you show me yours, I'll show you mine.'"

Weekdays with Bernie & Jane Segment 
Primetime violence on CBS
FNC's Bernie Goldberg and Jane Hall opined on CBS's decision to air the ultra-violent ultimate fighting in prime time. "Television has always been a business," Goldberg began, "so from a business point of view CBS made a good decision. But there's a big difference between good ratings and good taste. This used to be the 'Tiffany network,' and in the old days they'd never run a bloodbath like this in prime time. This cheapens and dumbs down the culture." Hall agreed that CBS is auctioning off its corporate soul. "You can get ratings with live executions, why not do that? This is very violent, it's boxing with more opportunity to inflict brain damage, and I would hope CBS is not proud of this. This is entertaining people with blood lust."

Reality Check Segment 
Honest dissent vs. hating America
The Factor pointed out the Chicago connections between Father Michael Pfleger and Minister Louis Farrakhan, and put forth this reality check: "No sane person disputes that Farrakhan is an extremist. By approving of him in a Catholic Church, Father Pfleger defines who he is and how he feels about America and Israel. The priest has a right to do this, but he doesn't have the right to preach it in a church. The IRS should investigate Father Pfleger, who is using his pulpit for political discourse, which is illegal to tax-exempt entities." In another example of harsh rhetoric, actress Jessica Lange lectured graduating seniors at Sarah Lawrence College about America's "torture" and "corporate armies." The Factor's reality check: "I would not call Jessica Lange anti-American, but I would call her unfair and naï¿½ve. All the stuff she talks about was put into place after the country was attacked on 9/11 and we have not been attacked again. Somehow Ms. Lange failed to mention that to the students."
